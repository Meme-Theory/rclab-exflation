THEOREM (Perturbative Exhaustion as Phase Boundary Diagnostic):

Let F_pert(eta) be the perturbative free energy of a system with order
parameter eta, evaluated to all orders in perturbation theory. Suppose:

  (H1) F_pert is convex: F_pert''(eta) > 0 for all eta in [0, eta_max]
  (H2) F_pert is monotonic: dF_pert/d(eta) > 0 for all eta > 0
  (H3) The cubic invariant is nonzero: F_pert'''(0) != 0
  (H4) An instability exists in a non-perturbative channel:
       the Pomeranchuk parameter f < f_crit in at least one sector
  (H5) The coupling in the unstable channel satisfies g*N(0) > 1

Then the perturbative free energy F_pert is NOT the true free energy of
the system. The true free energy F_true has a branch structure:

  F_true(eta) = min{ F_pert(eta), F_cond(eta) }

where F_cond is the condensate branch, satisfying:

  F_cond(eta_0) < F_pert(eta_0)  for some eta_0 > 0

The perturbative branch F_pert is metastable (local minimum at eta=0)
but not the global minimum. The transition from F_pert to F_cond is
first-order (by H3) with a non-perturbative barrier.

PROOF SKETCH:

Step 1 (Perturbative exhaustion). H1 + H2 establish that F_pert has no
structure: no minima, no spinodals, no inflection points beyond eta=0.
Every perturbative mechanism (tree-level, 1-loop CW, Casimir, spectral
sums of all types) has been computed and found monotonic. This is not an
approximation failure -- it is a theorem about the algebraic structure of
spectral sums on SU(3) with standard SM embedding (Dual Algebraic Trap,
Session 21c Theorem 1; block-diagonality, Session 22b Theorem 2).

The perturbative landscape is EXACTLY characterized. It is featureless.
This exhaustion is the FIRST necessary condition for a non-perturbative
phase transition: if perturbation theory could find the ground state,
the non-perturbative ground state would not exist as a distinct phase.

Step 2 (Instability identification). H4 establishes that the perturbative
ground state (eta=0, undeformed SU(3)) is unstable in a specific channel.
The Pomeranchuk criterion (Landau, Paper 11, Fermi liquid theory) states:
a Fermi liquid is unstable toward condensation in channel l if the Landau
parameter F_l satisfies F_l < -(2l+1). Session 22c F-1 computes:

  f(0,0) = -4.687 at tau = 0.30,  threshold = -3

The perturbative ground state is Pomeranchuk-unstable. This instability
is invisible to F_pert because the condensate is non-analytic in the
coupling (BCS gap: Delta ~ exp(-1/gN0), which vanishes to all orders
in perturbation theory around g=0).

Step 3 (Sufficient coupling). H5 establishes that the coupling strength
is sufficient for condensation. g*N(0) = 3.24 > 1 (Session 22c L-2).
At this coupling, the standard BCS formula breaks down (2*lam_F*exp(-1/gN0)
> lam_F), and the system is in the BEC crossover regime. The condensate
gap Delta is a substantial fraction of the Fermi energy (Delta/lam_F ~
0.5-0.9 from Nozieres-Schmitt-Rink interpolation), not exponentially
suppressed. The condensate energy scale is comparable to the gap energy
scale, not perturbatively small.

Step 4 (Branch structure). Combining Steps 1-3: F_pert is exactly known
and featureless; the perturbative ground state is unstable; and the
unstable channel has sufficient coupling for a non-perturbative condensate.
The true free energy must therefore have a branch structure where the
condensate branch F_cond lies below F_pert for some eta_0 > 0.

The cubic invariant (H3, V'''(0) != 0) guarantees the transition between
branches is first-order (Landau, Paper 04, Section 6.1). The barrier
between F_pert and F_cond is non-perturbative -- it cannot be computed
from F_pert alone.
