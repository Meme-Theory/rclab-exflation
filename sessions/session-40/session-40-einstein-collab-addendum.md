## 5. What Breaks

I would be dishonest if I did not identify the tensions.

**A. The modulus still moves.** The tau parameter is not a particle -- it is a shape parameter of the internal geometry. The spectral action gradient drives it monotonically. In the PI's picture, the substrate contracts (the internal space deforms). This is permitted: the stadium shrinks, but the spectators still do not move laterally. However, the RATE of contraction is set by the spectral action, and this rate determines the sweep speed that governs pair creation. The "breathing" of the instanton gas at each nexus point must be synchronized with the global contraction rate. This synchronization is built into the framework (the modulus is spatially homogeneous), but it is not derived from local substrate physics. It is imposed by the FRW ansatz. A truly local substrate picture would need to derive spatial homogeneity from local instanton-instanton interactions.

**B. Brownian motion and the substrate.** In my 1905 paper on Brownian motion (Paper 04), I showed that the random jittering of pollen grains proved that the fluid substrate consists of discrete molecular units. The OBSERVATION of diffusive motion (mean-squared displacement scaling as sqrt(t)) was evidence for the substrate's granularity. If particles in the phonon-exflation framework are substrate excitations, is there an analog of Brownian motion -- a random jitter of the "particle" caused by the discrete structure of the substrate lattice? The spectral geometry of SU(3) is continuous (a smooth Lie group), not a discrete lattice. There is no obvious source of stochastic fluctuation. This means either: (i) the substrate is truly continuous and there is no Brownian analog (the excitations propagate cleanly, like phonons in a perfect crystal at T = 0), or (ii) the instanton gas itself provides the fluctuation source (the breathing is stochastic, and excitations acquire a diffusive component from the instanton disorder). The distinction is computable: does the excitation spectrum of a BCS quasiparticle propagating through the instanton gas acquire a width from instanton scattering?

**C. The persistence of identity.** A stadium wave has no identity -- two waves passing through each other simply superpose. If particles are substrate excitations, what prevents two particles from passing through each other and losing their identity? In quantum mechanics, the answer is the exchange statistics (Bose or Fermi). In the framework, the BDI topological class (Session 17c) and the Peter-Weyl block-diagonal theorem (Session 22b) provide algebraic protections: excitations in different sectors cannot mix. But within a single sector, the question of particle identity under collision is open.
