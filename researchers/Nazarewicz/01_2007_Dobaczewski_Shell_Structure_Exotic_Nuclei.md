# Shell Structure of Exotic Nuclei

**Author(s):** J. Dobaczewski, N. Michel, W. Nazarewicz, M. Ploszajczak, J. Rotureau

**Year:** 2007

**Journal:** Progress in Particle and Nuclear Physics, Vol. 59, pp. 432-445

---

## Abstract

Theoretical predictions and experimental discoveries for neutron-rich, short-lived nuclei far from stability indicate that the familiar concept of nucleonic shell structure should be considered as less robust than previously thought. The notion of single-particle motion in exotic nuclei is reviewed with a particular focus on three aspects: (i) variations of nuclear mean field with neutron excess due to tensor interactions; (ii) importance of many-body correlations; and (iii) influence of open channels on properties of weakly bound and unbound nuclear states. These effects fundamentally modify the shell structure, leading to the emergence of new magic numbers and the disappearance of traditional ones in neutron-rich nuclei.

---

## Historical Context

The discovery of exotic nuclei far from beta stability has revolutionized our understanding of nuclear structure. While classical nuclear physics was formulated based on nuclei near stability, the advent of radioactive beam facilities revealed that nucleonic shell structure is not immutable. Magic numbers like N=50 and N=82, which characterize stable nuclei, are dramatically modified when moving toward the neutron drip line.

The tensor force, previously considered a minor ingredient of the nucleon-nucleon interaction, emerges as a critical component driving changes in the mean-field structure. In particular, the monopole part of the tensor interaction (which averages to zero over all possible nucleon pairs in stable nuclei) becomes sizeable when isovector asymmetry (N-Z) increases. This discovery provided a unified understanding of several key phenomena: the erosion of traditional magic numbers and the emergence of new shell closures in exotic nuclei.

Contemporaneously, many-body correlations become increasingly important as binding energies decrease. The continuum threshold plays a dominant role in the structure of loosely bound nuclei. Open channels—corresponding to particle emission—must be explicitly included in theoretical frameworks, moving beyond the conventional assumption of a closed system contained within a finite potential well.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### Single-Particle Motion and the Tensor Force

In conventional nuclear density functional theory, the mean-field potential is generated self-consistently from an effective nucleon-nucleon interaction. For stable nuclei near N=Z, the tensor component $V_T$ of the interaction averages to approximately zero due to parity and isospin considerations. However, in neutron-rich systems with large N-Z asymmetry, the monopole tensor force develops a strong attractive component in neutron-proton (T=0) channels while remaining repulsive in neutron-neutron and proton-proton channels.

The effect on the mean-field single-particle energies $\epsilon_i$ can be written as:

$$\epsilon_i = \langle i | U_0 | i \rangle + \langle i | V_{\text{Coulomb}} | i \rangle$$

where the effective mean-field potential $U_0$ receives contributions from both the central and tensor parts of the interaction. In neutron-rich nuclei, the tensor contribution to $U_0$ for neutrons differs substantially from that for protons, causing the shell structure to evolve with isotope.

### Shell Evolution Mechanism

The shell evolution occurs through changes in spin-orbit splitting. For a nucleus with N neutrons and Z protons, define the neutron asymmetry parameter:

$$\alpha = \frac{N - Z}{N + Z}$$

As $\alpha$ increases (moving toward the neutron drip line), shell-model calculations reveal that intruder orbitals—previously lying high in energy—progressively lower and eventually cross with traditional magic closures. For instance, the $1f_{7/2}$ orbital (which lies above the N=8 closure in $^{16}$O) moves below the $2p_{3/2}$ orbital in extremely neutron-rich systems, destroying the traditional N=8 magic number while potentially creating a new shell closure at N=14.

### Many-Body Correlations

Ground-state correlations become essential for systems approaching the drip line. The two-body correlation function:

$$g(\mathbf{r}_1, \mathbf{r}_2) = \langle \Psi | \hat{n}(\mathbf{r}_1) \hat{n}(\mathbf{r}_2) | \Psi \rangle - \langle \Psi | \hat{n}(\mathbf{r}_1) | \Psi \rangle \langle \Psi | \hat{n}(\mathbf{r}_2) | \Psi \rangle$$

exhibits pronounced short-range structure in weakly bound systems. Pairing correlations, described by the pair correlation amplitude $\kappa(\mathbf{r})$, become particularly important as the Fermi surface approaches zero.

### Continuum Effects

For a nucleus with low separation energy $S_n$, particle emission becomes an open channel. The single-particle spectrum extends into the continuum with energy $E > 0$, and weakly bound states couple resonantly to the continuum. The density of states near threshold exhibits the characteristic $E^{1/2}$ Wigner threshold cusp.

The continuum Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) formalism explicitly includes continuum states in the Bogoliubov basis:

$$|\Psi \rangle = \prod_{k} \left( u_k + v_k c^\dagger_k \right) | 0 \rangle$$

where both bound and continuum quasiparticles $k$ contribute to pairing correlations. The energy-dependent potential arising from continuum coupling modifies the effective interaction.

## Key Results

1. **Tensor-Driven Shell Evolution**: The monopole tensor force produces measurable shifts in shell closures, with new magic numbers appearing at N=14 and N=28 in extremely neutron-rich isotopes, while traditional closures (N=20, 28, 50) weaken or disappear.

2. **Erosion of N=8 and Z=8**: The oxygen isotope chain demonstrates dramatic shell evolution: $^{22}$O has a spherical or nearly spherical ground state despite being far from stability, contrary to naive mean-field expectations.

3. **Neutron Halos and Skins**: Extended density distributions in weakly bound nuclei produce neutron halos (e.g., $^{11}$Li with a two-neutron halo) and neutron skins, which cannot be explained by conventional nuclear models.

4. **Continuum Importance**: The inclusion of continuum degrees of freedom shifts theoretical predictions significantly; omitting continuum effects overestimates binding and misrepresents the coupling to threshold phenomena.

5. **Many-Body Effects**: Configuration mixing and advanced many-body methods beyond mean field (coupled-cluster, no-core shell model) become mandatory at the drip line.

6. **Tensor Force Dominance**: Modern ab initio approaches based on chiral effective field theory confirm that tensor forces are as important as central forces, particularly for exotic nuclei.

## Connection to Phonon-Exflation Framework

The shell structure of exotic nuclei exhibits an intriguing parallel to the phonon-exflation framework's treatment of pairing correlations on M4 x SU(3). In the framework, nucleonic pairing arises from collective phononic excitations of the deformed internal geometry. Nazarewicz's work on tensor-driven shell evolution demonstrates that pairing and mean-field structure are intimately coupled—changes in the mean field directly alter the landscape for pairing correlations.

The BCS mechanism in exotic nuclei operates in regimes where continuum effects dominate: weakly bound Cooper pairs couple resonantly to the continuum. The phonon-exflation perspective suggests that such pairing is fundamentally a collective mode of the underlying SU(3) metric, with the continuum coupling reflecting the open-channel structure of the internal space itself. Tensor interactions produce shell shifts that modify the single-particle density of states—precisely the quantity that governs the strength of pairing. In the NCG framework, such density-of-states effects could emerge from the spectral density of the Dirac operator on the deformed 3-manifold.
