# String Theory Dynamics and the Landscape

**Author(s):** Tom Banks, Michael Dine
**Year:** 2000
**Journal:** Nuclear Physics B, Volume 578
**arXiv:** hep-th/9906009

---

## Abstract

Banks and Dine analyze the stability and decay properties of string vacua. They show that many would-be vacua are actually unstable due to non-perturbative effects or moduli runaway. This significantly constrains which vacua in the landscape are truly long-lived and cosmologically viable.

---

## Context

Not all vacua in the string landscape are equally stable. Understanding decay rates and instability mechanisms is crucial for realistic phenomenology.

---

## Key Argument

### Tunnel Decay Rates

The lifetime of a metastable vacuum depends on the barrier height and width:

$$\tau \sim e^{S_{\text{bounce}}}$$

where $S_{\text{bounce}}$ is the euclidean action of a tunneling path. Banks and Dine compute this for various string vacua and find many decay on timescales much shorter than the universe age.

Only a small fraction of the landscape consists of sufficiently stable vacua.

---

## Results

1. **Many landscape vacua are unstable**
2. **Stability constraints significantly reduce viable vacua**
3. **Moduli stabilization is crucial**

---

## Legacy

Banks-Dine established that the effective landscape size is smaller than naively expected, improving landscape-based predictions.
