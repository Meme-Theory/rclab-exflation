# Pseudo-Riemannian Spectral Triples for SU(1,1)

**Author(s):** Jort de Groot
**Year:** 2026
**Source:** arXiv:2601.22171

---

## Abstract

This paper establishes that certain Kostant-cubic Dirac operators on SU(1,1) induce both pseudo-Riemannian and indefinite spectral triple structures. Using harmonic analysis of the non-compact group SU(1,1), the author constructs explicit representations of the Dirac operator and proves that the resulting spectral data encodes geometric information about the underlying pseudo-Riemannian manifold. The work extends classical spectral triple theory (Connes, 1989) into the regime of indefinite metrics and non-compact Lie groups.

---

## Historical Context

Spectral triples, pioneered by Connes in the 1990s, encode geometric information purely through spectral data: the Dirac operator's eigenvalues and eigenvectors determine the metric, topology, and dimension of a Riemannian manifold. However, classical spectral triples assume a positive-definite metric and a compact manifold structure. Many physically interesting geometries—particularly in particle physics (Minkowski spacetime) and cosmology (de Sitter/anti-de Sitter)—are fundamentally pseudo-Riemannian (indefinite signature).

De Groot's work addresses this gap by extending spectral triple formalism to non-compact groups with indefinite metrics. The Kostant cubic Dirac operator, originally developed to analyze representation theory of semisimple Lie groups, provides a natural arena for this generalization. For the framework, this is critical: the phonon-exflation substrate has SU(3) geometry (compact, Riemannian), but intermediate stages during the fold and external spacetime M^4 are pseudo-Riemannian. Understanding how spectral data encode indefinite geometry informs how D_K transitions between regimes.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### Section 1: Kostant's Cubic Dirac Operator on SU(1,1)

For a semisimple Lie group G with Lie algebra **g**, the Kostant cubic Dirac operator is defined as:

$$D_K = \sum_i e_i \otimes \gamma_i$$

where $\{e_i\}$ is a basis of **g** with respect to the Killing form $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$, and $\gamma_i$ are generators of the Clifford algebra $\text{Cl}(\mathfrak{g})$. The operator acts on spinors valued in both the Dirac spinor bundle and the adjoint representation of G.

For SU(1,1), the Lie algebra has basis:

$$\mathfrak{su}(1,1) = \{ X = \begin{pmatrix} ia & b \\ \overline{b} & -ia \end{pmatrix} : a \in \mathbb{R}, b \in \mathbb{C} \}$$

with Killing form $\langle X, Y \rangle = -\text{Tr}(XY)$ (which is indefinite: signature $(+, -, -)$ in the real basis $(H, E, F)$ where $H = i\sigma_3/2$, $E = \sigma_+/2$, $F = \sigma_-/2$).

The Dirac operator's square is:

$$D_K^2 = -\text{Cas}_{\mathfrak{g}} + \text{lower order}$$

where $\text{Cas}_{\mathfrak{g}} = \sum_i e_i^2$ is the Casimir operator. For SU(1,1), this is indefinite quadratic form:

$$\text{Cas}_{SU(1,1)} = H^2 - E F - F E$$

The spectrum of D_K is not purely positive—eigenvalues cluster around 0 and extend in both directions. This indefiniteness is essential: it mirrors the signature of pseudo-Riemannian metrics in physics.

### Section 2: Spectral Triple Structure

A spectral triple $(A, H, D)$ consists of:
- $A$: a C*-algebra (or *-algebra)
- $H$: a Hilbert space
- $D$: a self-adjoint operator (Dirac)

satisfying: $[D, a]$ is bounded for all $a \in A$, and $D$ has compact resolvent.

For pseudo-Riemannian spectral triples, the metric signature condition is:

$$g_{\mu\nu} = c \cdot \text{sgn}(D\lambda_i) \cdot \lambda_i^{-2}$$

(in a suitable scaling). Here, different eigenvalues can have opposite signs, encoding the indefinite geometry.

For SU(1,1), de Groot constructs $H$ as the tensor product:

$$H = L^2(SU(1,1)) \otimes S$$

where $S$ is the spinor representation (dimension 4 for SU(1,1)). The algebra $A$ is generated by left and right multiplication operators on SU(1,1), forming a double coset algebra.

The key theorem: The pair $(D_K, H)$ forms an indefinite spectral triple of **dimension 3** (matching the real dimension of SU(1,1) as a 3-dimensional manifold).

### Section 3: Harmonic Analysis and Eigenvalue Distribution

For compact Lie groups (e.g., SU(2)), the harmonic analysis of D_K is fully understood: eigenvalues are discrete, labeled by irreducible representations $\lambda \in \hat{G}$.

For non-compact SU(1,1), the spectrum is continuous (principal series) plus discrete series:

$$\sigma(D_K) = \sigma_{\text{cont}} \cup \sigma_{\text{disc}}$$

**Continuous spectrum**: For principal series representations $\pi_{\rho, \sigma}$ (labeled by $\rho \in i\mathbb{R}$ and sign $\sigma = \pm$), the Dirac operator eigenvalues form bands:

$$\lambda_{\rho,\sigma} = \sigma \sqrt{\rho^2 + (1/2)^2}$$

(The $(1/2)^2$ term is the Casimir eigenvalue in this representation.)

**Discrete spectrum**: For holomorphic/antiholomorphic discrete series $\pi_n^{\pm}$ (with $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$), eigenvalues are:

$$\lambda_n^{\pm} = \pm(n + 1/2)$$

These are isolated, of finite multiplicity. The discrete series are the "physical" representations in many applications.

The indefiniteness manifests in the sign of $\sigma$: principal series representations come in both holomorphic (+) and antiholomorphic (-) flavors, creating a spectrum symmetric around 0.

### Section 4: Heat Kernel and Dimension Spectral Sequence

For a Riemannian spectral triple, Wodzicki's noncommutative residue extracts the Seeley-DeWitt a_2 coefficient:

$$a_2 = \frac{1}{(4\pi)^{d/2}} \int_M \text{Tr}(1)$$

For pseudo-Riemannian triples, the heat kernel expansion picks up both positive and negative contributions:

$$\text{Tr}(e^{-tD^2}) \sim \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} a_j t^{(j-d)/2}$$

The dimension formula from the spectral action becomes:

$$S_{\text{spectral}} = \int_0^{\infty} \frac{dt}{t} \left[ \text{Tr}(e^{-tD^2}) - \text{(power divergences)} \right]$$

For SU(1,1), de Groot shows that the heat trace is:

$$\text{Tr}(e^{-tD_K^2}) = \int_0^{\infty} d\rho \frac{\sinh(2\pi\rho)}{2\pi} e^{-t(\rho^2 + 1/4)} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} 2 e^{-t(n+1/2)^2}$$

The integral term (continuous spectrum) dominates at large t; the sum (discrete series) gives subdominant contributions. Extracting a_2 requires careful handling of the continuous spectrum.

### Section 5: Isospectral Rigidity and Metric Reconstruction

A classical result (Kac, 1966) asks: "Can you hear the shape of a drum?" Specifically, do isospectral Laplacians imply isometric metrics?

For Riemannian spaces, the answer is "usually no"—there exist nonisometric Riemannian manifolds with the same Laplace spectrum. However, for Lie groups with left-invariant metrics, isospectral implies isometric (Lauret, 2017).

De Groot extends this: For SU(1,1) with pseudo-Riemannian left-invariant metric, if two metrics have the same D_K spectrum, they are pseudo-isometric.

The proof uses the metric reconstruction formula:

$$g_{\mu\nu}(x) = \lim_{s \to 0^+} s \cdot (\text{heat kernel})(x, x; s)$$

For indefinite metrics, the sign of the heat kernel at short times encodes the local signature.

## Key Results

1. **Pseudo-Riemannian spectral triple**: SU(1,1) with Kostant cubic Dirac forms both a standard indefinite spectral triple and obeys the axioms of a pseudo-Riemannian spectral triple.

2. **Dimension formula**: The dimension from spectral action is 3, matching $\dim_{\mathbb{R}} SU(1,1) = 3$.

3. **Eigenvalue classification**: Spectrum partitions into principal series (continuous, both signs) and discrete series (isolated, finite multiplicity).

4. **Heat kernel asymptotic**: Continuous spectrum dominates; Seeley-DeWitt a_2 is well-defined but differs from compact case by sign-flipping contributions.

5. **Isospectral rigidity**: Pseudo-isometric SU(1,1) metrics with left-invariant structure have identical Dirac spectra.

## Impact and Legacy

This work bridges representation theory, differential geometry, and spectral geometry. It opens a path toward spectral action formulations in indefinite-signature spacetimes. Most directly relevant: if M^4 x SU(3) can be realized as a pseudo-Riemannian spectral triple (M^4 indefinite, SU(3) Riemannian), the spectral action yields both gravitational action (from M^4) and Yang-Mills + Higgs (from SU(3)) simultaneously—Connes' original vision.

For quantum gravity, indefinite spectral triples provide a framework for quantizing Lorentzian geometry without breaking manifest relativistic covariance, in contrast to Euclidean path integrals or ADM formalism.
