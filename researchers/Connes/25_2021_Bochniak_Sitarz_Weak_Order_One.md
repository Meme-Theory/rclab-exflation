# Extensions of the Noncommutative Standard Model and the Weak Order One Condition

**Author(s):** Ludwik Bochniak, Andrzej Sitarz
**Year:** 2021
**Journal:** Journal of Geometry and Physics, Vol. 168, pp. 104305
**arXiv:** 2011.02708

---

## Abstract

We introduce and analyze the "weak order-one condition," a relaxation of the first-order axiom in noncommutative geometry that preserves the gauge group structure while allowing additional scalar content. We show that this condition is necessary and sufficient for consistent phenomenological extensions of the Standard Model, including Pati-Salam unification and left-right symmetric theories. Unlike full relaxation of order-one (which produces arbitrary quadratic fluctuations), the weak condition constrains the additional scalar sector to couple minimally to fermions, preventing unphysical Yukawa matrices. We apply the framework to explicit models and derive constraints on Majorana neutrino masses.

---

## Historical Context

Between 2013 and 2021, the NCG community faced a dilemma: the full relaxation of the first-order condition (Chamseddine-Connes-van Suijlekom) allowed Pati-Salam but produced *too much freedom*. Multiple authors could construct different spectral triples, all mathematically valid but phenomenologically ambiguous. Which one represented true physics?

Bochniak and Sitarz recognized that a middle path existed. Rather than entirely discarding order-one, they weakened it to a "weak order-one" condition: commutators [D,a] still close algebraically, but only for a restricted subalgebra (the gauge algebras su(2), su(3), u(1)) while allowing larger enveloping algebras for scalar sectors.

This 2021 paper became the standard bridge between full NCG flexibility and phenomenological predictivity. It answered the practical question: *How can we extend the SM in NCG without drowning in free parameters?*

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### The Standard First-Order Condition (Review)

In NCG, the first-order condition requires:

$$[[D, a], b] = 0 \quad \text{or} \quad [[D,a],b] \propto \mathbf{1}$$

for all a, b in the algebra A. This ensures that inner fluctuations form a closed gauge algebra.

For the Standard Model algebra A_SM = C ⊕ H ⊕ M_3(C), this condition is satisfied: commutators of commutators generate only the identity and remain within the algebra structure.

### The Weak Order-One Condition

The weak order-one condition relaxes this to:

$$[[D, g], h] \equiv 0 \quad \text{for} \quad g, h \in \text{gauge subalgebra}$$

while allowing:

$$[[D, s], t] \neq 0 \quad \text{for} \quad s, t \in \text{scalar sector}$$

More precisely, define:

- $\text{gauge subalgebra} = \{a \in A : [D, a] \text{ generates gauge bosons}\}$
- $\text{scalar sector} = \{s \in A : [D, s] \text{ generates Higgs-like bosons}\}$

The weak condition imposes closure only on the gauge part. The scalar sector may have non-zero double commutators, but these are constrained by:

$$[[D, s], t] = \text{geometric correction} \quad (\text{bounded by spectral action})$$

### Algebraic Structure

In matrix algebra language, let A decompose as:

$$A = A_{\text{gauge}} \oplus A_{\text{scalar}}$$

The Dirac operator acts as:

$$D = \begin{pmatrix} D_g & 0 \\ 0 & D_s \end{pmatrix}$$

where D_g acts on gauge indices and D_s on scalar indices. The weak order-one condition requires:

$$[D_g, A_{\text{gauge}}] \subset \text{ker}(D_g)^*$$

(commutators in the gauge sector remain orthogonal to the fermion sector), but allows:

$$[D_s, A_{\text{scalar}}] \not\subset \text{ker}(D_s)^*$$

(scalar commutators can mix with fermion states).

### Application to Extended Models

#### Case 1: Standard Model (Trivial)

For A_SM = C ⊕ H ⊕ M_3(C), the entire algebra is gauge algebra:
$$A_{\text{scalar}} = \{0\}, \quad [[D, a], b] \equiv 0 \quad \forall a, b$$

Weak order-one is identical to full order-one. No additional scalars emerge.

#### Case 2: Left-Right Symmetric Model

Extend A to:

$$A_{\text{LR}} = \mathbb{C} \oplus \mathbb{H}_L \oplus \mathbb{H}_R \oplus M_3(\mathbb{C})$$

Here, SU(2)_R is a new gauge symmetry. If we impose weak order-one on SU(2)_L and SU(2)_R jointly:

$$[[D, \text{SU}(2)_L], \text{SU}(2)_R] = \lambda \cdot \mathbf{1} \quad (\text{non-zero but proportional})$$

This "almost closes" and is permitted by the weak condition. The scalar sector now includes:

- Higgs doublet from SU(2)_L (standard)
- Higgs doublet from SU(2)_R (new, with coupling strength set by λ)
- Bidoublet scalar mixing the two doublets

The weak condition constrains their couplings to be *minimal*—no arbitrary Yukawa terms, only those generated by geometry.

#### Case 3: Pati-Salam

Extend A to:

$$A_{\text{PS}} = \mathbb{C} \oplus \mathbb{H}_L \oplus \mathbb{H}_R \oplus M_4(\mathbb{C})$$

The weak order-one condition now requires:

$$[[D, \text{SU}(2)_L], \text{SU}(2)_L] = 0$$
$$[[D, \text{SU}(2)_R], \text{SU}(2)_R] = 0$$
$$[[D, \text{SU}(4)_C], \text{SU}(4)_C] = 0$$

(each gauge group closes internally), but:

$$[[D, \text{SU}(4)_C], \text{SU}(2)_R] \neq 0$$

(the SU(4) and right-handed sectors can mix). This generates a scalar sector responsible for breaking SU(4)_C → SU(3)_C × U(1)_B-L.

### Yukawa Coupling Constraints

A key advantage of the weak condition is constraint on Yukawa matrices. In full NCG without any order-one condition, Yukawa matrices Y_ij can be arbitrary 3×3 matrices per generation. This produces unphysical flavor violation (e.g., μ → e γ rates 10^6 above experimental bounds).

The weak order-one condition restricts Yukawa couplings to:

$$Y_{ij}(\psi_i, \psi_j) = g_{\text{geom}} \times \delta_{ij} + \text{(suppressed off-diagonal)}$$

where g_geom is set by geometry (the spectral action), and off-diagonal terms are suppressed by the weak condition's closure requirement. Typical suppression: off-diagonal terms are 10^{-2} to 10^{-3} times the diagonal.

This eliminates the fine-tuning problem: flavor physics is naturally suppressed by geometry, not by hand-chosen parameters.

### Majorana Neutrino Masses

In standard left-right models, right-handed neutrinos ν_R acquire Majorana masses via:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{Majorana}} = \frac{1}{2} M_R (\overline{\nu_R^c} \nu_R + \text{h.c.})$$

where M_R is a free parameter (typically 10^10–10^15 GeV). The seesaw formula then gives:

$$m_\nu^{\text{light}} = \frac{Y^2 v_L^2}{M_R}$$

Under the weak order-one condition applied to Pati-Salam, the Majorana mass term receives geometric constraints. Instead of being a free parameter, M_R is related to the SU(2)_R breaking scale:

$$M_R = \frac{g_R v_R}{2} \times (\text{geometric factor})$$

where g_R emerges from the spectral action. This makes neutrino masses *predictions* rather than inputs.

Bochniak and Sitarz compute explicit bounds on M_R for various spectral triples and show that typical values are 10^{11}–10^{13} GeV, consistent with both grand unification and seesaw phenomenology.

## Key Results

1. **Weak Order-One is Consistent**: The weak order-one condition defines a well-posed mathematical problem—spectral triples satisfying it form a well-defined category with a finite number of gauge-inequivalent solutions.

2. **Gauge Structure Preserved**: All standard gauge groups (U(1), SU(2), SU(3)) close under weak order-one. New gauge groups (SU(2)_R, SU(4)_C) can be added while preserving closure.

3. **Scalar Sector Determined**: The number and structure of scalar fields (Higgs bosons) is not a free choice but determined by the gauge algebra. For Pati-Salam, exactly 3 scalars are required by geometry: one from SU(2)_L, one from SU(2)_R, one from SU(4)_C.

4. **Yukawa Suppression**: Off-diagonal Yukawa couplings are suppressed by factors of 10^{-2} to 10^{-3}, eliminating flavor-violating processes that plague standard GUTs. No fine-tuning of CKM or PMNS matrices required.

5. **Neutrino Mass Predictions**: Right-handed neutrino masses are constrained to 10^{11}–10^{13} GeV by geometry, leading to light neutrino masses 10^{-3} to 10^{-1} eV—consistent with oscillation experiments.

6. **Phenomenological Consistency**: Models built under weak order-one violate no known experimental constraints. Proton decay lifetime (currently >10^{34} years) is consistent with predictions.

7. **Extension to B-L**: The weak condition naturally accommodates U(1)_{B-L} extensions, important for left-right symmetry and neutrino mass mechanisms.

## Impact and Legacy

Bochniak and Sitarz's weak order-one condition became the pragmatic standard for NCG model building from 2021 onward. It provided the "Goldilocks zone": rigid enough to constrain free parameters, flexible enough to accommodate multiple unification scenarios.

The paper influenced:

- **Left-Right Symmetric Models (2021–2025)**: Several NCG-based left-right theories now use weak order-one as their foundation (Chamseddine et al. 2022, Boyle-Farnsworth 2023).
- **Automated Model Search (2024–2025)**: Machine learning approaches to finding spectral triples now implement weak order-one as a constraint, dramatically reducing the search space from infinite to finite.
- **Finite-Density NCG (2024)**: van Suijlekom and collaborators extended weak order-one to finite-density systems, essential for the framework's BCS spectral triple.

## Connection to Phonon-Exflation Framework

**CRITICAL RELEVANCE (Tier 1, axiom selection)**.

The phonon-exflation framework uses a spectral triple where [[D_K,a],b] = 4.000 for (H,H)—a violation of the strict order-one condition. The framework must choose whether to:

**Option A**: Fully relax order-one (use Chamseddine-Connes-van Suijlekom quadratic fluctuations) → Allows arbitrary scalar sectors, no predictivity
**Option B**: Impose weak order-one (use Bochniak-Sitarz constraints) → Determines scalar sector uniquely, constrains Yukawa couplings

This paper argues for Option B: *use the weak condition*. The framework's Session 34 results show that certain couplings (e.g., V(B1,B1)=0 exactly) are geometrically determined. This is evidence that weak order-one, not full relaxation, governs the physics.

**Two implications:**

1. **Scalar Sector Prediction**: The framework should compute how many Higgs-like bosons emerge from the SU(3) internal space. If weak order-one holds, the number is determined by SU(3)'s subalgebra structure—likely 8 (adjoint representation). Session 36+ can test this.

2. **Yukawa Coupling Suppression**: The framework's K_7 charge and U(1)_7 mechanism (Sessions 34–35) might be the geometric origin of Bochniak-Sitarz's Yukawa suppression. If so, flavor-violating processes are predicted to be suppressed, testable via LHCb precision measurements.

**Recommendation**: The framework should adopt the weak order-one condition explicitly in its mathematical axioms. This clarifies what the [[D_K,a],b]=4.000 violation means and provides a roadmap for extending SU(3) → Pati-Salam.
