# Tholins: Organic Chemistry of Interstellar Grains and Gas -- A Scholarly Analysis

**Authors:** Carl Sagan, W. Reid Thompson, and Bishun N. Khare
**Year:** 1980 (and subsequent publications through the 1980s-1990s)
**Journal:** Multiple publications; the foundational tholin work appeared in *Icarus* and *Nature* from 1979-1984
**Key Paper:** Khare, B.N., Sagan, C., Arakawa, E.T., Suits, F., Callcott, T.A., and Williams, M.W. (1984). "Optical Constants of Organic Tholins Produced in a Simulated Titanian Atmosphere: From Soft X-ray to Microwave Frequencies," *Icarus*, Vol. 60, pp. 127-137; and Sagan, C. and Khare, B.N. (1979). "Tholins: Organic Chemistry of Interstellar Grains and Gas," *Nature*, Vol. 277, pp. 102-107

## Abstract

The tholin research program, developed by Carl Sagan and Bishun Khare at Cornell University from the late 1970s through the 1990s, with significant contributions from W. Reid Thompson, represents one of the most sustained and productive experimental investigations in prebiotic chemistry and planetary science. Sagan coined the term "tholin" (from the Greek "tholos," meaning muddy or turbid) to describe the complex organic polymeric materials produced by ultraviolet irradiation or electrical discharge through mixtures of simple gases -- particularly nitrogen and methane -- that simulate the atmospheres of Titan, the early Earth, and interstellar medium environments. Through systematic laboratory experiments, the group demonstrated that energy input into simple gas mixtures readily produces an extraordinary diversity of complex organic molecules, including amino acids, nucleotide bases, and other biochemically significant compounds. This work provided crucial experimental support for the hypothesis that prebiotic organic synthesis occurs readily under a wide range of planetary and interstellar conditions, and it predicted the organic haze that would later be confirmed on Titan by the Cassini-Huygens mission. The tholin program stands as one of the most important experimental contributions to astrobiology and prebiotic chemistry of the late 20th century.

## Historical Context

### The Miller-Urey Legacy

The tholin program was a direct intellectual descendant of the landmark Miller-Urey experiment of 1953, in which Stanley Miller (working under Harold Urey at the University of Chicago) demonstrated that electrical discharges through a mixture of methane, ammonia, hydrogen, and water vapor produced amino acids and other organic compounds. The Miller-Urey experiment had shown that the building blocks of life could be synthesized from simple precursors under plausible early Earth conditions, providing the first experimental support for the Oparin-Haldane hypothesis of abiogenesis.

However, by the late 1970s, several developments had both enriched and complicated the Miller-Urey picture:

1. **Atmospheric composition uncertainty**: The assumption that Earth's early atmosphere was strongly reducing (CH4 + NH3 + H2) had been challenged. Many geochemists now favored a weakly reducing or neutral early atmosphere (CO2 + N2 + H2O), under which the Miller-Urey synthesis was less efficient.

2. **Extraterrestrial organic chemistry**: The discovery of complex organic molecules in meteorites (particularly the Murchison meteorite, 1969) and in the interstellar medium (through radio astronomy) had revealed that organic chemistry was ubiquitous in the cosmos, not restricted to planetary surfaces.

3. **Titan**: The Voyager 1 flyby of Titan in November 1980 revealed a dense nitrogen-methane atmosphere shrouded in an orange organic haze -- an environment that appeared to be undergoing large-scale prebiotic chemistry in real time.

### Sagan's Experimental Program

Sagan, primarily known as a theorist and synthesizer, made the decision in the late 1970s to establish a serious experimental laboratory at Cornell, with Bishun Khare as the principal experimentalist. This was an unusual career move for someone of Sagan's stature and inclination, reflecting his conviction that the question of prebiotic organic synthesis on Titan and the early Earth required experimental investigation, not just theoretical modeling.

The laboratory, eventually known as the "Laboratory for Planetary Studies," became one of the premier facilities for simulating extraterrestrial atmospheric chemistry. The experimental apparatus consisted of sealed reaction vessels containing gas mixtures, irradiated by UV lamps or subjected to electrical discharges (cold plasma), with the products collected and analyzed by a suite of analytical techniques including mass spectrometry, infrared spectroscopy, and chromatography.

## Key Arguments and Evidence

### The Tholin Concept

The central insight of the tholin program was that when energy is deposited into simple gas mixtures containing carbon, nitrogen, and hydrogen (with or without oxygen), the result is not a random collection of simple molecules but rather a complex polymeric material with specific optical, chemical, and physical properties. Sagan named this material "tholin" and argued that it was relevant to multiple astrophysical environments:

1. **Titan's atmosphere**: The orange haze that obscures Titan's surface was proposed to be tholin produced by UV photolysis and charged particle bombardment of the N2/CH4 atmosphere.

2. **Early Earth**: A reducing early atmosphere would have produced tholin, which would rain down onto the surface and accumulate in the oceans, forming the "primordial soup" of prebiotic chemistry.

3. **Interstellar medium**: The reddish color of some interstellar grains and cometary nuclei was proposed to result from tholin-like organic polymers formed by cosmic ray and UV processing of icy grain mantles.

4. **Outer solar system bodies**: The dark, reddish surfaces of many small outer solar system bodies (Centaurs, Kuiper Belt objects, some asteroids) were attributed to tholin deposits.

### Experimental Results

The Sagan-Khare-Thompson group produced tholins under a wide range of conditions and analyzed them extensively:

**Titan tholins** were produced by UV irradiation or electrical discharge through N2/CH4 mixtures (90:10 to 98:2, matching Titan's atmospheric composition). The resulting material was a brownish-orange polymer with:
- Mean molecular weight in the hundreds to thousands of daltons
- Elemental composition approximately C:H:N in the ratio of roughly 5:5:1
- Complex infrared absorption spectrum with features attributable to C-N, C=N, N-H, C-H, and aromatic bonds
- Upon hydrolysis (treatment with water), yielded amino acids including glycine, alanine, and others

**Ice tholins** were produced by UV irradiation of frozen mixtures containing H2O, CH4, NH3, and other simple molecules, simulating icy grain mantles in the interstellar medium. These had somewhat different properties from Titan tholins, tending to be more oxygen-rich.

**Triton tholins** were produced from N2/CH4 mixtures at very low temperatures, simulating conditions on Neptune's moon Triton.

### Amino Acid Production

One of the most significant results was the demonstration that amino acids were produced in substantial quantities upon hydrolysis of both Titan tholins and ice tholins. At least 16 amino acids were identified, including all of the simplest biologically relevant amino acids. The significance was twofold:

1. It demonstrated that amino acid synthesis was not restricted to the specific Miller-Urey conditions but occurred under a much broader range of atmospheric compositions.

2. It showed that the organic haze on Titan (and potentially on the early Earth) was not just a random polymer but contained biochemically significant building blocks locked within its structure, releasable upon contact with liquid water.

### Nucleotide Bases

In later experiments, the group also detected nucleotide bases (adenine, guanine, cytosine) in hydrolyzed tholins, extending the biological significance of the material beyond amino acids to include the building blocks of nucleic acids. This was consistent with earlier theoretical and experimental work showing that hydrogen cyanide (HCN), a major component of tholin chemistry, readily polymerizes to form adenine (Oro 1960).

### Optical Properties

The tholin program also characterized the optical properties (absorption and scattering spectra) of the synthetic material across wavelengths from the ultraviolet through the visible to the near-infrared. These measurements were critical for:
- Interpreting the spectrum of Titan's haze
- Modeling the radiative transfer in Titan's atmosphere
- Identifying tholin-like materials on other solar system surfaces through reflectance spectroscopy

The Titan tholin optical constants became a standard reference dataset used by atmospheric modelers for decades and were incorporated into virtually every model of Titan's atmosphere and haze layers.

## Methodology and Data

### Experimental Apparatus

The core experimental setup consisted of:

1. **Reaction vessels**: Pyrex or quartz flasks containing gas mixtures at controlled pressures and temperatures
2. **Energy sources**: UV lamps (primarily hydrogen discharge lamps producing Lyman-alpha radiation at 121.6 nm) and/or cold plasma discharges
3. **Gas mixtures**: Carefully controlled compositions matching target atmospheric or interstellar conditions
4. **Collection**: Tholin deposited on vessel walls and collected by dissolution in appropriate solvents
5. **Analysis**: Mass spectrometry, gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS), infrared spectroscopy (FTIR), elemental analysis, gel permeation chromatography (for molecular weight distribution)

### Controls and Systematics

The group performed extensive control experiments to rule out contamination (a persistent concern in prebiotic chemistry experiments since the Miller-Urey era) and to characterize the dependence of tholin properties on:
- Gas mixture composition (N2/CH4 ratio, presence/absence of H2O, CO, CO2)
- Energy source type (UV vs. discharge)
- Total energy deposited
- Temperature
- Pressure

### Analytical Challenges

Tholins are inherently difficult to analyze because they are complex polymeric mixtures, not pure compounds. The group used multiple complementary analytical techniques and developed specialized protocols for dissolving, hydrolyzing, and separating the components. The identification of specific compounds (amino acids, nucleotide bases) within the hydrolysis products required careful chromatographic separation and mass spectrometric identification.

## Physical Interpretation

### Atmospheric Photochemistry on Titan

The physical picture that emerged from the tholin program, as applied to Titan, was:

1. **Initiation**: Solar UV radiation (primarily Lyman-alpha) and charged particles from Saturn's magnetosphere dissociate N2 and CH4 in Titan's upper atmosphere, producing reactive radicals (N, CH, CH2, CH3, C2H, etc.).

2. **Polymerization**: These radicals recombine to form increasingly complex molecules (HCN, C2H2, C2H4, etc.) and eventually polymeric particles (tholin) through a cascade of addition and condensation reactions.

3. **Haze formation**: The tholin particles grow by further polymerization and coagulation, forming the optically thick haze layers observed by Voyager.

4. **Sedimentation**: The haze particles settle through the atmosphere over timescales of years, accumulating on Titan's surface as organic sediment.

5. **Surface deposits**: Over Titan's 4.5-billion-year history, this sedimentation could have deposited a global layer of tholin tens to hundreds of meters thick.

### Prebiotic Chemistry Connection

The prebiotic significance of tholins depended on the crucial step of hydrolysis -- contact with liquid water. On Titan, this could occur:
- During impact events, which could temporarily melt surface ice and mix water with tholin deposits
- In transient liquid water produced by cryovolcanism
- Potentially in a subsurface water ocean (as later confirmed by Cassini gravity measurements)

On the early Earth, tholin produced in the atmosphere would rain into the oceans, where hydrolysis would release amino acids and other building blocks into the aqueous environment. This mechanism provided a continuous source of biochemically relevant molecules to the prebiotic soup.

## Impact and Legacy

### Titan Science

The tholin program was foundational for Titan atmospheric science. Khare and Sagan's optical constants remained the standard reference for Titan haze modeling for over 25 years, until new laboratory measurements and Cassini observations provided updated data. The Cassini-Huygens mission (2004-2017) spectacularly confirmed the essential picture: Titan's atmosphere contains a complex organic haze, the haze particles have optical properties consistent with (though not identical to) laboratory tholins, and organic material accumulates on Titan's surface.

The Huygens probe, which landed on Titan's surface in January 2005, detected a complex mixture of organic molecules in the lower atmosphere and at the surface, qualitatively consistent with the tholin model. The Cassini VIMS (Visual and Infrared Mapping Spectrometer) and INMS (Ion and Neutral Mass Spectrometer) instruments revealed an atmospheric chemistry even more complex than the tholin experiments had suggested, with thousands of molecular species identified.

### Astrobiology

The tholin program made a fundamental contribution to astrobiology by demonstrating the ubiquity and ease of complex organic synthesis under planetary conditions. The key message -- that the chemistry of life's building blocks is not special or difficult, but occurs spontaneously and abundantly whenever simple carbon-nitrogen-hydrogen gases receive energy input -- has become a cornerstone of astrobiological thinking.

### Terminology

Sagan's coinage "tholin" has entered the scientific lexicon as the standard term for complex organic polymers of abiotic origin found in planetary and astrophysical environments. The term is routinely used in the planetary science literature to describe the reddish organic deposits on Titan, Pluto, Charon, Centaurs, and Kuiper Belt objects.

### Outer Solar System Surfaces

The recognition that tholin-like materials could explain the reddish colors of outer solar system bodies was a significant contribution to surface science. New Horizons' observations of Pluto (2015) revealed reddish-brown deposits (particularly in the Cthulhu Macula region) that are interpreted as tholins, produced by UV processing of methane frost. The red-colored Centaur objects and many Kuiper Belt objects are similarly interpreted as having tholin-coated surfaces.

## Connections to Modern Science

### Dragonfly Mission

The Dragonfly mission, selected by NASA in 2019 for launch to Titan, is a direct descendant of the scientific program Sagan's tholin work established. Dragonfly is a rotorcraft lander that will explore Titan's surface, analyzing the composition of surface organics and investigating prebiotic chemistry. Its scientific objectives -- characterizing the organic inventory, searching for chemical biosignatures, and investigating the role of liquid water interactions with organic material -- are precisely the questions that the tholin program identified as central.

### Cassini-Huygens Legacy

The Cassini-Huygens mission (1997-2017) provided the most comprehensive dataset ever obtained for Titan's atmosphere and surface. The INMS instrument detected large positive and negative ions in the upper atmosphere, confirming and extending the tholin formation chemistry. The mass spectra showed ion masses exceeding 10,000 daltons, indicating that the polymerization process begins in the ionosphere and produces very large molecules before the particles even settle to the main haze layers.

### Pluto and the Kuiper Belt

New Horizons' Pluto flyby (2015) revealed a world with extensive tholin deposits, confirming the applicability of the tholin concept to Kuiper Belt objects. The Charon-Pluto system's reddish colors, particularly at the poles and in dark regions, are attributed to tholins produced by UV processing of methane and nitrogen ices. The Sagan-Khare optical constants and formation models remain relevant reference points for interpreting these observations.

### Exoplanet Hazes

The characterization of exoplanet atmospheres by JWST has revealed that hazes and clouds are common features that complicate atmospheric spectroscopy. Understanding the properties of photochemical hazes -- their formation mechanisms, optical properties, and compositional dependence on atmospheric composition -- draws directly on the tholin research program. Laboratory haze analog studies continue at multiple institutions worldwide, extending Sagan and Khare's methodology to the much wider range of atmospheric compositions found in the exoplanet population.

### Modern Prebiotic Chemistry

The prebiotic chemistry community continues to investigate the role of atmospheric photochemistry in supplying organic building blocks to planetary surfaces. Recent experiments have extended the tholin paradigm to CO2-N2 atmospheres (more representative of the weakly reducing early Earth atmosphere that modern geochemistry favors) and have found that while the yields are lower than in reducing atmospheres, complex organic synthesis still occurs. The general conclusion of the tholin program -- that prebiotic chemistry is robust and occurs under a wide range of conditions -- has been strengthened, not weakened, by subsequent work.
