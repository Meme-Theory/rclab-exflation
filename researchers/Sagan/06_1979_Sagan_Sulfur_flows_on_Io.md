# Sulfur Flows on Io: A Scholarly Analysis

**Authors:** Carl Sagan
**Year:** 1979
**Journal:** *Nature*, Vol. 280, pp. 750-753
**Context:** Published shortly after Voyager 1's encounter with the Jovian system in March 1979

---

## Abstract

Sagan's 1979 paper on sulfur flows on Io was published in the immediate wake of one of the most dramatic discoveries of the Voyager era: the observation of active volcanism on Jupiter's innermost Galilean moon. Voyager 1, passing through the Jovian system in March 1979, had revealed Io as the most volcanically active body in the solar system, with towering volcanic plumes, vast calderas, and a surface completely resurfaced by volcanic deposits. Sagan analyzed the distinctive color patterns and flow morphologies visible in Voyager images and proposed that many of the observed surface features were composed of sulfur and sulfur compounds in various allotropic forms. He correlated the color variations (ranging from white through yellow, orange, red, and dark brown/black) with the temperature-dependent allotropy of elemental sulfur, arguing that the Io surface was essentially a sulfur lava landscape where the colors recorded the thermal history of volcanic flows. The paper exemplified Sagan's ability to move rapidly from new observational data to physical interpretation and contributed to the understanding of Io as a world where sulfur geochemistry dominates surface processes.

---

## Historical Context

### Pre-Voyager Understanding of Io

Before Voyager, Io was known to be an unusual satellite. Ground-based observations had revealed several peculiarities:

1. **Anomalous color**: Io was distinctly reddish-orange, unlike the other Galilean satellites. Its visible spectrum showed features that had been tentatively attributed to sulfur compounds.

2. **Post-eclipse brightening**: Io showed anomalous brightness variations when emerging from Jupiter's shadow, suggesting surface or atmospheric processes responding to solar illumination.

3. **Sodium cloud**: A cloud of sodium atoms had been detected surrounding Io, extending along its orbit. The source of this sodium was mysterious.

4. **Plasma torus**: The Io flux tube -- an electrical current system linking Io to Jupiter's magnetosphere -- had been inferred from the decametric radio emissions triggered by Io's orbital position.

In a remarkable piece of predictive science, Peale, Cassen, and Reynolds published a paper in *Science* just days before the Voyager 1 encounter (March 1979) predicting that Io should be volcanically active, based on tidal heating calculations. They showed that the gravitational tides raised on Io by Jupiter (amplified by orbital resonances with Europa and Ganymede) would generate enormous internal heating -- enough to melt the interior and drive volcanism far exceeding anything on Earth.

### The Voyager 1 Discovery

The Voyager 1 flyby spectacularly confirmed Peale et al.'s prediction. Navigation engineer Linda Morabito discovered the first volcanic plume in a Voyager image taken for navigation purposes: a 300-km-high umbrella-shaped eruption on the limb, emanating from a caldera named Pele. Subsequent analysis revealed at least eight active volcanic plumes, extensive lava flows, calderas (called paterae) hundreds of kilometers across, and a surface completely devoid of impact craters -- indicating that the entire surface had been resurfaced by volcanic deposits within the last few million years.

The surface colors were striking and bizarre: patterns of white, yellow, orange, red, brown, and black that bore no resemblance to any other body in the solar system. These colors demanded explanation, and Sagan moved quickly to provide one.

## Key Arguments and Evidence

### Sulfur Allotropy and Color

The central argument of Sagan's paper exploited a well-known property of elemental sulfur: its color depends strongly on its temperature and the specific allotropic form (molecular configuration) it assumes.

At room temperature and below, sulfur exists as orthorhombic crystals of S8 (cyclo-octasulfur), which are bright yellow. Upon heating:

- **~95 C**: Sulfur transitions to monoclinic S8, remaining yellow
- **~119 C**: Sulfur melts to a yellow, low-viscosity liquid
- **~160 C**: The liquid darkens to orange, then red, as S8 rings begin to break open into polymeric chains (catena-sulfur, S_infinity)
- **~200 C**: The liquid becomes deep red to dark brown and its viscosity increases enormously (by a factor of ~10,000) as the polymer chains lengthen
- **~250-300 C**: The liquid becomes dark brown to nearly black, with very high viscosity
- **~445 C**: Sulfur boils

The color of quenched (rapidly cooled) sulfur depends on the temperature at which it solidified, preserving a record of its thermal state. Slowly cooled sulfur reverts to the yellow orthorhombic form, while rapidly cooled sulfur can be trapped in metastable colored states (orange, red, brown, black) depending on the quenching temperature.

### Color Mapping on Io

Sagan proposed that the diverse colors visible on Io's surface could be mapped to this temperature-color relationship:

- **White deposits**: SO2 frost (confirmed spectroscopically by infrared observations)
- **Bright yellow regions**: Orthorhombic sulfur at or near ambient temperature (~130 K on Io)
- **Orange-to-red flows**: Sulfur that solidified from moderate-temperature melts (~160-200 C)
- **Dark red to brown flows**: Sulfur quenched from higher-temperature melts (~200-300 C)
- **Black spots and flows**: Either very high-temperature quenched sulfur or silicate lavas

Sagan argued that the spatial distribution of colors around volcanic centers followed the expected pattern: the hottest (darkest) material near the vent, transitioning to progressively cooler (lighter) colors with distance, as the sulfur flow cooled during emplacement.

### Flow Morphology

Beyond color, Sagan analyzed the morphology of the visible flows. He noted that the flow shapes -- lobate, sinuous, with well-defined margins -- were consistent with the known rheological properties of liquid sulfur. Sulfur's unusual viscosity-temperature relationship (very low viscosity at the melting point, enormously high viscosity at ~200 C, then decreasing again at higher temperatures) would produce distinctive flow morphologies depending on the eruption temperature.

Low-temperature sulfur flows (near the melting point) would be thin and extensive, analogous to low-viscosity basaltic flows on Earth. Medium-temperature flows (in the viscosity maximum) would be thick and stubby, analogous to high-viscosity silicic flows. Very high-temperature flows (above the viscosity maximum) would again be extensive. Sagan argued that the diversity of flow morphologies on Io was consistent with eruption at a range of temperatures.

### Sulfur Volcanism vs. Silicate Volcanism

Sagan addressed the question of whether Io's volcanism was primarily sulfurous or siliceous. He argued that the color evidence strongly favored sulfur as the dominant surface material, but acknowledged that silicate volcanism might also occur, particularly for the highest-temperature features. The energy source (tidal heating) was easily sufficient to drive silicate volcanism; the question was whether the surface and near-surface were dominantly sulfur-rich or silicate-rich.

## Methodology and Data

### Image Analysis

Sagan's primary data source was the Voyager 1 imaging data -- color-composite images constructed from the spacecraft's narrow-angle camera using different spectral filters. The spatial resolution ranged from about 0.5 km at closest approach to tens of kilometers at greater distances. The color information, while not full spectroscopy, provided important constraints when interpreted through the lens of sulfur allotropy.

### Laboratory Comparison

Sagan compared the Voyager color data with laboratory measurements of sulfur in various allotropic forms and at various temperatures. He constructed a "sulfur color chart" relating temperature history to color and compared it visually with the Io images. This was a qualitative methodology by modern standards, but effective given the available data.

### Rheological Modeling

The flow morphology analysis drew on laboratory measurements of sulfur viscosity as a function of temperature. The distinctive non-monotonic viscosity curve of sulfur (low at melting, high at ~200 C, low again above ~250 C) provided specific predictions for flow thickness and extent that could be compared with the observed features.

### Thermal Modeling

Sagan performed simple thermal calculations to estimate the cooling rate of sulfur flows on Io's surface, which has a surface temperature of approximately 130 K and is essentially in vacuum (Io's atmosphere is negligible). The rapid cooling in these conditions would favor quenching of high-temperature allotropes, consistent with the preservation of the observed color patterns.

## Physical Interpretation

### Io as a Sulfur World

Sagan's interpretation painted a picture of Io as a world where elemental sulfur played a role analogous to water on Earth or silicate rock on the Moon -- it was the dominant geological material, cycling between surface deposits, subsurface reservoirs, volcanic eruptions, and atmospheric plumes. The SO2 served a complementary role, analogous to water ice, forming frost deposits and driving the volcanic plumes through pressure release.

### Tidal Heating and Sulfur Cycling

The ultimate energy source for Io's sulfur volcanism was tidal heating. Sagan connected the surface observations to the tidal mechanism: Jupiter's tidal forces, amplified by orbital resonance with Europa and Ganymede, generate internal heating of roughly 10^14 watts (about 2 watts per square meter, comparable to the solar flux at Jupiter's distance). This enormous heat source drives convection in Io's interior, melting subsurface sulfur (and possibly silicate) reservoirs and driving them to the surface through volcanic conduits.

### Color as a Thermometer

A key physical insight was that the color of sulfur deposits on Io's surface served as a geological thermometer, recording the eruption and cooling history of volcanic flows. This was analogous to the use of mineral assemblages as geothermometers in terrestrial petrology, but exploited the unique temperature-dependent allotropy of sulfur rather than mineral equilibria.

## Impact and Legacy

### Reframing Io Science

Sagan's paper, along with contemporaneous work by others (particularly Smith et al. 1979, the Voyager imaging team's initial report), established the framework for Io science that persisted for years. The sulfur-dominated surface model became the default interpretation of Io's geology, guiding the analysis of subsequent Voyager 2 observations and, later, Galileo orbiter data.

### The Sulfur vs. Silicate Debate

Sagan's sulfur interpretation, while highly influential, was not the final word. Subsequent observations, particularly from the Galileo mission (1995-2003), revealed that at least some of Io's volcanism involves very high-temperature silicate lavas. Temperature measurements of volcanic hot spots by Galileo's Near-Infrared Mapping Spectrometer (NIMS) and Solid-State Imager (SSI) showed eruption temperatures exceeding 1500 K in some cases -- far too hot for sulfur (which boils at 718 K) and consistent with ultramafic silicate magmas. The current understanding is that Io has a dual volcanic system: silicate volcanism from deep sources producing the highest-temperature eruptions, overlain by a sulfur-rich veneer that produces lower-temperature sulfur flows and contributes to the distinctive surface colors. Sagan's sulfur color interpretation remains valid for the surface deposits, but the underlying volcanic engine is now understood to be primarily silicate.

### Volcanology Beyond Earth

Io's volcanism, as interpreted by Sagan and others, dramatically expanded the scope of volcanology from a purely terrestrial science to a planetary one. The recognition that tidal heating could drive volcanism more intense than anything on Earth, in a compositional system (sulfur) with no terrestrial analog, forced a fundamental rethinking of volcanic processes. This expansion continues with the study of cryovolcanism on Enceladus, Titan, and possibly Pluto and Charon.

### Tidal Heating as a Habitability Factor

The demonstration that tidal heating could maintain geological (and potentially biological) activity in worlds far from the Sun has become one of the most important results in astrobiology. Europa's subsurface ocean, Enceladus's geysers, and potentially Titan's liquid water layer are all maintained by tidal heating. While Sagan's paper focused on sulfur geochemistry rather than habitability, the broader framework of tidally heated worlds that it helped establish has become central to the search for life beyond Earth.

## Connections to Modern Science

### Io Volcano Observer (IVO)

The proposed Io Volcano Observer (IVO) mission, which has been studied as a candidate for NASA's New Frontiers or Discovery program, would directly address many of the questions Sagan's paper raised: the relative contributions of sulfur and silicate volcanism, the thermal structure of volcanic plumes, the composition of surface deposits, and the dynamics of tidal heating. The mission concept traces its scientific lineage directly to the Voyager-era observations that Sagan interpreted.

### Juno Observations

NASA's Juno spacecraft, in orbit around Jupiter since 2016, has obtained new observations of Io during close flybys. Its infrared instruments have mapped the distribution and temperatures of volcanic hot spots, providing data that refine the sulfur vs. silicate question that Sagan's paper opened. The JunoCam visible-light images show the same striking color patterns that Sagan interpreted, now at higher resolution.

### Europa Clipper and Enceladus

While not directly about Io, the Europa Clipper mission (launched 2024) and proposed Enceladus missions are motivated by the same tidal heating physics that drives Io's volcanism. The intellectual connection is direct: Peale et al.'s tidal heating calculation, confirmed by Voyager's discovery of Io's volcanism, led immediately to the prediction that Europa should also be tidally heated, which led to the hypothesis of a subsurface ocean, which motivated Europa Clipper. Sagan's contribution was to the interpretive framework for what tidal heating produces at the surface.

### Exoplanet Volcanism

The detection of volcanic activity on exoplanets or exomoons is a long-term goal of astronomical observation. Io provides the key reference case: what does intense volcanism look like spectroscopically? Sagan's work on the color (and implicitly the spectroscopic signature) of sulfur-dominated volcanism is relevant to predictions of what volcanic signatures might be detectable in exoplanet or exomoon spectra.

### Sulfur Geochemistry and Life

There is a growing appreciation that sulfur geochemistry may be important for the origin and sustenance of life. Terrestrial sulfur-metabolizing organisms are among the most ancient on Earth, and sulfur cycling is thought to have been important in early Earth's biogeochemistry. Understanding sulfur chemistry on Io -- a world where sulfur cycles are dominant but biology is absent -- provides a useful abiotic comparison case for distinguishing biological from purely geological sulfur signatures.
