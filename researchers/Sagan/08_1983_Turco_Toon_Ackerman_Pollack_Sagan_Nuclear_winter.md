# Nuclear Winter: Global Consequences of Multiple Nuclear Explosions -- A Scholarly Analysis

**Authors:** R.P. Turco, O.B. Toon, T.P. Ackerman, J.B. Pollack, and Carl Sagan (TTAPS)
**Year:** 1983
**Journal:** *Science*, Vol. 222, No. 4630, pp. 1283-1292
**Context:** Published during the height of Cold War nuclear tensions

---

## Abstract

The TTAPS paper (named from the authors' initials: Turco, Toon, Ackerman, Pollack, Sagan) is one of the most consequential scientific papers of the 20th century, not only for its scientific content but for its unprecedented policy impact. The paper presented the results of one-dimensional atmospheric modeling showing that a large-scale nuclear exchange between the superpowers could inject enough soot and dust into the stratosphere to produce a prolonged period of cold, dark conditions -- a "nuclear winter" -- that would devastate global agriculture, potentially leading to mass extinction and the end of civilization even in countries not directly targeted by nuclear weapons. The key physical mechanism was the absorption of sunlight by lofted soot particles from massive urban firestorms, which would heat the upper atmosphere while cooling the surface by 15-40 degrees Celsius for weeks to months. The paper fundamentally changed the terms of the nuclear arms debate by demonstrating that nuclear war could be suicidal for the aggressor as well as the target -- that the climatic consequences constituted a form of mutual assured destruction that went far beyond the direct blast and radiation effects. Despite subsequent refinements that moderated some of the most extreme predictions (leading to the somewhat gentler term "nuclear autumn" in some scenarios), the core finding -- that nuclear war would produce severe, prolonged, global climatic disruption -- has been confirmed by every subsequent study using increasingly sophisticated atmospheric models.

## Historical Context

### The Nuclear Arms Race

By 1983, the United States and Soviet Union collectively maintained arsenals of approximately 50,000 nuclear warheads with a combined yield of roughly 13,000 megatons -- sufficient to destroy every major city on Earth many times over. The doctrine of Mutual Assured Destruction (MAD) was predicated on the assumption that the consequences of nuclear war would be limited, however devastating, to the direct effects of blast, thermal radiation, and radioactive fallout. The possibility of indirect climatic effects had received relatively little scientific attention.

### Precursors

Several lines of research converged to make the TTAPS study possible:

**Crutzen and Birks (1982)** published a landmark paper in *Ambio* titled "The Atmosphere After a Nuclear War: Twilight at Noon," which first identified the potential for massive smoke production from nuclear-ignited fires to affect global climate. They estimated that fires in cities, forests, and oil installations could produce enough smoke to significantly reduce sunlight reaching the surface, but did not perform detailed climate calculations.

**The Alvarez hypothesis (1980)**: The proposal by Luis Alvarez and colleagues that a large asteroid impact caused the end-Cretaceous mass extinction through dust-induced darkness and cooling provided a natural analog for the nuclear winter scenario. Sagan and his collaborators at NASA Ames were already studying the climatic effects of volcanic eruptions and large impacts, providing the atmospheric modeling expertise that would be applied to the nuclear case.

**The Martian dust storm analog**: Sagan and Pollack's extensive work on Martian dust storms and their climatic effects (following the 1971 global dust storm observed by Mariner 9) had given them detailed understanding of how atmospheric particulate loading affects radiative transfer and surface temperatures. The nuclear winter study was, in a real sense, an application of Mars science to an Earth problem.

### The TTAPS Team

The five authors brought complementary expertise:
- **Richard Turco** (UCLA): atmospheric chemistry, aerosol microphysics
- **Owen Brian Toon** (NASA Ames): atmospheric radiation, cloud physics
- **Thomas Ackerman** (NASA Ames; later Penn State from 1988): radiative transfer
- **James Pollack** (NASA Ames): planetary atmospheres, dust-climate interactions
- **Carl Sagan** (Cornell): planetary science, public communication

This was an unusual team in that four of the five (all except Turco) had primary backgrounds in planetary science rather than terrestrial atmospheric science. Their expertise in modeling extreme atmospheric states -- the thick CO2 atmosphere of Venus, the dust-laden atmosphere of Mars, the volcanic and impact winter of the Cretaceous -- gave them the tools and the perspective to analyze a scenario that conventional Earth climate scientists had not seriously considered.

## Key Arguments and Evidence

### Source Term: Fire and Soot

The TTAPS analysis began with a detailed assessment of the source of atmospheric particulate matter in a nuclear exchange:

**Urban fires**: Nuclear detonations over or near cities would ignite massive firestorms, burning buildings, infrastructure, petroleum stocks, plastics, and other materials. These fires would produce enormous quantities of black carbon soot, which is an extremely effective absorber of visible light.

**Forest fires**: Nuclear detonations and thermal radiation pulses could ignite forests over large areas. Forest fires produce significant but somewhat less absorptive smoke than urban fires.

**Dust injection**: Ground-level and near-surface detonations (as would be used against hardened military targets like missile silos) would loft millions of tons of pulverized soil and rock into the atmosphere, analogous to a volcanic eruption or asteroid impact.

The TTAPS team estimated that a full-scale nuclear exchange (5,000+ megatons) would inject approximately 225 million tonnes of smoke into the troposphere, with a significant fraction (estimated at 30-50%) reaching the stratosphere through the pyroconvection of the firestorms -- the intense convective columns generated by large fires that can punch through the tropopause and inject material directly into the stratosphere.

### Atmospheric Residence Time

Material injected into the troposphere is removed by precipitation within about a week. But material that reaches the stratosphere is above the weather and has a residence time of months to a year or more, similar to volcanic aerosol. The TTAPS team argued that the pyroconvective injection mechanism would place a substantial fraction of the soot into the stratosphere, where it would persist long enough to produce significant climatic effects.

### Radiative Transfer Calculation

The core of the paper was a one-dimensional (column) radiative-convective atmospheric model that computed the temperature response to the injection of specified quantities of soot and dust at specified altitudes. The key physics:

1. **Solar absorption**: Black carbon soot absorbs visible and near-visible solar radiation very efficiently. A layer of soot with sufficient optical depth (the TTAPS baseline case had visible optical depth of ~3 at the surface) would absorb most incoming sunlight, heating the layer where the soot resides while dramatically reducing the amount of sunlight reaching the surface.

2. **Infrared emission**: The heated soot layer would emit thermal infrared radiation both upward to space and downward to the surface. However, because soot is a strong absorber in the visible but a weaker absorber in the thermal infrared (relative to its visible absorption), the net effect is to trap less thermal radiation than it blocks in the visible. This leads to net surface cooling.

3. **Surface temperature response**: The TTAPS model predicted surface temperature drops of 15-25 degrees C in the baseline scenario, lasting weeks to months. In extreme scenarios (highest soot loading, maximum stratospheric injection), temperature drops of up to 35-40 degrees C were predicted, producing sub-freezing temperatures even in continental interiors during summer -- conditions that would be lethal to most crops and many natural ecosystems.

### The "Nuclear Winter" Scenarios

TTAPS analyzed a range of scenarios:

- **Baseline case** (5,000 MT): Surface temperature drops of ~20-25 degrees C lasting 2-3 months, gradually recovering over a year. Sunlight reduced to a few percent of normal for weeks.

- **Severe case** (10,000 MT): Temperature drops approaching 35-40 degrees C, extended darkness, recovery timescale of 6-12 months.

- **"Threshold" case** (~100 MT targeted at cities): Even a "limited" exchange, if concentrated on urban targets (maximizing fire and soot production), could produce significant cooling of 5-15 degrees C.

The last finding was particularly important because it suggested that even nuclear exchanges far smaller than the full arsenals could produce devastating climatic effects -- there was no safe level of nuclear war.

## Methodology and Data

### The One-Dimensional Model

The TTAPS model was a one-dimensional radiative-convective column model -- essentially the same type of model used in early climate sensitivity studies and planetary atmosphere calculations. It computed the vertical temperature profile of the atmosphere as a function of time, given specified injections of absorbing and scattering particles. The model included:

- Spectrally resolved solar and thermal infrared radiative transfer
- Convective adjustment (to prevent superadiabatic lapse rates)
- Simple parameterizations of soot scavenging and removal
- Multiple particle types (soot, dust) with different optical properties

### Limitations of the Model

The one-dimensional model had significant limitations that the authors acknowledged:

1. **No horizontal transport**: A 1-D model cannot represent the geographical distribution of effects, hemispheric asymmetries, or the dynamics of atmospheric circulation changes induced by the temperature perturbations.

2. **No ocean dynamics**: The thermal inertia of the oceans, which would moderate coastal temperatures, was not represented.

3. **No cloud feedbacks**: Changes in cloud cover and type in response to the temperature perturbations were not included.

4. **No precipitation scavenging dynamics**: The removal of soot by precipitation was parameterized rather than computed from first principles.

These limitations meant that the TTAPS results were best interpreted as estimates of the magnitude and duration of the climatic perturbation, not as precise quantitative predictions.

### The Soot Optical Properties

A critical input was the optical properties of soot -- its absorption and scattering efficiencies as a function of wavelength. The TTAPS team used laboratory measurements of soot from various combustion sources. Subsequent studies (particularly those led by Robock, Toon, and colleagues in the 2000s) have refined these optical properties, generally confirming the TTAPS estimates while providing more detailed spectral characterization.

## Physical Interpretation

### The Anti-Greenhouse Effect

The nuclear winter mechanism can be understood as an "anti-greenhouse" effect. The normal greenhouse effect traps outgoing infrared radiation while being transparent to incoming solar radiation, warming the surface. The soot layer does the opposite: it absorbs incoming solar radiation while being relatively transparent to outgoing infrared, cooling the surface. This is physically analogous to (but much more severe than) the climatic effect of large volcanic eruptions, which inject sulfate aerosols into the stratosphere and produce temporary cooling of ~0.5-1 degree C (as observed after the 1991 Pinatubo eruption).

### Comparison with Geological Events

The TTAPS team explicitly compared their results with known geological analogs:

- **Pinatubo-type volcanic eruptions**: ~0.5-1 degree C cooling for 1-2 years
- **Tambora eruption (1815)**: ~1-2 degrees C cooling, the "Year Without a Summer" (1816)
- **Cretaceous-Paleogene (K-Pg) impact**: The Alvarez asteroid would have produced darkness and cooling far exceeding nuclear winter, lasting months to years, and was associated with a mass extinction. Nuclear winter, while less severe than the K-Pg event, operates through the same physical mechanism.

### Agricultural Implications

Sagan and Ehrlich (1984, in a companion paper) analyzed the biological consequences, concluding that even a nuclear autumn (moderate cooling scenario) would be devastating to global agriculture. A 5-10 degree C drop in growing-season temperatures in the Northern Hemisphere mid-latitudes would eliminate most grain crops. Combined with darkness (reducing photosynthesis), radioactive contamination, and disruption of agricultural infrastructure, the result could be global famine affecting billions of people, including those in the Southern Hemisphere and in non-combatant nations.

## Impact and Legacy

### Policy Impact

The TTAPS paper had policy impact arguably unmatched by any other scientific publication of the late 20th century. Its findings were:

- Presented by Sagan and others directly to the U.S. Congress, the Soviet Academy of Sciences, and world leaders
- Credited by both American and Soviet officials with influencing nuclear arms control negotiations
- Cited by Mikhail Gorbachev as a factor in his decision to pursue nuclear arms reductions
- Instrumental in the negotiation of the Intermediate-Range Nuclear Forces (INF) Treaty (1987)
- Influential in the subsequent Strategic Arms Reduction Treaties (START I and II)

The political impact was amplified by Sagan's ability to communicate the findings to a broad public audience. His television appearances, popular writing, and public lectures brought the nuclear winter concept to mass consciousness.

### Scientific Debate

The TTAPS results were intensely debated within the atmospheric science community. Key criticisms included:

1. **The 1-D model limitation**: Three-dimensional GCM studies (beginning with those by Thompson and Schneider, 1986, and later by Robock and colleagues) showed that while the nuclear winter effect was real, the 1-D model overestimated the temperature drop in some scenarios by not accounting for oceanic thermal inertia and horizontal heat transport.

2. **Soot injection uncertainty**: The amount of soot reaching the stratosphere was difficult to estimate, and some researchers argued that TTAPS had overestimated this quantity.

3. **Scavenging efficiency**: Precipitation scavenging of soot might be more efficient than TTAPS assumed, reducing the atmospheric residence time.

4. **"Nuclear autumn" vs. "nuclear winter"**: Thompson and Schneider's (1986) 3-D GCM study found temperature drops of "only" 10-20 degrees C in many scenarios, leading them to propose the term "nuclear autumn" -- still devastating but less extreme than the TTAPS "nuclear winter."

However, every subsequent study has confirmed the core finding: large-scale nuclear war would produce significant, prolonged, global climatic disruption. The most recent studies by Robock, Toon, and colleagues (2007-2019) using modern GCMs have actually found results closer to the original TTAPS estimates than to the more moderate Thompson-Schneider predictions, because improved modeling of pyroconvection shows more efficient stratospheric injection than earlier 3-D studies had assumed.

### The "Nuclear Winter is Dead" Myth

During the 1980s and 1990s, some commentators (particularly those politically opposed to nuclear arms control) claimed that nuclear winter had been "disproven" or "discredited" by the Thompson-Schneider results. This characterization was inaccurate. The refinements moderated some of the most extreme TTAPS predictions but confirmed the central conclusion. The scientific consensus, as expressed in multiple subsequent reviews and studies, has consistently supported the reality of nuclear-winter-type effects.

## Connections to Modern Science

### Robock-Toon Studies (2007-present)

Alan Robock and Owen Toon (one of the original TTAPS authors) have led a modern reassessment of nuclear winter using state-of-the-art GCMs (ModelE from NASA GISS and WACCM from NCAR). Their findings:

- A regional nuclear war between India and Pakistan (100 Hiroshima-sized weapons) could produce global cooling of 1-2 degrees C lasting about a decade, with agricultural disruption affecting billions
- A large U.S.-Russia exchange (4,400 weapons) would produce cooling of 8-10 degrees C in the Northern Hemisphere, lasting more than a decade
- The agricultural effects would produce a global famine potentially killing billions of people through starvation, even in non-combatant nations

These modern results generally confirm TTAPS, with some modification of the timescales (longer duration due to improved modeling of stratospheric soot persistence) and geographical patterns (more severe in the Northern Hemisphere).

### Geoengineering Analog

The nuclear winter mechanism is, from a purely physical standpoint, the inverse of stratospheric aerosol injection (SAI), a proposed solar geoengineering approach to counteract greenhouse warming. In SAI, reflective particles (typically sulfate aerosols) would be intentionally injected into the stratosphere to increase planetary albedo and cool the surface. The TTAPS analysis and its successors have provided important insights into the physics and risks of such interventions, including the potential for rapid warming ("termination shock") if injection ceases abruptly.

### Asteroid Impact Science

The nuclear winter scenario is closely analogous to the climatic effects of large asteroid impacts, and the two research programs have been mutually informative. Modern models of the K-Pg impact winter draw on the same atmospheric physics developed for nuclear winter, and vice versa. The TTAPS team's planetary science background was directly relevant to both problems.

### Climate Model Validation

The nuclear winter problem has served as a test case for atmospheric models, particularly their treatment of aerosol-radiation interactions and their ability to simulate extreme climate perturbations. The comparison between TTAPS-era 1-D models and modern 3-D GCMs illustrates both the value and limitations of simple models in bounding the magnitude of climate perturbations.

### Policy Relevance

With approximately 12,500 nuclear warheads still in existence (as of the mid-2020s) and renewed geopolitical tensions, the nuclear winter findings remain urgently policy-relevant. The TTAPS paper and its successors continue to be cited in arms control discussions and in assessments of nuclear weapons policy.
