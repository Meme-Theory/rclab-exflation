# Phosphine Gas in the Cloud Decks of Venus: A Scholarly Analysis

**Authors:** Jane S. Greaves, Anita M.S. Richards, William Bains, Paul B. Rimmer, Hideo Sagawa, David L. Clements, Sara Seager, Janusz J. Petkowski, Clara Sousa-Silva, Sukrit Ranjan, Emily Drabek-Maunder, Helen J. Fraser, Annabel Cartwright, Ingo Mueller-Wodarg, Zhuchang Zhan, Per Friberg, Iain Coulson, E'Lisa Lee, and Jim Hoge
**Year:** 2020 (original publication September 14, 2020; revised November 2020)
**Journal:** *Nature Astronomy*, Vol. 5, pp. 655-664 (revised version)
**Context:** Published amidst growing interest in Venus as a potentially habitable world and in the expanded biosignature gas framework

---

## Abstract

Greaves et al.'s 2020 announcement of the detection of phosphine (PH3) in the cloud decks of Venus generated one of the most intense scientific debates in recent astrobiology. Using the James Clerk Maxwell Telescope (JCMT) in Hawaii and the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) in Chile, the team reported detecting a spectral absorption feature at 266.94 GHz, corresponding to the J=1-0 rotational transition of phosphine, in the Venus atmosphere at an altitude corresponding to the temperate cloud layer (~50-60 km). The inferred abundance was approximately 20 parts per billion (ppb) in the original paper, later revised downward to approximately 1-7 ppb after reanalysis of the ALMA data. The detection was significant because phosphine has no known abiotic production mechanism on a rocky planet that could produce the observed abundance; on Earth, phosphine is associated primarily with anaerobic biological processes (and industrial chemistry). The authors suggested, with appropriate caveats, that the phosphine could represent a potential biosignature -- evidence for microbial life in the Venus cloud layer. The paper ignited fierce debate about the data processing, the spectral identification, and the plausibility of both biological and abiotic explanations, and as of the mid-2020s, the question remains unresolved. Regardless of the ultimate resolution, the paper reinvigorated Venus science, stimulated major investment in Venus missions, and provided a real-world test case for the biosignature detection frameworks developed by Seager and others.

## Historical Context

### Venus Cloud Habitability

The idea that the Venus cloud layer might be habitable -- despite the hellish surface conditions that Sagan had demonstrated in 1961 -- has a surprisingly long history. Sagan himself, along with Harold Morowitz, proposed in 1967 that microorganisms might float in the temperate Venus cloud layer, where temperatures (0-60 degrees C) and pressures (0.4-2 atm) at altitudes of 50-65 km are comparable to Earth's surface. This idea was revisited periodically by Cockell (1999), Schulze-Makuch et al. (2004), and Limaye et al. (2018), who noted that:

1. The cloud layer has temperatures compatible with liquid water
2. Sulfuric acid, while hostile, might be tolerable for sufficiently adapted organisms (concentrated acid-tolerant organisms exist on Earth)
3. The "unknown UV absorber" in the Venus clouds had spectral properties potentially consistent with biological pigments
4. Particles in the clouds had size distributions and shape distributions not fully explained by known chemistry

### Phosphine as a Biosignature

The selection of phosphine as a biosignature target was directly motivated by the expanded biosignature gas research of Sara Seager, Clara Sousa-Silva, and collaborators. Sousa-Silva et al. (2020, published before the Greaves detection) had identified phosphine as a promising biosignature gas for rocky planets because:

1. On rocky planets with oxidizing atmospheres (like Venus and Earth), phosphine is thermodynamically unstable and should be rapidly destroyed by oxidation. Any detectable presence requires a continuous source.

2. On Earth, phosphine is produced by anaerobic bacteria (in swamps, sewage, the intestines of animals) and by industrial processes. No known abiotic geochemical pathway produces phosphine in significant quantities on rocky planets. (On gas giant planets, by contrast, PH3 is produced thermochemically at depth and transported upward.)

3. Phosphine has a strong rotational transition at 266.94 GHz, detectable by submillimeter telescopes.

### The Observational Campaign

Greaves, an astronomer at Cardiff University specializing in submillimeter observations of protoplanetary disks, initiated the Venus phosphine search as an exploratory observation -- essentially a long-shot test of the biosignature gas concept. The initial detection with JCMT was surprising and motivated the follow-up ALMA observations to confirm the signal at higher spectral resolution and sensitivity.

## Key Arguments and Evidence

### The Spectral Detection

**JCMT observations (June 2017)**: The JCMT detected an absorption feature at 266.94 GHz in the disk-integrated Venus spectrum. The feature had an apparent depth of ~20 ppb when interpreted as PH3 absorption against the Venus thermal continuum. The JCMT data had relatively coarse spectral resolution but clearly showed a feature at the correct frequency.

**ALMA observations (March 2019)**: ALMA observed Venus at much higher spectral resolution and spatial resolution. The original analysis reported a detection of phosphine at a similar abundance, localized to mid-latitudes rather than uniformly distributed across the disk.

**Revised ALMA analysis (November 2020)**: Following intense community scrutiny, the ALMA data were re-reduced by ALMA staff, who identified a calibration issue in the original spectral bandpass normalization. The revised analysis showed a weaker, less certain signal. The authors issued a revised paper with the abundance estimate reduced to approximately 1-7 ppb, with larger uncertainties.

### The Abiotic Source Assessment

Greaves et al. performed an extensive assessment of potential abiotic sources of phosphine in the Venus atmosphere:

1. **Photochemistry**: Known photochemical pathways do not produce PH3 in oxidizing atmospheres. The Venus atmosphere, while rich in sulfur compounds, is oxidizing in the relevant altitude range.

2. **Volcanism**: Volcanic outgassing on Earth does not produce significant PH3. While Venus has extensive volcanism, there is no known volcanic pathway to PH3 at the observed abundance.

3. **Lightning**: Electrical discharges in the Venus atmosphere might produce trace PH3, but estimated production rates fall many orders of magnitude short of the observed abundance.

4. **Meteoritic infall**: Phosphorus from meteoritic material could be reduced to PH3, but the flux is insufficient by several orders of magnitude.

5. **Surface-atmosphere reactions**: Reactions between phosphorus-bearing minerals and the hot, dense lower atmosphere could potentially produce PH3, but the authors argued this was insufficient.

The authors concluded that no known abiotic mechanism could produce 20 ppb (or even 1 ppb) of PH3 in the Venus atmosphere, leaving biology as a possible explanation. They were careful to state that this was not a claim of life detection but rather an observation that, if confirmed, would require either unknown chemistry or biology.

### The Biological Hypothesis

If the phosphine was biogenic, the implied biological scenario would be:

- Microbial life in the cloud layer at ~50-60 km altitude
- Anaerobic metabolism producing PH3 as a byproduct (as terrestrial anaerobes do)
- Organisms adapted to the extreme acidity (concentrated sulfuric acid), UV radiation, and desiccating conditions of the Venus clouds
- A microbial ecosystem sustained by solar energy and atmospheric chemical nutrients

This is a plausible (if speculative) scenario that connects to the earlier Venus cloud habitability hypotheses of Sagan-Morowitz, Cockell, and Limaye.

## Methodology and Data

### Submillimeter Spectroscopy

The observations used two of the world's premier submillimeter facilities:

**JCMT**: A 15-meter single-dish telescope at 4,092 m elevation on Maunakea, Hawaii. Operating at 266 GHz (~1.1 mm wavelength), it provided disk-integrated spectra of Venus with spectral resolution sufficient to detect a broad absorption feature.

**ALMA**: An interferometric array of 66 antennas in the Atacama Desert, Chile, at 5,000 m elevation. ALMA provided much higher spectral resolution (~1 MHz) and spatial resolution (capable of resolving features on Venus's disk).

### Spectral Processing Challenges

The Venus phosphine detection pushed the limits of submillimeter spectral data processing because:

1. **Venus is bright**: At 266 GHz, Venus is an extremely strong thermal emitter, requiring careful calibration of the receiver gain and bandpass shape.

2. **The signal is small**: The PH3 absorption feature represents a fractional change in the Venus continuum of only ~10^-4, requiring exquisite bandpass calibration.

3. **Spectral ripples**: Instrumental effects (standing waves in the receiver, bandpass calibration errors) can produce spurious spectral features at the level of the claimed detection.

4. **SO2 contamination**: Sulfur dioxide (SO2), which is abundant in the Venus atmosphere, has spectral lines near 266.94 GHz that could potentially be confused with PH3.

### The Calibration Controversy

The most significant technical challenge to the detection came from the ALMA data reprocessing. ALMA staff identified that the original spectral bandpass calibration (polynomial fitting to remove the instrumental spectral response) had been performed in a way that could introduce artifacts. When the data were reprocessed with a different calibration approach, the signal weakened substantially. The revised analysis showed a less significant detection at a lower abundance, with some researchers arguing that the residual signal was consistent with noise or SO2 contamination.

## Physical Interpretation

### Phosphine Chemistry on Venus

The physical interpretation hinges on the chemistry of phosphine in the Venus atmosphere:

**Destruction**: PH3 in the Venus cloud layer would be rapidly destroyed by reaction with oxidizing species (H2SO4, SO2, O, OH) and by UV photolysis. The estimated photochemical lifetime of PH3 at 55 km altitude is approximately 10^3 seconds -- very short. Maintaining any steady-state abundance requires a continuous production rate.

**Required production rate**: For 1 ppb PH3, the required global production rate is approximately 10^6-10^7 molecules cm^-2 s^-1 -- comparable to, but somewhat less than, the PH3 production rate on Earth from biological sources.

**Thermodynamic context**: PH3 is thermodynamically unstable in the oxidizing Venus atmosphere. Its presence at any detectable level represents a thermodynamic disequilibrium that requires a continuous energy source -- exactly the type of signature that Lovelock and Sagan identified as potentially biogenic.

### The SO2 Question

A critical interpretive challenge is the possible confusion of PH3 with SO2. While PH3 and SO2 have their primary transitions at different frequencies, SO2 has a weak spectral line very close to the PH3 J=1-0 transition. Lincowski et al. (2021) and Villanueva et al. (2021) argued that the claimed PH3 feature could be entirely or partially explained by SO2 absorption, depending on the assumed SO2 vertical profile. The Greaves team disputed this interpretation, arguing that the SO2 abundance required to explain the feature was inconsistent with other observations.

### The Mesospheric SO2 Problem

An additional complication is the "mesospheric SO2 problem": above ~70 km, the SO2 abundance in the Venus atmosphere drops dramatically due to photodissociation. If the absorbing species is SO2 rather than PH3, the required mesospheric SO2 abundance may be unrealistically high. This argument has been debated in detail, with different atmospheric models giving different conclusions.

## Impact and Legacy

### Scientific Debate

The Greaves et al. paper generated an extraordinary volume of follow-up analysis, replication attempts, and criticism:

**Skeptical analyses**: Villanueva et al. (2021) reanalyzed the ALMA data and found no significant PH3 signal after correcting for calibration artifacts. Snellen et al. (2020) found that the JCMT data, processed differently, showed a less significant detection. Thompson (2021) argued that the signal processing introduced systematic artifacts.

**Supportive analyses**: Greaves et al. (2021, 2022) re-examined the data with multiple independent processing methods and argued that a signal at 1-5 ppb persisted across analyses. Mogul et al. (2021) found evidence for phosphine in archival Pioneer Venus mass spectrometry data, though this interpretation was debated.

**Independent observations**: Cordiner et al. (2022) used the SOFIA airborne telescope and did not detect PH3, placing an upper limit of ~0.8 ppb. Encrenaz et al. (2020) reported non-detection in infrared ground-based observations. These non-detections were consistent with the revised (lower) Greaves estimate if the PH3 abundance varies temporally or is close to the detection limits.

### Venus Mission Acceleration

Perhaps the most tangible impact of the paper was its catalytic effect on Venus mission selection:

- **DAVINCI** (Deep Atmosphere Venus Investigation of Noble gases, Chemistry, and Imaging): Selected by NASA in June 2021 as a Discovery-class mission. Its atmospheric probe will descend through the Venus atmosphere, directly sampling the cloud layer and measuring chemical composition including phosphorus species.

- **VERITAS** (Venus Emissivity, Radio Science, InSAR, Topography, and Spectroscopy): Also selected by NASA in June 2021 for orbital mapping of Venus's surface and atmosphere.

- **EnVision**: Selected by ESA in June 2021 for detailed orbital investigation of Venus.

While these missions had been in development before the phosphine announcement, the public and scientific excitement generated by Greaves et al. is widely credited with increasing their priority and likelihood of selection.

### Test Case for Biosignature Frameworks

The phosphine controversy provided the first real-world test of the biosignature assessment frameworks that Seager, Catling, and others had developed. The community's response -- immediate skepticism about the detection, systematic evaluation of abiotic alternatives, demands for independent confirmation, and careful Bayesian assessment of the biological hypothesis -- demonstrated that the intellectual infrastructure for biosignature evaluation had matured. Whether or not PH3 is confirmed on Venus, the episode validated the approach of the framework and identified areas where it needs strengthening (particularly regarding the treatment of systematic observational uncertainties).

### Public Engagement

The paper generated enormous public interest -- it was the most widely covered astrobiology result since ALH84001. The combination of a familiar planet (Venus), a sensational claim (possible life), and a transparent scientific debate (played out in real time through preprints and social media) made it a compelling science story. It demonstrated both the public appetite for astrobiology news and the importance of scientific self-correction in maintaining public trust.

## Connections to Modern Science

### JWST and Venus

JWST has the capability to observe Venus in reflected sunlight and thermal emission, potentially detecting or constraining PH3 and other trace gases with higher sensitivity than ground-based facilities. Venus observations with JWST have been discussed and may provide independent constraints on the phosphine question.

### DAVINCI Mission

DAVINCI, expected to arrive at Venus in the early 2030s, will provide the definitive measurement. Its atmospheric probe includes a mass spectrometer (VAMS) capable of measuring PH3 and other phosphorus species directly during descent through the cloud layer. If PH3 is present at any level above ~0.01 ppb, DAVINCI should detect it and determine its altitude profile, providing the data needed to distinguish biological from abiotic sources.

### Expanded Venus Habitability Research

The phosphine controversy stimulated a broader reassessment of Venus's potential habitability. New studies have:
- Modeled the water activity in Venus cloud droplets (finding it may be too low for known life, but not conclusively so)
- Investigated potential metabolisms that could function in concentrated sulfuric acid
- Examined the atmospheric circulation patterns that could support a cloud-based biosphere
- Proposed new biosignature gases specific to a sulfuric acid environment

### Lessons for Exoplanet Biosignatures

The Venus phosphine experience has provided crucial lessons that will inform the interpretation of future exoplanet biosignature detections:

1. **Systematic uncertainties dominate**: The debate has centered not on photon noise but on calibration systematics, spectral confusion, and data processing choices. Future biosignature detections will face similar challenges.

2. **Context is essential**: The interpretation of PH3 depends critically on the Venus atmospheric context -- SO2 abundance, photochemistry, volcanism, cloud composition. Similarly, exoplanet biosignature interpretation will require detailed contextual information.

3. **Independent confirmation is mandatory**: A single detection, no matter how carefully analyzed, is insufficient for a claim as significant as extraterrestrial life. Multiple independent observations with different instruments, at different wavelengths, are needed.

4. **The null hypothesis must be rigorously tested**: Before invoking biology, every plausible abiotic explanation must be evaluated quantitatively. The community's systematic exploration of abiotic PH3 sources on Venus exemplifies this principle.

5. **Communication matters**: The way the result was communicated -- as a tentative detection requiring confirmation, not as a claim of life -- was appropriate and should serve as a model for future announcements.

### Broader Astrobiology

The phosphine episode illustrates a general truth about the search for life beyond Earth: the first detection is unlikely to be a clean, unambiguous signal. It will more likely be a marginal, debated, context-dependent observation that requires years of follow-up to resolve. The Venus phosphine experience is preparing the community -- scientifically, methodologically, and culturally -- for this reality. In this sense, regardless of whether PH3 is confirmed on Venus, the Greaves et al. paper has already made a lasting contribution to astrobiology by demonstrating what the process of biosignature detection actually looks like in practice.
