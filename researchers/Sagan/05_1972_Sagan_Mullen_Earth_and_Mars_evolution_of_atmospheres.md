# Earth and Mars: Evolution of Atmospheres and Surface Temperatures -- A Scholarly Analysis

**Authors:** Carl Sagan and George Mullen
**Year:** 1972
**Journal:** *Science*, Vol. 177, No. 4043, pp. 52-56

---

## Abstract

Sagan and Mullen's 1972 paper identified and named what has become one of the most important problems in planetary science and paleoclimatology: the Faint Young Sun Paradox. Standard stellar evolution theory predicts that the Sun's luminosity has increased by approximately 25-30% over the 4.6-billion-year history of the solar system. If Earth's atmosphere had always had its present composition, this lower solar luminosity would have resulted in a globally frozen planet for roughly the first 2 billion years of Earth's history. Yet geological evidence unambiguously demonstrates that liquid water has been present on Earth's surface continuously since at least 3.8 billion years ago, and possibly since 4.4 billion years ago. Sagan and Mullen proposed that the paradox could be resolved by invoking a stronger greenhouse effect in Earth's early atmosphere, specifically suggesting ammonia (NH3) as the greenhouse gas responsible for maintaining clement temperatures under the fainter young Sun. They extended the analysis to Mars, arguing that a similar early greenhouse could explain evidence for liquid water on the Martian surface in the planet's past. While their specific proposal of ammonia has not withstood scrutiny (NH3 is rapidly photodissociated by ultraviolet light), the paradox they identified remains one of the central unsolved problems in Earth and planetary science, and their framing of the problem has shaped all subsequent work on early planetary climates.

## Historical Context

### Stellar Evolution and Solar Luminosity

By the early 1970s, the theory of stellar evolution on the main sequence was well established. Stars like the Sun generate energy through hydrogen fusion in their cores. As hydrogen is converted to helium, the mean molecular weight of the core increases, the core contracts slightly, and the fusion rate increases to maintain hydrostatic equilibrium. The result is a steady increase in luminosity over the star's main-sequence lifetime. For the Sun, standard models predicted (and still predict) that the zero-age main-sequence luminosity was approximately 70% of the present value, increasing quasi-linearly over 4.6 billion years.

This prediction was robust and essentially model-independent -- it followed from fundamental nuclear physics and the equation of stellar structure. It was not a controversial or uncertain result.

### The Geological Record

Simultaneously, geologists had established that liquid water had been present on Earth's surface for most of its history. The evidence included:

- **Sedimentary rocks** dating back to at least 3.8 billion years (from the Isua Supracrustal Belt in Greenland), which formed through water-mediated processes
- **Pillow basalts** of similar age, which form when lava erupts underwater
- **Stromatolites** dating to at least 3.5 billion years ago (possibly 3.7 billion), indicating photosynthetic microbial communities living in shallow water
- **Detrital zircons** from Jack Hills, Western Australia, with oxygen isotope ratios suggesting interaction with liquid water at 4.4 billion years ago (this evidence postdated Sagan's paper but strengthened the paradox)

The geological evidence was unambiguous: Earth had not been frozen during the Archean eon. Something had kept the planet warm despite the fainter Sun.

### The State of Knowledge Before Sagan and Mullen

Remarkably, the tension between stellar evolution and the geological record had not been clearly articulated before Sagan and Mullen's paper. Individual researchers in stellar physics and geology were of course aware of their respective findings, but the sharp contradiction between them had not been framed as a unified problem requiring resolution. This is a characteristic example of how disciplinary boundaries can prevent the recognition of fundamental problems that span multiple fields. Sagan, with his characteristic ability to synthesize across disciplines, was ideally positioned to identify the paradox.

## Key Arguments and Evidence

### Quantifying the Paradox

Sagan and Mullen began by computing the effective temperature of Earth as a function of time, using the standard solar luminosity evolution. The effective temperature is:

T_eff = [S(t)(1-A) / (4 sigma)]^(1/4)

where S(t) is the solar flux at Earth's orbit, A is the planetary albedo, and sigma is the Stefan-Boltzmann constant. With a constant albedo equal to the present value (~0.30), this gives an effective temperature that falls below the freezing point of water for all times earlier than about 2 billion years ago.

The actual situation is even worse than this calculation suggests, because a frozen planet would have a higher albedo (due to ice and snow cover), leading to further cooling through the ice-albedo feedback. Once initiated, global glaciation would be difficult to reverse -- this is the "Snowball Earth" scenario that would be developed quantitatively by Budyko (1969) and Sellers (1969) and applied to the Neoproterozoic by Kirschvink (1992) and Hoffman et al. (1998). The positive feedback means that the paradox is not merely a quantitative discrepancy but a qualitative instability: under a fainter Sun with the present atmosphere, Earth should have frozen solid and remained frozen.

### The Greenhouse Solution

Sagan and Mullen proposed that the resolution lay in a stronger greenhouse effect in Earth's early atmosphere. They argued that the early atmosphere was likely very different from the present one, having formed from volcanic outgassing of the mantle rather than from the oxygen-rich atmosphere produced by later photosynthesis. A reducing or weakly oxidizing early atmosphere could contain greenhouse gases in much higher concentrations than today, providing the additional warming needed to compensate for the fainter Sun.

### The Ammonia Proposal

Specifically, Sagan and Mullen proposed ammonia (NH3) as the principal early greenhouse gas. Their reasoning:

1. **Volcanic outgassing**: NH3 could be produced by outgassing from a reducing mantle, which the early Earth likely had before the oxidation of the mantle through loss of hydrogen to space.

2. **Greenhouse potency**: NH3 is a very effective greenhouse gas, with strong absorption bands in the thermal infrared, particularly in the 8-13 micrometer "atmospheric window" where neither CO2 nor H2O absorbs strongly. Even small concentrations (parts per million to parts per billion) could provide significant greenhouse warming.

3. **Quantitative adequacy**: Sagan and Mullen calculated that NH3 mixing ratios of roughly 10 parts per million could provide sufficient greenhouse warming to maintain liquid water under the faint young Sun.

4. **Biological implications**: An early NH3-rich atmosphere would have implications for prebiotic chemistry, since ammonia is a key feedstock for the synthesis of amino acids and nucleobases (as demonstrated in Miller-Urey-type experiments).

### Extension to Mars

Sagan and Mullen extended the analysis to Mars, noting that:

1. Mars receives about 43% of Earth's solar flux, making the faint young Sun problem even more severe for early Mars.
2. Evidence for past liquid water on Mars (valley networks, outflow channels) was beginning to emerge from Mariner 9 images (the mission was contemporaneous with the paper).
3. A stronger early greenhouse on Mars, perhaps from a thicker CO2 atmosphere supplemented by other greenhouse gases, could explain how Mars was warm enough for liquid water in its early history.
4. The subsequent loss of Mars's early atmosphere (through a combination of escape to space, sputtering by the solar wind in the absence of a global magnetic field, and carbonate formation) would explain the transition from early warm-wet conditions to the present cold-dry state.

## Methodology and Data

### Energy Balance Modeling

The methodology was straightforward energy balance modeling -- the same approach that Budyko and Sellers had recently applied to Earth's present climate. The key equation balances absorbed solar radiation against emitted thermal radiation, with the greenhouse effect parameterized as a correction to the effective temperature:

T_surface = T_eff + Delta_T_greenhouse

Sagan and Mullen computed Delta_T_greenhouse as a function of NH3 concentration using simplified radiative transfer calculations for the additional infrared opacity.

### Stellar Evolution Models

The solar luminosity as a function of time was taken from standard stellar evolution models. Sagan and Mullen used the parameterization:

L(t) / L_0 = 1 / [1 + 0.4(1 - t/t_0)]

where t_0 is the present age and L_0 is the present luminosity. This gives L ~ 0.70 L_0 at formation and ~0.75 L_0 at 3.8 Gyr ago. Modern stellar models give very similar values, confirming that the input was robust.

### Geological Constraints

The constraints on early liquid water were drawn from the geological literature on Archean and early Proterozoic sedimentary rocks. Sagan and Mullen did not conduct original geological fieldwork but synthesized the existing evidence to establish the boundary condition: liquid water at the surface continuously since at least 3.8 billion years ago.

## Physical Interpretation

### The Paradox as a Constraint

The most important intellectual contribution of the paper was reframing a seeming inconsistency as a powerful constraint on early atmospheric composition. If the Sun was fainter and the Earth was not frozen, then the atmosphere must have been different. This logical structure -- using the existence of liquid water as a thermometer for the early atmosphere -- has proven enormously productive.

### Why Not Just Higher CO2?

Sagan and Mullen's choice of ammonia rather than CO2 as the principal warming agent reflected the geochemical understanding of the early 1970s. The idea that the early atmosphere might have had very high CO2 concentrations was not yet well developed. Subsequent work by Walker, Hays, and Kasting (1981) and Kasting (1987) would show that CO2 was a more plausible candidate for several reasons:

1. **Photolysis problem**: NH3 is rapidly photodissociated by ultraviolet radiation, with a photochemical lifetime of only about 10 years in the atmosphere. Maintaining significant NH3 concentrations would require an enormous continuous source, far exceeding plausible volcanic outgassing rates.

2. **CO2 stability**: CO2 is photochemically stable and can accumulate in the atmosphere over geological time. The carbonate-silicate cycle provides a natural thermostat (Walker et al. 1981) that tends to maintain surface temperatures by adjusting CO2 levels in response to temperature changes.

3. **Geological evidence**: There is no geological evidence for high NH3 concentrations in the Archean atmosphere, while there is some evidence (though debated) for elevated CO2.

However, even CO2 alone may be insufficient to resolve the paradox for the earliest Archean (there are limits on how much CO2 the atmosphere can hold before it begins to condense at high altitudes), which has led to proposals for additional greenhouse contributions from methane (CH4), produced by early methanogenic bacteria, and other gases. The search for the complete solution to the Faint Young Sun Paradox continues.

### Mars: A Case of Failed Climate Stability

Sagan's extension to Mars was prescient. Mars likely did have a thicker early atmosphere and warmer conditions, but unlike Earth, it lost its early greenhouse through atmospheric escape (facilitated by the loss of its global magnetic field and its lower gravity). The contrast between Earth (which maintained its greenhouse through geological feedbacks) and Mars (which lost its greenhouse irreversibly) is one of the central comparative planetology results of the subsequent half-century.

## Impact and Legacy

### A Foundational Problem

The Faint Young Sun Paradox has become one of the most cited and discussed problems in Earth system science, paleoclimatology, and astrobiology. Virtually every textbook on planetary science, atmospheric physics, or Earth history discusses it, invariably citing Sagan and Mullen (1972) as the founding paper. The problem has spawned hundreds of subsequent papers and remains an active area of research.

### The Carbonate-Silicate Cycle

The most important theoretical response to the paradox was the identification of the carbonate-silicate cycle as a planetary thermostat (Walker, Hays, and Kasting, 1981). This negative feedback loop, in which silicate weathering removes CO2 from the atmosphere (faster at higher temperatures) while volcanic outgassing returns it, naturally tends to maintain surface temperatures near the liquid water stability range. The carbonate-silicate cycle is now considered the fundamental mechanism for long-term climate regulation on terrestrial planets and is a key factor in defining the habitable zone. This entire line of research was initiated by the need to resolve the paradox Sagan and Mullen identified.

### Habitability Zone Calculations

The Faint Young Sun Paradox directly influences calculations of the habitable zone around other stars. If a planet's climate stability depends on the carbonate-silicate cycle or other atmospheric feedbacks, then the habitable zone is not simply determined by the stellar flux but also by the planet's geological activity, atmospheric composition, and feedback mechanisms. This enriched understanding of habitability owes its origin to the paradox.

### Snowball Earth

The Faint Young Sun Paradox is intellectually linked to the Snowball Earth hypothesis, which proposes that Earth underwent episodes of near-complete glaciation during the Neoproterozoic (~720-635 Mya) and possibly the Paleoproterozoic (~2.4-2.1 Gya). The Paleoproterozoic Snowball episodes may be directly related to the faint young Sun problem, potentially triggered by the rise of atmospheric oxygen (which would have destroyed methane, a potent greenhouse gas) combined with still-low solar luminosity.

## Connections to Modern Science

### Ongoing Debate

The Faint Young Sun Paradox remains unsolved in detail. While the carbonate-silicate cycle and elevated CO2/CH4 are the leading candidates for maintaining early warmth, quantitative modeling has not yet produced a universally accepted solution. Recent proposals include:

- **Nitrogen pressure**: A thicker N2 atmosphere would enhance pressure broadening of CO2 absorption lines, increasing greenhouse warming (Goldblatt et al. 2009)
- **Reduced cloud cover**: Lower concentrations of cloud condensation nuclei in the pristine early atmosphere could have reduced cloud albedo, allowing more solar absorption (Rosing et al. 2010)
- **Solar mass loss**: If the young Sun was slightly more massive and lost mass through a stronger solar wind, it might have been more luminous than standard models predict (though this requires fine-tuning)
- **Methane haze**: High CH4/CO2 ratios can produce an organic haze that both absorbs incoming solar radiation and provides some greenhouse warming

### Exoplanet Habitability

The paradox is directly relevant to the assessment of habitability for planets around other stars, particularly around K and M dwarfs (which evolve more slowly and may present even more severe versions of the faint young star problem for different reasons). Understanding how Earth solved the faint young Sun problem is essential for predicting whether similar planets around other stars can maintain habitable conditions.

### Early Mars Climate

The Mars component of Sagan and Mullen's analysis has become an entire subfield. The evidence for early liquid water on Mars (valley networks, phyllosilicate minerals, deltaic deposits) has grown enormously since 1972, but the mechanism for early Mars warming remains debated. CO2 alone appears insufficient (CO2 ice clouds form before adequate warming is achieved), leading to proposals for additional greenhouse contributions from H2, SO2, or impact-generated reducing atmospheres.

### The Habitability of Icy Moons

The general principle that Sagan and Mullen articulated -- that a planet's surface temperature depends on atmospheric greenhouse warming as well as stellar flux -- has been extended to icy moons where tidal heating (rather than greenhouse warming) provides the energy for liquid water. The conceptual framework of identifying energy sources that can maintain liquid water in environments that would otherwise be frozen applies broadly across planetary science.
