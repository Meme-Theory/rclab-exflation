# The Long Winter Model of Martian Biology: A Scholarly Analysis

**Author:** Carl Sagan
**Year:** 1971
**Journal:** *Icarus*, Vol. 15, pp. 511-514
**Context:** Written in the period between the Mariner 6/7 flybys (1969) and the Mariner 9 orbital mission (1971)

---

## Abstract

Sagan's 1971 paper proposed a model for how biology might persist on Mars despite the planet's hostile surface conditions. Following the disappointing Mariner 4 (1965) and Mariner 6/7 (1969) results, which had revealed a cratered, apparently lifeless surface with a thin CO2 atmosphere, low surface pressure, no detectable magnetic field, and intense ultraviolet radiation, many scientists had essentially written off the possibility of extant Martian life. Sagan argued against premature closure of this question by constructing what he called the "long winter model": a scenario in which Martian organisms had evolved to survive the current hostile epoch as dormant forms in subsurface refugia, analogous to terrestrial organisms that survive extreme seasonal conditions through hibernation or sporulation. He noted that Mars's obliquity (axial tilt) undergoes large oscillations over geological time, and that during high-obliquity epochs, the Martian climate could be substantially warmer and wetter. Organisms that had evolved during such favorable periods might persist through the intervening hostile epochs in dormant states, awaiting the return of habitable conditions. The paper is notable for introducing the concept of subsurface biological refugia on Mars, for its early application of extremophile biology to planetary science, and for its influence on the design of subsequent life-detection missions.

## Historical Context

### Post-Mariner Pessimism

The early Mars missions had a devastating effect on optimism about Martian life. Mariner 4 (1965) returned the first close-up images of Mars, showing a cratered, Moon-like surface rather than the geologically active world many had hoped for. Mariners 6 and 7 (1969) confirmed the thin CO2 atmosphere (surface pressure ~6 mbar), detected no nitrogen or oxygen in significant quantities, and found surface temperatures too cold for liquid water over most of the surface. The ultraviolet environment at the Martian surface was shown to be intensely sterilizing, with no ozone layer to block short-wavelength UV radiation.

These findings produced a consensus among many planetary scientists that Mars was essentially sterile. The romantic image of a Mars with canals and civilizations had already been abandoned, but now even the modest hope for microbial life seemed to be fading. Sagan's paper was a deliberate counterargument to this premature pessimism.

### The Obliquity Connection

A critical piece of Sagan's argument was the work of Ward (1973, though earlier preliminary results were available) and others on the long-term orbital dynamics of Mars. Unlike Earth, whose obliquity is stabilized by the Moon at approximately 23.5 degrees, Mars lacks a large stabilizing satellite and consequently undergoes large chaotic oscillations in obliquity, ranging from near 0 degrees to over 60 degrees on timescales of tens of millions of years. At high obliquity, the poles receive intense summer insolation, potentially releasing large quantities of CO2 and H2O from the polar caps, thickening the atmosphere, and creating episodic warm, wet conditions. This orbital/climate connection was essential to Sagan's argument, though the detailed calculations of obliquity evolution would be refined substantially in subsequent decades (Laskar, Touma, and Wisdom in the 1990s).

### Extremophile Biology

Sagan also drew on the emerging understanding of extremophile organisms on Earth. By the early 1970s, microorganisms had been discovered in increasingly hostile environments: hot springs, hypersaline lakes, Antarctic dry valleys, deep subsurface rocks. The concept that life could persist under conditions far more extreme than those typical of Earth's surface was becoming established, though the full scope of extremophile diversity would not be appreciated until later decades (culminating in the discovery of deep subsurface microbial ecosystems in the 1990s and 2000s).

## Key Arguments and Evidence

### The Environmental Challenge

Sagan began by honestly cataloguing the hostile conditions on the Martian surface:

1. **Low atmospheric pressure**: At ~6 mbar, the surface pressure is below the triple point of water (6.1 mbar), meaning that liquid water is thermodynamically unstable at the surface over most of Mars. Water would sublimate directly from ice to vapor.

2. **Extreme UV radiation**: With no ozone layer and a thin atmosphere, the Martian surface receives a flux of UV-C radiation (wavelengths below 280 nm) that is lethal to unshielded terrestrial organisms on timescales of seconds to minutes. This radiation damages DNA and other biomolecules through direct photolysis and through the production of reactive oxygen species.

3. **Low temperatures**: Mean surface temperatures on Mars are approximately 210 K (-63 degrees C), with nighttime temperatures dropping to 150 K or below at high latitudes. Even in the warmest regions at the warmest times, surface temperatures rarely exceed 290 K (17 degrees C) and only briefly.

4. **Oxidizing surface chemistry**: The Martian surface, as would later be confirmed by Viking, contains strong oxidants (now known to include perchlorates) that would destroy organic molecules at or near the surface.

5. **Absence of detectable organic matter**: No organic molecules had been detected in Martian surface material (this constraint would be strengthened by the Viking GCMS results in 1976).

### The Refugia Hypothesis

Against this inventory of hostility, Sagan proposed that Martian organisms need not survive the current conditions as active metabolizers. Instead, they might persist in one or more types of refugia:

**Subsurface refugia**: Below a few centimeters to meters of regolith, UV radiation is completely attenuated. If subsurface ice exists (as orbital and thermal modeling suggested), then even at current conditions, microenvironments with liquid water could exist at depth, particularly in geothermally heated regions or in localized pockets where salts depress the freezing point.

**Rock interiors**: By analogy with terrestrial endolithic organisms (which live inside rocks in the Antarctic dry valleys, as would be extensively documented by Friedmann beginning in the late 1970s), Martian organisms might inhabit the interiors of porous rocks, where they would be shielded from UV and where thin films of liquid water might periodically form.

**Polar regions**: The seasonal cycling of CO2 and possibly H2O at the poles might create transient habitable microenvironments, where organisms could be active briefly during favorable conditions and dormant the rest of the time.

### The Long Winter Mechanism

The "long winter" concept was Sagan's key innovation. He argued by analogy with terrestrial biology:

- Bacterial spores can survive for millions of years in a dormant state, withstanding extreme desiccation, radiation, and temperature fluctuations.
- Many terrestrial organisms survive unfavorable seasons (winter, dry seasons) through dormancy mechanisms, resuming activity when conditions improve.
- If the Martian climate oscillates between hostile and habitable states on million-year timescales (due to obliquity variations), then organisms that evolved during habitable epochs could survive the intervening hostile epochs as spores or other dormant forms in protected subsurface locations.
- The current epoch might represent one such "long winter" -- a hostile period in a much longer climatic cycle that includes intermittent habitable episodes.

### Predictions

Sagan made several testable predictions:

1. Evidence of past habitable conditions (warmer, wetter climate) should be found in the geological record.
2. Subsurface environments should be more habitable than the surface.
3. If organisms exist, they should show adaptations for extreme dormancy, UV resistance, and desiccation tolerance.
4. The best places to search for life are subsurface locations, rock interiors, and high-latitude regions near seasonal frost deposits.

## Methodology and Data

### Analogy-Based Reasoning

Sagan's methodology was primarily argument by analogy, supported by physical calculations. The paper was not a data analysis in the conventional sense but rather a synthesis of observational constraints from Mariner missions, theoretical climate modeling, orbital dynamics calculations, and terrestrial extremophile biology into a coherent hypothesis. This type of synthetic, hypothesis-generating paper was characteristic of Sagan's best work, and while it does not carry the evidentiary weight of a measurement paper, it plays a different and essential role in science: identifying what should be tested and why.

### Physical Calculations

The paper included order-of-magnitude calculations for:

- UV penetration depth in Martian regolith (a few millimeters to centimeters for UV-C)
- Subsurface temperature profiles and the depth at which liquid water might persist
- The energetics of dormancy and the metabolic requirements for long-term survival
- The timescales of obliquity variation and the duration of potentially habitable epochs

### Biological Plausibility Arguments

Sagan drew on the known capabilities of terrestrial extremophiles to argue for the biological plausibility of his model. He noted that:

- *Deinococcus radiodurans* (known at the time as *Micrococcus radiodurans*) could survive radiation doses far exceeding those at the Martian surface
- Bacterial spores had been revived after millions of years in amber (a claim that was controversial then and remains so)
- Terrestrial organisms had been found in essentially every environment where liquid water existed, no matter how extreme the other conditions

## Physical Interpretation

### The Climate-Biology Coupling

The most important physical insight in the paper is the coupling between orbital mechanics, climate, and biology on timescales far longer than any individual organism's lifetime. Sagan recognized that the relevant timescale for assessing Mars's habitability was not the current epoch but the full range of conditions the planet experiences over its obliquity cycle. This reframing -- from "Is Mars habitable now?" to "Has Mars ever been habitable, and could life persist through the intervening hostile periods?" -- fundamentally changed the scientific question and kept the possibility of Martian life open for serious investigation.

### The Dormancy Bridge

The concept of biological dormancy as a mechanism for bridging unfavorable conditions on planetary timescales was scientifically novel. On Earth, dormancy typically bridges seasonal variations (months) or at most glacial-interglacial cycles (tens of thousands of years). Sagan was proposing dormancy on million-year timescales -- far longer than any demonstrated terrestrial case, but not obviously impossible given the known stability of bacterial spores and the absence of any clear physical limit to dormancy duration (as opposed to empirical limits set by the current oldest revived spore claims).

### UV and Oxidant Shielding

Sagan's emphasis on the subsurface as a potential refuge from UV and oxidants anticipated the modern understanding of Mars's habitability gradient with depth. The surface is indeed sterilizing, but conditions improve rapidly with depth: UV is blocked within millimeters, temperature fluctuations are damped within centimeters, and oxidant concentrations may decrease within meters. This vertical habitability gradient is now a central concept in Mars astrobiology.

## Impact and Legacy

### Influence on Viking

Sagan's arguments were influential in the design and interpretation of the Viking biology experiments (1976). The Viking landers included three life-detection experiments (Labeled Release, Gas Exchange, and Pyrolytic Release) specifically designed to detect microbial activity in Martian surface soil. While the Viking results were officially interpreted as negative (the positive Labeled Release results were attributed to abiotic oxidant chemistry), the fact that life detection was included as a primary mission objective owed much to Sagan and others who argued that the question remained scientifically open.

### Subsurface Astrobiology

The concept of subsurface refugia has become the dominant paradigm in Mars astrobiology. Modern Mars missions (Curiosity, Perseverance) are designed to investigate past habitable environments, and the most promising targets for extant life are universally agreed to be subsurface: the polar ice sheets, deep aquifers (if they exist), and subsurface brines. The Mars Sample Return mission architecture and the ExoMars drill (capable of reaching 2 meters depth) are direct reflections of Sagan's argument that the surface is the wrong place to look.

### Extremophile Revolution

The subsequent discovery of an astonishing diversity of terrestrial extremophiles has broadly supported Sagan's biological plausibility arguments. Deep subsurface microbial communities have been found in terrestrial rock to depths of several kilometers. Organisms have been found in the driest, coldest, most radiation-exposed environments on Earth. The discovery of subglacial lakes in Antarctica (Lake Vostok and others) provided direct analogs for potential subsurface water bodies on Mars. Each of these discoveries has made Sagan's refugia hypothesis more plausible.

### Obliquity and Climate

The detailed calculation of Mars's obliquity evolution by Laskar and colleagues (1993, 2004) confirmed the essential features of Sagan's climate argument. Mars's obliquity has indeed varied chaotically between roughly 10 and 60 degrees over the past several billion years, and at high obliquities, substantial atmospheric thickening and polar ice mobilization would occur. Whether these episodes produce conditions warm and wet enough for biological activity remains debated, but the basic mechanism Sagan invoked has been quantitatively validated.

## Connections to Modern Science

### Mars Subsurface Water

The discovery by Mars Express (MARSIS radar) of what appears to be a subsurface lake or brine pocket beneath the south polar ice cap (Orosei et al. 2018) represents perhaps the most direct confirmation of the type of subsurface habitable environment Sagan envisioned. If confirmed, such a body would satisfy all the requirements of a refugium: liquid water, UV shielding, temperature moderation, and potential energy sources from water-rock interactions.

### Perchlorate Discovery

The detection of perchlorates in Martian soil by the Phoenix lander (2008) and Curiosity rover added a new dimension to the habitability question. Perchlorates are simultaneously a challenge (they are toxic to most terrestrial life and create a strongly oxidizing surface environment) and an opportunity (they depress the freezing point of water, potentially enabling liquid brines at temperatures and pressures where pure water would be frozen). This duality exemplifies the complexity of habitability assessment that Sagan's simple but prescient analysis anticipated.

### Sample Return and Life Detection

The Mars 2020 Perseverance rover is currently collecting samples in Jezero Crater for eventual return to Earth via the Mars Sample Return (MSR) campaign. The primary science objective is to search for biosignatures -- exactly the investigation Sagan argued was necessary. The emphasis on past habitability (Jezero is a dried lake bed) and the inclusion of subsurface-accessed samples in the collection plan reflect the intellectual framework Sagan helped establish.

### Enceladus and Europa

The refugia concept has been extended far beyond Mars. The subsurface oceans of Europa and Enceladus are essentially the ultimate expression of Sagan's subsurface refugium idea: entire liquid water oceans shielded from surface radiation by kilometers of ice, potentially harboring life. The intellectual path from Sagan's Martian subsurface refugia to the Europa Clipper and Enceladus missions is direct and traceable.
