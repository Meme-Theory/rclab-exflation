# The Significance of Non-Empirical Confirmation in Fundamental Physics

**Author(s):** Richard Dawid
**Year:** 2017
**Journal:** In "Why Trust a Theory?" (Oxford University Press, 2015); expanded as arXiv:1702.01133

---

## Abstract

Ellis and Silk (2014) argued that non-empirical theories (string theory, inflation, multiverse) should be excluded from physics because they are non-falsifiable. Dawid offers a counterargument: non-empirical confirmation can be scientifically meaningful. He identifies three types of arguments that structurally resemble empirical confirmation and can provide reliable non-empirical support for theories:

1. **Structural constraints arguments** — elegant mathematical frameworks that restrict possible theories
2. **No-alternatives arguments** — absence of competing explanations
3. **Expected explanatory connections** — theories that promise to explain currently mysterious phenomena

These arguments are not empirical, but they share features with empirical confirmation and deserve philosophical respect. They do not replace empirical testing but supplement it when empirical data are inaccessible. This position challenges the Popperian demarcation criterion and defends string theory, inflation, and other non-empirical frameworks as scientifically legitimate.

## Historical Context

Dawid, a philosopher of physics, wrote this paper in explicit response to Ellis-Silk 2014:

- **September 2014**: Nature publishes Ellis-Silk op-ed demanding falsifiability
- **October 2014 – March 2015**: String theory community drafts responses. Susskind, Vafa, and others argue that consistency constraints *are* empirical (in the sense that they select among theories). Dawid undertakes a formal philosophical analysis.
- **2015**: Dawid's monograph "String Theory and the Scientific Method" (Cambridge University Press) develops the full argument
- **2017**: Expanded article on arXiv for broader audience

The debate is essential: Is philosophy of science (Popper's falsifiability) binding on physics? Or can physics develop standards beyond falsifiability?

## Key Arguments

### The Inadequacy of Falsifiability Alone

Popper's criterion (a theory is scientific iff falsifiable) has problems:

1. **Auxiliary hypotheses problem**: Any theory can be saved from falsification by adding ad-hoc assumptions. Example: Newton's mechanics was "falsified" by Mercury's perihelion precession until we added the Lorentz force and gravity's retardation. Were the 200 years of Newtonian physics "unscientific"?

2. **Inductive underdetermination**: No finite set of data uniquely determines a theory. Infinitely many theories fit the same data. How do we choose? Falsifiability says: whichever is more falsifiable. But this is circular (we prefer falsifiable theories because they are falsifiable?).

3. **String theory admits some falsification**: If string theory predicts the Standard Model and gravity, and the SM predictions match experiments, then string theory has *passed* experimental tests (indirectly). It is not falsifiable only because it's hard to test, not because it's intrinsically untestable.

Dawid argues: **Falsifiability is necessary but not sufficient**. Physics needs additional criteria.

### Criterion 1: Structural Constraints Arguments

**Definition**: A theory is supported if it embodies elegant mathematical structures that constrain possible theories. The stronger the constraints, the more the theory is supported (even without empirical data).

**Example 1: General Relativity**

Einstein derived GR from **structure** alone (equivalence principle, covariance, energy conservation). No empirical data entered the derivation. Yet GR is one of the most successful theories ever. Why? Because the structural constraint (covariance requirement + equivalence principle) is so restrictive that almost no other theory fits.

If GR had emerged from fitting data (Mercury precession, light bending), we would be less confident it captures deep truth. Instead, GR emerged from **logical necessity** (Einstein's principles). This made it powerful.

**Example 2: String Theory**

String theory imposes constraints:
- **Consistency with quantum mechanics**: Preserves unitarity (no negative probabilities, no acausal information transfer)
- **Consistency with gravity**: String theory is the *only* known quantum theory that includes gravity without divergences (no infinities in scattering amplitudes)
- **Lorentz invariance**: Requires extra dimensions (10 or 11)
- **Supersymmetry**: Needed for consistency (cancellation of bosonic and fermionic loops)

Proponents argue: these are not free assumptions; they emerge from logical requirements. A theory *must* include gravity, *must* preserve unitarity, *must* be Lorentz-invariant. String theory is the unique solution (or one of very few). This is a powerful structural argument.

**Ellis-Silk counter**: "But infinitely many theories are consistent with quantum mechanics and gravity if you allow enough parameters." Yes, but they don't all have the *same* mathematical structure. String theory's structure is very special. It's not arbitrary; it's forced by consistency.

### Criterion 2: No-Alternatives Arguments

**Definition**: If a theory is the only plausible solution to a major open problem, it gains credibility *even without empirical confirmation*.

**Example 1: String Theory**

Before string theory (1960s–1980s), there was no quantum theory of gravity. This was the biggest unsolved problem in physics. String theory emerged as a solution (even if incomplete). The argument: "We need a quantum gravity theory. String theory is the only candidate. Therefore, it deserves research funding and credibility, even if not yet empirically verified."

**Counter**: But by 2014, alternatives existed (loop quantum gravity, asymptotically safe gravity, causal sets). So the "no-alternatives" argument weakened.

**Example 2: Dark Energy**

Before 1998, nobody knew what the universe's acceleration was due to. Cosmological constant was the simplest option, but theoretically unmotivated (why vacuum energy?). By 2014, we had:
- Cosmological constant (simplest, unphysical)
- Quintessence (scalar field, natural, but many variants)
- Modified gravity (challenges GR, requires new degrees of freedom)
- Emergent gravity (from entanglement or holography, speculative)

No single alternative dominates. So "no-alternatives" argument is weak.

### Criterion 3: Expected Explanatory Connections

**Definition**: A theory gains credibility if it promises to explain currently mysterious phenomena, even if those explanations are not yet formulated.

**Example 1: Unification**

String theory promises:
- **Unification of forces**: Electromagnetism, weak, strong, gravity all emerge from one structure (string vibrations)
- **Explanation of parameters**: Why is the electron mass 1/1836 of the proton mass? String theory (allegedly) derives this from the geometry of extra dimensions. (In practice, the calculation hasn't been done successfully, but the promise matters.)
- **Explanation of CP violation, neutrino masses, dark matter**: All should emerge from extra dimensions and compactification.

The argument: "String theory promises to explain all remaining mysteries. That's a good sign, even if the explanations aren't ready."

**Counter (Ellis-Silk)**: "Promise without delivery is not confirmation. Many false theories (astrology, phrenology) also promised to explain everything."

**Dawid response**: "True, but the *structure* of the promise matters. String theory derives its promises from mathematical structure, not empirical fitting. If the structure is sound, the promises are credible."

### Synthesis: A Bayesian Framework for Non-Empirical Confirmation

Dawid proposes that these three arguments can be formalized in a Bayesian framework:

$$P(\text{theory} | \text{structural argument 1}, \text{no-alternatives argument}, \text{explanatory promise}) \propto P(\text{arguments} | \text{theory}) \times P(\text{theory})$$

Each argument acts like an observation $D_i$ in Bayes' theorem:

$$P(T | D_1, D_2, D_3) = \frac{P(D_1 | T) P(D_2 | T) P(D_3 | T) P(T)}{P(D_1, D_2, D_3)}$$

If we have:
- **$D_1$**: Structural elegance (likelihood $P(D_1 | T)$ high if theory is elegant)
- **$D_2$**: No alternatives (likelihood high if T is unique solution to major problem)
- **$D_3$**: Promises explanatory power (likelihood high if promises are deep and natural)

Then combined posterior $P(T | \text{all arguments})$ can be substantial, *even without $D_4$ = empirical data*.

**Caveat**: This is not a proof of truth. It's an argument that non-empirical confirmation can be rational (in a Bayesian sense).

## Specific Defenses

### String Theory is Not a Landscape Escape Hatch

Dawid distinguishes:

**String theory proper**: A quantum theory of gravity with specific mathematical structure (strings, extra dimensions, supersymmetry). Falsifiable if: (1) extra dimensions not found, (2) supersymmetry not found at predicted scales, (3) specific predictions on Standard Model parameters fail.

**String landscape**: The claim that string theory admits 10^500 vacua, each with different physical laws. *This* is not falsifiable. But it's not string theory; it's a *consequence* of string theory if taken seriously (eternal inflation). Dawid does not defend the landscape as scientific.

Rather, Dawid defends *string theory* as a consistent quantum gravity framework that makes testable predictions (even if some are at inaccessible energies).

### Inflation is Falsifiable in Principle

Inflation predicts:
- Flat spatial geometry (test: WMAP, Planck — confirmed to 0.5%)
- Scale-invariant primordial spectrum (test: galaxy surveys — confirmed to 5%)
- Gravitational wave floor (test: LIGO in progress — targets next 10 years)

These are falsifiable predictions. *Specific* inflationary models (e.g., $V(\phi) = \lambda \phi^4$) can be ruled out if gravitational waves are below the predicted floor.

The fact that many inflation models exist is not a problem; it means there are many testable options. As data improves, models will be ruled out one by one. This is *how science works*, not a sign of non-falsifiability.

Dawid: The Ellis-Silk critique conflates "multiple theories in a class" with "one unfalsifiable super-theory." Inflation is the former.

## Key Results

1. **Falsifiability is necessary but not sufficient** for science. Popper's criterion is too restrictive.

2. **Non-empirical confirmation exists and is rational**: Structural elegance, absence of alternatives, and expected explanatory connections can provide genuine support for theories (in a Bayesian sense).

3. **String theory, inflation, and other non-empirical theories are not automatically unscientific**. They can satisfy falsifiability in principle (even if empirically inaccessible now) and are supported by non-empirical arguments.

4. **The landscape is a problem, but a manageable one**: The 10^500 vacua issue is not unique to string theory; any theory with continuous parameters has infinitely many possible configurations. Dawid argues we should select vacua by other means (anthropic reasoning, environmental selection, future understanding).

5. **Empirical data remain primary**: Non-empirical arguments *supplement* but do not replace empirical testing. A theory that passes empirical tests trumps a non-empirical competitor.

6. **Institutional problem remains unresolved**: Even if string theory is scientific (by Dawid's criteria), the question of funding allocation (billions for string theory, little for alternatives) remains separate from the philosophical question of scientific status.

## Impact and Legacy

Dawid's defense of non-empirical confirmation sparked continued debate:

- **Supporters**: String theorists (Susskind, Vafa, Gross), some philosophers (Fraser, Smolin's recent shift toward structure-based arguments)
- **Critics**: Ellis, Steinhardt, empiricists, particle physicists frustrated by lack of LHC discoveries
- **Institutional outcome**: Slow shift. By 2020, funding agencies began demanding "testable predictions" alongside theoretical elegance. Neither camp won completely.

The paper demonstrated that the demarcation problem (what is science?) is still open. It is not settled by Popper, nor by the Ellis-Silk critique. This remains one of the most important questions in philosophy of science.
