# Direct Contact Among Galactic Civilizations by the Transport of Organisms: A Scholarly Analysis

**Author:** Carl Sagan
**Year:** 1963
**Journal:** *Planetary and Space Science*, Vol. 11, No. 5, pp. 485-498

---

## Abstract

In this 1963 paper, Sagan undertook one of the first rigorous quantitative analyses of the probability of direct physical contact between spacefaring civilizations in the Milky Way galaxy. Building on the framework that Frank Drake had begun to formalize with his famous equation (1961), Sagan estimated the number of technologically advanced civilizations in the galaxy, the mean distance between them, and the expected frequency of interstellar visitation under various assumptions about the motivations and capabilities of such civilizations. He concluded that if even a modest fraction of civilizations developed interstellar travel, the expected number of visits to any given star system (including our own) could be non-negligible over geological time -- perhaps once every few thousand to few million years. The paper is notable for its combination of careful statistical reasoning with bold extrapolation, and for its influence on the subsequent development of SETI and the broader scientific discussion of extraterrestrial intelligence.

---

## Historical Context

### The Birth of SETI

The early 1960s witnessed the birth of the scientific search for extraterrestrial intelligence (SETI) as a legitimate research endeavor. Two landmark events framed the intellectual environment in which Sagan wrote this paper:

**Cocconi and Morrison (1959)** published their seminal letter in *Nature* arguing that microwave radio frequencies, particularly the 21-cm hydrogen line, represented a natural "watering hole" at which advanced civilizations might attempt to communicate. Their paper concluded with the famous statement: "The probability of success is difficult to estimate; but if we never search, the chance of success is zero."

**Frank Drake's Project Ozma (1960)** represented the first systematic observational search for extraterrestrial signals, targeting the nearby Sun-like stars Tau Ceti and Epsilon Eridani with the 85-foot telescope at Green Bank, West Virginia. Though the search detected no artificial signals, it demonstrated that such searches were technically feasible and scientifically respectable.

**The Green Bank Conference (1961)** brought together a small group of scientists -- including Drake, Sagan, Philip Morrison, Giuseppe Cocconi, Su-Shu Huang, and others -- to discuss the prospects for detecting extraterrestrial intelligence. It was at this meeting that Drake formulated his famous equation, N = R* x f_p x n_e x f_l x f_i x f_c x L, as an agenda for organizing the discussion. The equation estimates the number of communicative civilizations in the galaxy as a product of astronomical, biological, and sociological factors.

### Sagan's Distinctive Contribution

While Drake and Morrison had focused primarily on electromagnetic communication (radio SETI), Sagan's paper addressed a qualitatively different question: physical transport. Could civilizations actually travel between stars, and if so, how often might such visits occur? This was a more audacious question, requiring extrapolation not just about the existence of intelligence but about its technological capabilities and motivations over timescales of millions to billions of years.

Sagan was at this point a young researcher at Harvard University and the Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory (having moved from the University of California, Berkeley, where he was a Miller Fellow from 1960-1962), building his reputation in planetary science while simultaneously developing a serious interest in the question of extraterrestrial life. His willingness to address speculative but quantifiable questions with scientific rigor was already becoming a defining characteristic.

## Key Arguments and Evidence

### Estimating the Number of Civilizations

Sagan worked through a version of what would become known as the Drake Equation, estimating each factor with the best available astronomical and biological knowledge of the early 1960s:

**Rate of star formation**: The Milky Way contains roughly 10^11 stars, with a formation rate of several per year. Of these, a significant fraction are long-lived enough (spectral types F, G, K) to permit the development of complex life.

**Fraction with planets**: In 1963, there was no direct evidence for extrasolar planets. Sagan relied on theoretical arguments about stellar formation (the nebular hypothesis) and the observation that the Sun's angular momentum budget suggested planetary formation was common. He estimated that a substantial fraction of suitable stars possessed planetary systems.

**Habitable planets per system**: Sagan considered the range of orbital distances at which liquid water could persist on a planetary surface (what is now called the habitable zone), the requirement for suitable planetary mass, and the probability of adequate atmospheric retention. He estimated roughly one potentially habitable planet per system on average, though with large uncertainty.

**Origin of life**: Sagan drew on the Miller-Urey experiments (1953) and subsequent prebiotic chemistry work to argue that the origin of life was likely on planets with appropriate conditions. He treated this as having moderate-to-high probability, though acknowledging the enormous uncertainty.

**Development of intelligence**: This was and remains the most uncertain factor. Sagan argued from Earth's example that intelligence could develop through natural selection on geologically long timescales, but acknowledged that Earth provided only a single data point.

**Longevity of technological civilizations (L)**: Sagan identified this as the critical unknown. If civilizations typically destroy themselves shortly after developing advanced technology (through nuclear war being the obvious concern in 1963), then N would be small. If they typically survive for millions or billions of years, N could be very large. Sagan explored a range of values for L.

### The Contact Probability Calculation

The paper's central quantitative contribution was to estimate the probability of physical contact between civilizations, given an assumed value of N. The key steps:

1. **Mean separation**: Given N civilizations uniformly distributed in the galactic disk, the mean separation d scales as d ~ (V_disk / N)^(1/3), where V_disk is the volume of the galactic habitable zone. For N = 10^6 (a moderately optimistic estimate), the mean separation is roughly 100-300 light-years.

2. **Interstellar travel time**: Sagan considered various propulsion technologies and concluded that velocities of 0.01c to 0.1c (1-10% of the speed of light) were plausible for advanced civilizations, based on extrapolation of nuclear pulse propulsion concepts (the Orion project was then under study). At 0.01c, crossing 200 light-years requires 20,000 years -- long by human standards but short compared to galactic timescales.

3. **Exploration motivation**: Sagan argued that even without specific knowledge of target civilizations, the scientific motivation to explore neighboring star systems would be compelling for any species with the requisite technology. He estimated the rate of exploratory missions a civilization might launch per unit time.

4. **Cumulative visitation probability**: Integrating over the lifetime of civilizations and the volume of space accessible to them, Sagan estimated the expected number of times a given star system would be visited over its lifetime. His calculations suggested that for optimistic values of N and L, every star system in the galaxy might be visited at least once every 10^4 to 10^7 years.

### The Implications for Earth

Sagan drew the provocative but carefully qualified conclusion that if the galaxy contained a substantial number of long-lived technological civilizations, it was not implausible that Earth had been visited at some point during its 4.5-billion-year history -- perhaps multiple times. He was careful to distinguish this probabilistic statement from any claim about specific alleged visitations (he was firmly skeptical of UFO claims and would remain so throughout his career). The argument was purely statistical: given reasonable assumptions about galactic demographics and the feasibility of interstellar travel, the prior probability of at least one visit was non-trivial.

## Methodology and Data

### Statistical Framework

Sagan's methodology was essentially Bayesian in spirit, though he did not use that terminology. He established prior distributions (with acknowledged large uncertainties) for each factor in the contact probability calculation, then propagated these through to a posterior estimate. The key methodological contribution was demonstrating that even with enormous uncertainties in individual factors, the product could be bounded in informative ways.

### The Role of Longevity

Sagan's analysis revealed that the single most important factor determining the probability of contact was the longevity L of technological civilizations. If L is measured in centuries, the galaxy is essentially empty of contemporaneous civilizations and contact is vanishingly improbable. If L is measured in millions of years, the galaxy could be teeming with civilizations and contact becomes likely. This sensitivity to L was a key insight that shaped all subsequent discussion of SETI.

### Technological Extrapolation

Sagan's treatment of propulsion technology was necessarily speculative but grounded in the physics of the era. He considered chemical rockets (inadequate for interstellar travel), nuclear fission and fusion propulsion (potentially adequate for speeds of a few percent of c), and relativistic vehicles (theoretically possible but requiring energy budgets far beyond mid-20th-century capabilities). His conclusion that interstellar travel was physically possible but enormously expensive in energy and time has held up well.

## Physical Interpretation

### The Fermi Paradox Connection

Sagan's analysis is intimately connected to what became known as the Fermi Paradox (though Fermi's original lunchtime question dates to 1950). If the galaxy is old enough and large enough that civilizations should have had time to spread throughout it, why do we see no evidence of their presence? Sagan's paper, by quantifying the expected visitation rate, sharpened this question. His calculations showed that under optimistic assumptions, the absence of obvious evidence for visitation required explanation. Several possible explanations were considered: civilizations may not survive long (small L), interstellar travel may be impractical despite being physically possible, or evidence of past visitation may exist but be unrecognized.

### The Role of Self-Replicating Probes

While Sagan did not explicitly discuss self-replicating probes (von Neumann probes) -- that analysis would come later, notably from Tipler (1980) and Bracewell (1960) -- his framework implicitly raised the possibility. If a civilization could build autonomous exploration vehicles that replicated at each destination, the effective exploration rate would grow exponentially, and complete galactic colonization could occur on timescales of 10^6 to 10^8 years -- far less than the age of the galaxy. This line of reasoning has become one of the strongest formulations of the Fermi Paradox.

### Directed Panspermia

Sagan's paper also touched on the concept that civilizations might deliberately seed other worlds with life -- an idea he would develop further with Shklovskii in their 1966 book *Intelligent Life in the Universe*. The "transport of organisms" in the paper's title referred not only to the organisms comprising the traveling civilization but potentially to biological payloads intended for colonization or seeding purposes. This anticipates the concept of directed panspermia later elaborated by Crick and Orgel (1973).

## Impact and Legacy

### Intellectual Impact on SETI

Sagan's paper was influential in broadening the SETI discussion beyond pure radio searches. While the mainstream SETI community ultimately focused on electromagnetic searches (for practical reasons -- they are far cheaper than interstellar travel), the physical contact scenario that Sagan analyzed provided an important complementary perspective and helped define the full scope of the problem.

### The Drake Equation Tradition

Sagan's quantitative approach -- carefully estimating each factor while acknowledging huge uncertainties -- became the template for all subsequent Drake Equation analyses. His identification of L as the dominant uncertainty has been confirmed by every subsequent treatment of the problem, from Shklovskii and Sagan (1966) through the modern statistical analyses of Sandberg, Drexler, and Ord (2018).

### Influence on Science Fiction and Public Discourse

The paper contributed to the scientific legitimization of concepts that had previously been confined to science fiction. Sagan's willingness to treat interstellar contact as a quantifiable scientific question rather than dismissing it as fantasy helped establish the intellectual framework within which organizations like the SETI Institute would later operate.

### Sagan's Own Career

This paper was an early expression of what would become one of Sagan's signature themes: the cosmic perspective on humanity's place in the universe. The arguments developed here would be refined and extended in *Intelligent Life in the Universe* (1966, with Shklovskii), *The Cosmic Connection* (1973), *Contact* (1985, his novel), and throughout his career in public science communication.

## Connections to Modern Science

### Exoplanet Demographics

The most dramatic change in the empirical foundation of Sagan's analysis has been the revolution in exoplanet detection. Where Sagan was forced to estimate the fraction of stars with planets from theoretical arguments alone, the Kepler mission (2009-2018) and subsequent surveys have established that essentially every star has planets, that rocky planets in habitable zones are common (~10-20% of Sun-like stars), and that the galaxy contains billions of potentially habitable worlds. These findings have generally pushed Sagan's estimates in the optimistic direction for the astronomical factors in the Drake Equation.

### The Great Silence and Modern Fermi Paradox Research

Sagan's framework continues to inform the modern Fermi Paradox literature. Hart (1975), Tipler (1980), and more recently Sandberg et al. (2018) have refined the calculations of galactic colonization timescales. The "dissolving" of the Fermi Paradox through proper treatment of parameter uncertainties (Sandberg et al. showed that the probability of being alone in the observable universe is substantial even with generous parameter estimates) represents a direct evolution of the quantitative approach Sagan pioneered.

### Breakthrough Starshot and Interstellar Probes

The Breakthrough Starshot initiative (announced 2016), which aims to send gram-scale probes to Alpha Centauri at 0.2c using laser-driven light sails, represents the first serious engineering effort toward the kind of interstellar exploration Sagan discussed. While the technology differs from what Sagan envisioned, the fundamental concept -- using directed energy or nuclear propulsion to send probes to nearby stars on multi-decade timescales -- is precisely what his paper analyzed.

### Astrobiology as a Discipline

The broader scientific question that motivated Sagan's paper -- is life common in the universe, and if so, does it develop intelligence? -- has matured into the discipline of astrobiology. NASA's establishment of the Astrobiology Institute (1998) and the incorporation of biosignature detection into major mission architectures (JWST, the Habitable Worlds Observatory concept) represent the institutional embodiment of questions that Sagan was among the first to treat with scientific rigor.

### The Technosignature Revival

After decades of focusing on biosignatures, NASA and the broader astronomical community have recently begun to take technosignatures (observational evidence of technology) more seriously as a search target. This represents a partial return to the questions Sagan raised: if civilizations exist and develop technology, what observable evidence might they produce? The search for megastructures, atmospheric pollution, laser emission, and other technosignatures descends directly from the tradition Sagan helped establish.
