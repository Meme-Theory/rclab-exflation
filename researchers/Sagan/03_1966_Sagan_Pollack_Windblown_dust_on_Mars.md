# Windblown Dust on Mars: A Scholarly Analysis

**Authors:** Carl Sagan and James B. Pollack
**Year:** 1969
**Journal:** *Nature*, Vol. 223, pp. 791-794

---

## Abstract

Sagan and Pollack's 1966 paper on Martian dust transport addressed one of the longstanding puzzles of Mars observation: the seasonal and secular changes in surface albedo (brightness) patterns that had been documented by telescopic observers for over a century. The authors proposed that these changes were caused by windblown dust redistributed across the Martian surface by atmospheric circulation patterns, rather than by the growth and recession of vegetation (the hypothesis that had been surprisingly persistent in some quarters) or by purely chemical/frost processes. Through calculations of the threshold wind speeds required to saltate and suspend dust particles in the thin Martian atmosphere, and comparison with estimated wind speeds in Martian atmospheric circulation models, they demonstrated that aeolian (wind-driven) dust transport was plausible and could account for the observed albedo variations. This paper helped establish the paradigm that Mars is an aeolian-dominated world, a conclusion dramatically confirmed by subsequent spacecraft observations, and represented an early triumph of the Sagan-Pollack collaboration that would produce seminal work across planetary science for two decades.

---

## Historical Context

### The Mystery of Martian Darkening

Since the 19th century, telescopic observers had noted that certain regions of Mars underwent seasonal changes in brightness. Dark areas appeared to darken further and expand during Martian spring and summer, while bright areas shifted in extent and intensity. These changes were roughly correlated with the recession of the seasonal polar caps.

The most persistent interpretation, championed by astronomers from Lowell through the mid-20th century, was that the darkening represented the growth of vegetation -- plant life responding to the seasonal availability of water from the melting polar caps. While this "biological" interpretation had been increasingly questioned on physical grounds (the Martian atmosphere was known to be thin and CO2-dominated), it retained surprising currency even into the 1960s. The Sinton bands -- infrared absorption features initially attributed to organic molecules -- had lent it a superficial spectroscopic support before being reinterpreted as terrestrial contamination artifacts.

Alternative non-biological explanations included chemical darkening of surface minerals by moisture, frost deposition and sublimation, and volcanic activity. None had been developed quantitatively to the point of explaining the full pattern of observed changes.

### The Aeolian Hypothesis

The idea that wind-driven dust might explain Martian surface changes was not entirely new. Several researchers had suggested it qualitatively. However, a significant objection existed: Mars's atmosphere was known to be extremely thin (surface pressure ~6-10 mbar, roughly 1% of Earth's), and it was unclear whether winds in such a tenuous atmosphere could exert sufficient force on surface particles to initiate and sustain dust transport. Sagan and Pollack's contribution was to quantify this question.

### The Sagan-Pollack Partnership

James B. Pollack would become one of Sagan's most important scientific collaborators. A graduate student at Harvard when this work began, Pollack would go on to join NASA Ames Research Center and co-author dozens of influential papers with Sagan on topics ranging from Mars to Venus to Titan to nuclear winter. Their partnership combined Sagan's broad vision and physical intuition with Pollack's meticulous computational skills. This 1966 paper was one of their earliest joint publications.

## Key Arguments and Evidence

### The Observational Constraints

Sagan and Pollack began by establishing the observational phenomena requiring explanation:

1. **Seasonal wave of darkening**: A progressive darkening that appears to propagate from the receding polar cap toward the equator during spring and summer in each hemisphere. The wave advances at roughly 35 km/day.

2. **Opposition-to-opposition variations**: The overall pattern of bright and dark markings changes on timescales of years, beyond the regular seasonal cycle. Some features that were prominent in one apparition may be muted or absent in the next.

3. **Recovery after global dust storms**: Major dust storms (observed telescopically as "yellow clouds") could temporarily obliterate surface features, which then re-emerged over weeks to months as the dust settled. The re-emerged pattern sometimes differed from the pre-storm pattern.

4. **Albedo range**: The contrast between the brightest and darkest regions of Mars was modest -- the dark regions had albedos of roughly 0.1-0.15, while the bright regions were roughly 0.25-0.30. This modest contrast was consistent with varying thicknesses of bright dust overlying a darker substrate.

### The Dust Transport Calculation

The central quantitative argument proceeded in several steps:

**Threshold friction velocity**: Sagan and Pollack calculated the minimum wind shear stress required to initiate particle motion (saltation) on the Martian surface. This depends on particle size, particle density, atmospheric density, and gravitational acceleration. For Mars, the lower gravity (0.38g) reduces the threshold, but the much lower atmospheric density (~0.01 that of Earth) dramatically increases it. The net effect is that threshold wind speeds on Mars are much higher than on Earth -- roughly 50-100 m/s for optimally-sized particles (~100 micrometers), depending on assumptions about surface roughness and particle cohesion.

**Atmospheric wind speeds**: Using the best available estimates of Martian atmospheric circulation (derived from theoretical models of solar-driven flow on a rotating planet with thin atmosphere), they argued that near-surface wind speeds of 50-100 m/s were achievable, particularly during periods of maximum thermal contrast (near perihelion, when Mars is closest to the Sun and receives maximum solar heating). Topographic channeling could further enhance local winds.

**Suspension and transport**: Once saltation lifts particles from the surface, smaller particles can be suspended by atmospheric turbulence and transported over long distances. Sagan and Pollack estimated the suspension times and transport distances, showing that global-scale redistribution was plausible during major dust-lifting events.

**Bright dust / dark substrate model**: The authors proposed a two-component model: Mars has a dark basaltic substrate (similar in composition to terrestrial basalts) overlain by a variable thickness of bright, fine-grained dust (iron oxide-rich material giving Mars its characteristic red color). The seasonal and secular albedo changes result from wind-driven redistribution of this bright dust layer, alternately covering and uncovering the dark substrate.

### The Seasonal Mechanism

Sagan and Pollack proposed that the "wave of darkening" could be explained by a seasonal shift in atmospheric circulation patterns. As the polar cap recedes, the meridional temperature gradient changes, driving shifts in wind patterns that preferentially remove bright dust from certain regions (causing darkening) and deposit it in others (causing brightening). The apparent propagation speed of the darkening wave was consistent with the expected rate of change in atmospheric circulation patterns, not with any biological growth rate.

## Methodology and Data

### Particle Physics on Mars

The core methodology was an application of aeolian geomorphology -- the physics of wind-particle interaction -- to Martian conditions. This required:

1. **Bagnold's threshold curve**: Ralph Bagnold's fundamental work on terrestrial sand transport (1941) provided the theoretical framework. Sagan and Pollack adapted Bagnold's threshold curve to Martian conditions by substituting the appropriate values for atmospheric density, gravity, and particle properties.

2. **Particle size distribution**: The optimum particle size for saltation (the size that minimizes the threshold friction velocity) depends on the balance between gravitational and aerodynamic forces. On Mars, this optimum is somewhat larger than on Earth due to the lower atmospheric density.

3. **Atmospheric density profile**: The Martian atmospheric density at the surface, estimated from spectroscopic observations and occultation measurements, was a critical input. Sagan and Pollack used a surface pressure of roughly 10 mbar (later revised downward to ~6 mbar by Mariner observations, which actually makes dust lifting harder).

### Wind Speed Estimates

The atmospheric circulation estimates were necessarily crude by modern standards, derived from simple theoretical models rather than GCM simulations (which did not yet exist for Mars). Sagan and Pollack relied on thermal wind balance calculations and analogy with terrestrial desert meteorology. Their wind speed estimates proved to be roughly correct in magnitude, though the detailed circulation patterns are now known to be more complex than the simple models suggested.

### Comparison with Terrestrial Analogs

The authors drew on terrestrial desert geomorphology, particularly observations of dust storms in the Sahara and Arabian deserts, to validate their methodology. They noted that terrestrial dust storms produce albedo changes qualitatively similar to those observed on Mars, and that the physics of dust lifting and transport is well-understood on Earth.

## Physical Interpretation

### Mars as an Aeolian World

The central physical conclusion -- that Mars is a world where wind-driven processes dominate surface modification in the current epoch -- has been thoroughly confirmed by subsequent observations. The absence of liquid water (the dominant surface-modifying agent on Earth) means that aeolian processes operate essentially unopposed on Mars, producing the dune fields, erosional features, and dust deposits that spacecraft have documented extensively.

### The Dust Cycle

Sagan and Pollack's paper laid the groundwork for understanding the Martian dust cycle -- the continuous process of dust lifting, atmospheric transport, deposition, and re-lifting that operates on seasonal and longer timescales. This cycle is now known to be one of the most important processes governing the Martian climate, since atmospheric dust is a major absorber of solar radiation and its distribution strongly influences atmospheric heating rates and circulation patterns.

### Global Dust Storms

The paper's predictions about dust transport were spectacularly confirmed when Mariner 9 arrived at Mars in November 1971 to find the entire planet engulfed in one of the most intense global dust storms ever observed. As the storm cleared over the following months, the spacecraft imaged a surface that was being revealed through the settling dust -- precisely the process Sagan and Pollack had described. The timing was fortunate and gave their aeolian model immediate observational support.

## Impact and Legacy

### Paradigm Shift

The Sagan-Pollack dust model effectively ended the vegetation hypothesis as a serious scientific proposal. By providing a quantitative physical mechanism that could explain all the observed albedo changes without invoking biology, they removed the principal observational argument that had kept the vegetation hypothesis alive. This was scientifically important not because anyone seriously believed in Martian vegetation by the mid-1960s, but because the lack of a clear alternative explanation had left the question technically open.

### Foundation for Mars Atmospheric Science

The paper was foundational for the subfield of Mars atmospheric science and aeolian geomorphology. All subsequent work on Martian dust transport, dust storm dynamics, and the dust-climate feedback builds on the framework Sagan and Pollack established. Key successors include:

- The Mariner 9 observations of the 1971 global dust storm
- Viking lander meteorological measurements of surface winds and dust devils
- Mars Pathfinder, Spirit, and Opportunity observations of aeolian features
- Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter monitoring of seasonal and interannual albedo changes
- The extensive dune fields documented by HiRISE that confirm ongoing aeolian activity

### The Sagan-Pollack Collaboration

This paper launched a collaboration that would produce many of the most important results in planetary science over the next two decades. Sagan and Pollack would go on to work together on Venus atmospheric models, Titan haze calculations, the nuclear winter hypothesis, and numerous other topics. Pollack's early death in 1994 was a significant loss to the field.

### Aeolian Planetary Science

The methodology Sagan and Pollack developed for studying wind-particle interactions on other planets has been applied to essentially every solid-surfaced body with an atmosphere: Venus (where aeolian features have been identified by Magellan radar), Titan (where Cassini discovered extensive dune fields of organic sand), Pluto (where New Horizons found evidence of aeolian transport), and even Triton. The framework of adapting Bagnold's threshold curve to non-terrestrial conditions has become a standard tool in planetary geomorphology.

## Connections to Modern Science

### Mars Climate Science

Modern Mars GCMs (such as those developed at LMD, NASA Ames, and the Open University) include detailed dust transport modules that are direct descendants of the Sagan-Pollack framework. These models track the lifting, transport, and deposition of dust and are essential for understanding Mars's climate variability, including the triggering mechanisms for global dust storms (which remain incompletely understood despite decades of study).

### Mars Exploration Rovers and Landers

Every Mars surface mission has had to contend with the aeolian environment Sagan and Pollack described. The dust that accumulates on solar panels was a constant concern for the Spirit and Opportunity rovers (and conversely, occasional "cleaning events" by dust devils extended their missions). The Curiosity and Perseverance rovers have documented active aeolian transport in situ. InSight's seismometer detected the pressure fluctuations from passing dust devils and vortices.

### Dust and Climate Feedback

The dust-climate feedback that Sagan and Pollack's work implies -- dust affects atmospheric heating, which affects winds, which affect dust lifting -- is now recognized as one of the key nonlinear feedbacks in the Martian climate system. Understanding this feedback is essential for interpreting the geological record of Mars, including evidence for past climate changes recorded in layered deposits at the poles and in equatorial sedimentary basins.

### Exoplanet Atmospheres

The general question of aeolian dust transport in non-terrestrial atmospheres is relevant to the characterization of rocky exoplanets. Dusty atmospheres can significantly affect planetary spectra, and understanding the conditions under which dust is lofted into and maintained in an atmosphere draws on the same physics that Sagan and Pollack applied to Mars, adapted to a much wider range of atmospheric densities and compositions.
