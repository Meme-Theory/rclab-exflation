## Historical Context

### The Supernova

On February 23, 1987, a Type II supernova was observed in the Large Magellanic
Cloud (LMC), a satellite galaxy of the Milky Way at a distance of approximately
51.4 kiloparsecs (~168,000 light-years). The progenitor star was identified as
Sanduleak -69 202, a blue supergiant of spectral type B3I with an estimated
main-sequence mass of ~20 solar masses.

SN 1987A was the closest observed supernova since Kepler's supernova of 1604 and
the first naked-eye supernova since that event. It reached a peak visual
magnitude of about +3 in May 1987. The optical discovery was made by Ian Shelton
and Oscar Duhalde at Las Campanas Observatory, Chile, on February 24, 1987 --
but the neutrino signal had already been recorded approximately three hours
before the optical brightening, providing the first observational confirmation
that the core collapse precedes the shock breakout.

### The State of Neutrino Detection in 1987

By 1987, three underground detectors were operational that could detect
supernova neutrinos:

1. **Kamiokande-II** (Kamioka, Japan): 2,140 tonnes of water, 948 PMTs.
   Originally built to search for proton decay (Kamioka Nucleon Decay
   Experiment). Upgraded to "II" with improved PMTs and water purification to
   lower the energy threshold to ~7.5 MeV.

2. **IMB** (Irvine-Michigan-Brookhaven, Fairport, Ohio): 6,800 tonnes of water,
   2,048 PMTs. Also a proton decay experiment. Energy threshold ~20 MeV.

3. **Baksan** (Baksan Neutrino Observatory, Caucasus, USSR): 200 tonnes of
   liquid scintillator. The Baksan Underground Scintillation Telescope (BUST).

None of these detectors was designed for supernova neutrino detection -- they
were all proton decay experiments that happened to be sensitive to the
$\bar{\nu}_e$ flux from a galactic (or near-galactic) supernova.

### Core-Collapse Supernova Theory

The theoretical framework for neutrino emission from core-collapse supernovae
had been developed over decades by Baade and Zwicky (1934), Colgate and White
(1966), Arnett (1966, 1977), and others. The key predictions were:

1. When the iron core of a massive star ($M \gtrsim 8 M_\odot$) exceeds the
   Chandrasekhar mass (~1.4 $M_\odot$), it collapses in ~100 ms.

2. The gravitational binding energy released is approximately:
   $$E_{\text{bind}} \approx \frac{3 G M_{\text{NS}}^2}{5 R_{\text{NS}}} \approx 3 \times 10^{53} \text{ erg}$$
   for a neutron star of mass $M_{\text{NS}} \approx 1.4 \, M_\odot$ and radius
   $R_{\text{NS}} \approx 10$ km.

3. Approximately **99% of this energy** is carried away by neutrinos and
   antineutrinos of all flavors, emitted over a timescale of ~10 seconds.
   Only ~1% goes into the kinetic energy of the explosion, and ~0.01% into
   photons.

4. The neutrino luminosity is roughly equipartitioned among six species
   ($\nu_e$, $\bar{\nu}_e$, $\nu_\mu$, $\bar{\nu}_\mu$, $\nu_\tau$,
   $\bar{\nu}_\tau$), with mean energies:
   - $\langle E_{\nu_e} \rangle \approx 11$ MeV
   - $\langle E_{\bar{\nu}_e} \rangle \approx 16$ MeV
   - $\langle E_{\nu_x} \rangle \approx 25$ MeV (where $x = \mu, \tau$)

   The hierarchy arises because $\nu_e$ have the largest cross-section in
   neutron-rich matter (charged-current on neutrons) and decouple at the
   largest radius (lowest temperature).

## The Detection

### Kamiokande-II: 11 Events

The Kamiokande-II detector recorded **11 events** in a 13-second window beginning
at 07:35:35 UT on February 23, 1987. The events were detected via inverse beta
decay:

$$\bar{\nu}_e + p \to e^+ + n$$

The positron produces Cherenkov light, which is detected by the PMTs. The
threshold for this reaction is $E_{\bar{\nu}_e} > 1.8$ MeV, but the effective
detector threshold was ~7.5 MeV due to background.

The 11 events, with their properties:

| Event | Time (s) | Energy (MeV) | Angle (deg) |

| 1 | 0.000 | 20.0 +/- 2.9 | 18 +/- 18 |
| 2 | 0.107 | 13.5 +/- 3.2 | 40 +/- 27 |
| 3 | 0.303 | 7.5 +/- 2.0 | 108 +/- 32 |
| 4 | 0.324 | 9.2 +/- 2.7 | 70 +/- 30 |
| 5 | 0.507 | 12.8 +/- 2.9 | 135 +/- 23 |
| 6 | 1.541 | 6.3 +/- 1.7 | 68 +/- 77 |
| 7 | 1.728 | 35.4 +/- 8.0 | 32 +/- 16 |

| 10 | 10.433 | 13.0 +/- 2.6 | 45 +/- 24 |
| 11 | 12.439 | 8.9 +/- 1.9 | 36 +/- 32 |

Event 6 is below the normal analysis threshold and was sometimes excluded in
later analyses, leaving 10 or 11 events depending on the analysis.

### IMB: 8 Events

The IMB detector recorded **8 events** in a 6-second window, beginning at
07:35:41.37 UT (approximately 6 seconds after the Kamiokande-II trigger, though
absolute timing uncertainties of ~1 minute made direct synchronization
difficult).

| Event | Time (s) | Energy (MeV) |
|:------|:---------|:-------------|
| 1 | 0.000 | 38 +/- 7 |
| 2 | 0.412 | 37 +/- 7 |
| 3 | 0.650 | 28 +/- 6 |
| 4 | 1.141 | 39 +/- 7 |
| 5 | 1.562 | 36 +/- 9 |
| 6 | 2.684 | 36 +/- 6 |
| 7 | 5.010 | 19 +/- 5 |
| 8 | 5.582 | 22 +/- 5 |

The IMB energies are systematically higher because of the higher threshold
(~20 MeV), which biased the detected sample toward higher-energy events.

### Baksan: 5 Events

The Baksan Scintillation Telescope recorded **5 events** in a 10-second window
at approximately 07:36:11 UT (with ~2 s absolute timing uncertainty):

Events at energies of approximately 12, 18, 23, 17, and 20 MeV.

### Combined Significance

A total of **24 neutrino events** (11 + 8 + 5) were detected across three
detectors on three continents within a ~13-second window. The probability of
this being a background fluctuation is negligible. The background rate in
Kamiokande-II at the relevant energies was ~0.7 events per 13-second window;
the detection of 11 events constitutes a >10-sigma excess.

## Physics Results

### Confirmation of Core-Collapse Theory

The total energy in $\bar{\nu}_e$ detected at Kamiokande-II can be used to
estimate the total neutrino energy release. Given:

- Distance: $d = 51.4$ kpc $= 1.58 \times 10^{23}$ cm
- Detector mass: 2,140 tonnes $\to$ $N_p \approx 1.43 \times 10^{32}$ free protons
- Number of events: $N_{\text{det}} = 11$
- Mean cross-section at $\langle E \rangle \approx 15$ MeV:
  $\langle \sigma \rangle \approx 9.5 \times 10^{-44} \, \langle E_\nu \rangle^2 / m_e^2$ cm$^2$
  $\approx 2.5 \times 10^{-42}$ cm$^2$

The fluence at Earth:

$$\Phi = \frac{N_{\text{det}}}{N_p \cdot \langle \sigma \rangle} = \frac{11}{1.43 \times 10^{32} \times 2.5 \times 10^{-42}} \approx 3.1 \times 10^{10} \text{ cm}^{-2}$$

The total energy in $\bar{\nu}_e$:

$$E_{\bar{\nu}_e} = 4\pi d^2 \cdot \Phi \cdot \langle E_\nu \rangle \approx 4\pi (1.58 \times 10^{23})^2 \times 3.1 \times 10^{10} \times 2.4 \times 10^{-5} \text{ erg}$$

$$\approx 7 \times 10^{52} \text{ erg}$$

Assuming equipartition among 6 neutrino species:

$$E_{\text{total}} \approx 6 \times E_{\bar{\nu}_e} \approx 4 \times 10^{53} \text{ erg}$$

This is consistent with the theoretical prediction of $\sim 3 \times 10^{53}$
erg to within the large uncertainties, providing spectacular confirmation of the
core-collapse supernova model.

### Neutrino Mass Constraints from Time-of-Flight

For a neutrino with mass $m$ and energy $E$, the velocity is:

$$v = c \sqrt{1 - \frac{m^2 c^4}{E^2}} \approx c \left(1 - \frac{m^2 c^4}{2E^2}\right)$$

The time delay relative to a massless particle over distance $d$ is:

$$\Delta t = \frac{d}{c} \cdot \frac{m^2 c^4}{2E^2}$$

For two neutrinos with energies $E_1$ and $E_2$, the differential time delay is:

$$\Delta t_{12} = \frac{d \, m^2 c^4}{2c} \left(\frac{1}{E_1^2} - \frac{1}{E_2^2}\right)$$

From the Kamiokande-II data, the spread of arrival times (~13 s) combined with
the energy spread (~7--35 MeV) constrains:

$$m_{\nu_e} < 16 \text{ eV} \quad (90\% \text{ CL, Kamiokande-II alone})$$

Later combined analyses (using all three detectors and more sophisticated
statistical methods) tightened this to:

$$m_{\nu_e} \lesssim 5.7 \text{ eV} \quad (\text{Loredo and Lamb, 2002})$$

These are "kinematic" mass limits from the neutrino time-of-flight, independent
of whether neutrinos are Dirac or Majorana particles.

### Neutrino Lifetime Constraint

The fact that neutrinos arrived at Earth after traversing 168,000 light-years
implies:

$$\tau_\nu / m_\nu > 5.7 \times 10^{5} \text{ s/eV} \quad (90\% \text{ CL})$$

This rules out many exotic neutrino decay scenarios and establishes that
neutrinos are effectively stable on cosmological timescales for the mass range
of interest ($m_\nu \lesssim 1$ eV).

### Neutrino Speed Constraint

The near-simultaneous arrival of neutrinos and photons (within ~3 hours,
consistent with the expected delay between core collapse and shock breakout)
constrains:

$$|v_\nu - c| / c < 2 \times 10^{-9}$$

This is consistent with special relativity and provides a test of Lorentz
invariance in the neutrino sector over cosmological baselines.

### Neutrino Electric Charge Constraint

If neutrinos had a small electric charge $q_\nu$, the galactic magnetic field
would deflect them, causing an energy-dependent time delay. The absence of such
effects constrains:

$$|q_\nu| < 3 \times 10^{-17} \, e$$

### Neutrino Magnetic Moment Constraint

The neutrino signal duration and spectrum constrain anomalous energy loss
mechanisms in the proto-neutron star, including magnetic moment interactions:

$$\mu_\nu < (1 - 4) \times 10^{-12} \, \mu_B$$

where $\mu_B$ is the Bohr magneton.

## The Birth of Neutrino Astronomy

### Significance

SN 1987A marked the birth of **neutrino astronomy** -- the first detection of
neutrinos from an identified astrophysical source beyond the Sun. Key aspects
of this achievement:

1. **Multi-messenger astronomy**: The correlation of neutrino and optical signals
   from the same event established the multi-messenger paradigm that would later
   be extended to gravitational waves (LIGO/Virgo, 2015+) and the
   neutron star merger GW170817/GRB 170817A (2017).

2. **Core-collapse confirmation**: The neutrino signal directly probed the
   collapsing core -- a region completely opaque to photons. The ~10-second
   duration confirmed the Kelvin-Helmholtz cooling timescale of the
   proto-neutron star.

3. **Energy budget**: The total energy in neutrinos (~$3 \times 10^{53}$ erg)
   confirmed that neutrinos carry away 99% of the gravitational binding energy,
   as predicted.

4. **Detector serendipity**: None of the three detectors was built for supernova
   neutrino detection. The scientific payoff of proton decay experiments was
   realized in a completely unexpected direction.

### Subsequent Developments

The success of SN 1987A neutrino detection catalyzed the development of
dedicated supernova neutrino detection capabilities:

- **Super-Kamiokande** (1996--): 50,000 tonnes water Cherenkov, ~8,000 events
  expected for a supernova at 10 kpc.
- **IceCube** (2010--): 1 km$^3$ ice Cherenkov at South Pole. Would detect the
  collective rise in PMT rates from $O(10^6)$ interactions.
- **JUNO** (under construction): 20,000 tonnes liquid scintillator. ~5,000
  events at 10 kpc with excellent energy resolution.
- **Hyper-Kamiokande** (under construction): 260,000 tonnes water Cherenkov.
  ~50,000 events at 10 kpc.
- **SNEWS** (SuperNova Early Warning System): network connecting neutrino
  detectors worldwide to provide real-time alerts.

The galactic supernova rate is estimated at 1--3 per century. The next galactic
supernova will produce a neutrino signal orders of magnitude richer than
SN 1987A, enabling detailed tests of supernova physics, neutrino properties,
and potentially new physics.

## Nobel Prize Context

Masatoshi Koshiba received the 2002 Nobel Prize in Physics (shared with Raymond
Davis Jr. and Riccardo Giacconi) "for pioneering contributions to astrophysics,
in particular for the detection of cosmic neutrinos." Koshiba's contributions
included:

- Leading the Kamiokande experiment from a proton decay search to a neutrino
  observatory.
- The critical upgrade to Kamiokande-II that lowered the energy threshold,
  enabling the SN 1987A detection.
- Subsequent contributions to Super-Kamiokande that led to the discovery of
  atmospheric neutrino oscillations.

Davis received the prize for the Homestake solar neutrino experiment (1968--1998).
Giacconi received it for X-ray astronomy (unrelated to neutrinos).

### Mass Constraints and the Lowest Eigenvalues

The SN 1987A time-of-flight mass constraint ($m_\nu < 16$ eV from K-II, later
refined to $\lesssim 5.7$ eV) constrains the absolute scale of the lowest Dirac
eigenvalues. In the framework, the neutrino masses are:

$$m_{\nu_i} = y_\nu \cdot v \cdot f(\lambda_i(s_0))$$

where $y_\nu$ is the effective Yukawa coupling, $v = 246$ GeV is the Higgs

The constraint $m_\nu < 5.7$ eV requires that the lowest Dirac eigenvalues

$$f(\lambda_i(s_0)) < \frac{5.7 \text{ eV}}{y_\nu \cdot 246 \text{ GeV}} \sim 10^{-11} / y_\nu$$

eigenvalues of $D_K$ so much smaller than the rest? In the standard seesaw
