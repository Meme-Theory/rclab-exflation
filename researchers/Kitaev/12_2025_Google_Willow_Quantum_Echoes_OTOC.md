# Quantum Echoes and Verifiable Quantum Advantage on Willow

**Author(s):** Google Quantum AI Team (including research staff at Google, Caltech, and academic collaborators)
**Year:** 2025
**Journal:** Nature (publication October 2025)
**Context:** Willow superconducting quantum processor, 105 qubits

---

## Abstract

The Google Quantum AI team demonstrated the first experimental measurement of out-of-time-ordered correlators (OTOCs) on a scalable quantum processor and achieved verifiable quantum advantage for a task involving OTOC computation. The "Quantum Echoes" algorithm uses a time-reversal sequence to create a quantum echo effect: an initial perturbation is evolved forward, then the evolution is reversed, creating an echo that amplifies the scrambling signal.

The algorithm measures the OTOC F(t) = <[W(t), V(0)]^2> by:

1. Preparing an initial state
2. Applying a perturbation (single-qubit gate)
3. Evolving forward in time
4. Reversing the evolution
5. Measuring the echo amplitude

The Willow processor achieved a speedup of **13,000x** over classical computers for OTOC computation on systems with 15-28 qubits. The measurements also revealed clear signatures of information scrambling and chaos in the 2D qubit array.

---

## Historical Context

Since Maldacena, Shenker, and Stanford introduced the OTOC formalism in 2016, experimental measurements remained limited to small systems. By 2024-2025, several platforms had attempted OTOC measurements (trapped ions, nuclear spins, superconducting qubits), but none had demonstrated:

1. **Scalability** beyond ~10 qubits
2. **Verifiable quantum advantage** (provably impossible for classical simulation)
3. **Clear chaos signatures** in a realistic many-body system

Google's Willow breakthrough addressed all three. The key innovation was the **Quantum Echoes algorithm**, which reframes OTOC measurement as a time-reversal problem (closer to standard quantum error correction) rather than a four-point correlation function (which requires complex multi-body measurements).

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### Quantum Echoes Protocol

Traditional OTOC measurement requires measuring four operators at different times: **O_1(t_1) O_2(t_2) O_3(t_3) O_4(t_4)**, which is experimentally challenging.

The Quantum Echoes protocol instead uses time-reversal symmetry:

```
1. Prepare state |psi_0>
2. Apply unitary U(t) for time t
3. Apply perturbation W at time 0 (perturb initial state)
4. Apply U^dag(t) (reverse evolution for time t)
5. Measure overlap <psi_0|psi_final>
```

The overlap encodes the OTOC via:

```
<psi_0|psi_final> = 1 - F(t)/2  (approximately, in weak perturbation limit)
```

where F(t) is the OTOC. This is because the time-reversal amplifies the effect of the perturbation: if the forward-backward evolution were perfect, the overlap would return to 1; perturbations cause deviations.

### Measurement Protocol on Willow

The 105-qubit Willow processor used a 2D superconducting qubit array with:

- **Qubit count**: 105 (subset of 120-qubit array)
- **Connectivity**: Square lattice with nearest-neighbor couplings
- **Gate fidelities**: ~99.9% for single-qubit gates, ~99.2% for two-qubit gates
- **Coherence times**: T_1 ~ 100 microseconds, T_2 ~ 50 microseconds

The OTOC measurement involved:

```
Subsystem A (left half): perturbed by single-qubit X gate
Subsystem B (right half): probed by measurement
Timescale: t = 0 to 50 qubits depth (equivalent to ~20 ns in physical time, adjusted for SWAP)
```

The evolution U(t) was generated by time-evolving a random XZ Hamiltonian:

```
H = sum_i J_i Z_i Z_{i+1} + h_i X_i + ...
```

### Observed Scrambling Growth

The OTOC F(t) decayed from 1 (at t=0) to smaller values as information spread:

```
t=0: F(0) = 1 (no spreading)
t=few layers: F(t) ~ 0.8 (some spreading)
t=10-20 layers: F(t) ~ 0.2-0.3 (significant scrambling)
```

The decay timescale matched the light-cone spreading of information from subsystem A to subsystem B, confirming the butterfly effect in a real quantum system.

### Verifiable Quantum Advantage

The classical computation of OTOC on 15-28 qubits requires either:

1. **Exact tensor network contraction**: exponential in system size for 2D (O(exp(n)))
2. **Monte Carlo sampling**: requires many samples to converge, overall time >> 10^14 operations

Google's quantum processor computed the same task in microseconds, achieving speedup factors of 1000-13000x depending on system size.

Crucially, the results were **verifiable**: independent measurements of subsystems allowed classical computation of bounds on F(t), and the quantum results fell within the predicted ranges, proving the quantum processor was computing OTOC correctly (not just returning random numbers).

### Chaos Signatures in the Measurement

Beyond raw speedup, the measurements revealed **signatures of quantum chaos**:

```
F(t) ~ exp(-lambda_L * t)  for t in effective chaos regime
```

The effective Lyapunov exponent was extracted as:

```
lambda_L,eff ~ 0.1 to 0.3 (in units of inverse gate depth)
```

This is significantly slower than the SYK model's lambda_L = 2*pi*T, but consistent with expectations for a 2D qubit array with local interactions and disorder from fabrication imperfections.

## Key Results

1. **First Experimental OTOC on Scalable Processor**: Measured out-of-time-ordered correlators on up to 28-qubit systems using Willow.

2. **Verifiable Quantum Advantage**: 13,000x speedup over classical computers for OTOC computation, with independent verification that the speedup is genuine.

3. **Scrambling Signatures**: Observed exponential decay of F(t) with timescale consistent with information spreading from one subsystem to another.

4. **Chaos Evidence**: Extracted effective Lyapunov exponents from the decay data, providing experimental evidence for quantum chaos in a realistic many-body system.

5. **Scalability**: Demonstrated that OTOC measurements scale to tens of qubits, suggesting future extension to hundreds.

6. **Time-Reversal Protocol Innovation**: The Quantum Echoes algorithm is more robust to noise than direct four-point correlation measurement, improving scalability.

## Impact and Legacy

Google's Willow OTOC measurements marked a turning point in quantum chaos research:

- **Experimental Quantum Chaos**: Proved that quantum chaos can be measured in engineered quantum systems
- **Quantum Advantage Proof of Concept**: Demonstrated quantum advantage for a task (OTOC) that is theoretically motivated but previously inaccessible
- **Benchmarking Tool**: OTOC measurements can serve as benchmark for quantum processor quality
- **Cross-Disciplinary Interest**: Results attracted attention from condensed matter, quantum information, and high-energy physics communities
