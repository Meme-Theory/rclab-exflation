## Abstract

This seminal paper proposes that the same arguments which lead to Hawking's prediction of black-hole evaporation also predict that a thermal spectrum of sound waves should be emitted from the sonic horizon in transsonic fluid flow. The paper demonstrates the mathematical analogy between the dynamics of sound waves in a supercritical fluid and quantum fields at the event horizon of gravitational black holes, suggesting that analog black hole experiments might be experimentally feasible.

---

## Historical Context

Before 1981, Hawking radiation was a purely theoretical prediction with no experimental test imaginable. Unruh recognized that the mathematical structure of Hawking's derivation could be reproduced in hydrodynamic systems where a sonic horizon exists. If a flowing fluid's velocity exceeds the speed of sound locally (transsonic flow), sound waves cannot propagate upstream from that region. The locus where flow velocity equals sound speed defines a sonic horizon, mathematically analogous to a gravitational event horizon. At this interface, pair-creation should operate for phonons as for gravitons.

The paper's title includes a question mark—a hedge acknowledging how speculative experimental verification seemed. Yet the mathematics was rigorous, establishing the foundation for an entirely new experimental approach to testing quantum gravitational physics.

## Impact and Legacy

Unruh's 1981 paper catalyzed the field of analog gravity. It demonstrated that gravitational physics could be mimicked by carefully engineered condensed matter systems. The paper laid groundwork for decades of work in BEC analog black holes, optical systems, and fundamental quantum gravity testing. Today, analog gravity is a mature field with dedicated conferences and recognized contributions to quantum information.
