# Superfluid Analogies of Cosmological Phenomena

**Author:** Grigory E. Volovik
**Year:** 2001
**Journal:** Physics Reports, Vol. 351, No. 4, pp. 195–348

---

## Abstract

This seminal paper demonstrates that superfluid 3He-A exhibits an effective spacetime metric and supports quasiparticle excitations behaving like relativistic fermions and gauge bosons—a complete realization of quantum field theory in a condensed matter system. The superfluid's order parameter determines a non-Euclidean effective metric in momentum space, leading to: (1) chiral Weyl fermion spectrum at low energy; (2) spontaneous emergence of gauge fields and general covariance; (3) Hawking-like radiation at defects and interfaces (vortex cores, domain walls); (4) particle creation and anomaly-mediated processes (axial anomaly, baryon asymmetry); (5) vacuum energy fine-tuning problem paralleled by cosmological constant in the superfluid. The paper argues that quantum field theory is not fundamental but emergent—an effective description of low-energy excitations in a system with more complex microscopic structure. The superfluid provides a physical laboratory for testing cosmological and high-energy physics concepts experimentally, offering insights into vacuum structure, symmetry breaking, and the origin of particle physics.

---

## Historical Context

The idea that quantum field theory and general relativity might emerge from condensed matter has deep roots but gained traction after:

1. **Ginzburg-Landau theory (1950s)**: Showed that superconductivity can be understood from a symmetry-breaking condensate, without importing quantum mechanics from outside. The phase transition and collective modes emerged naturally.

2. **Landau's two-fluid model (1941)**: Demonstrated that superfluidity in liquid 4He arises from phonon and roton excitations—emergent quasiparticles that behave like independent particles despite being collective modes of the underlying liquid.

3. **Effective field theory (1970s-1980s)**: Led to recognition that quantum field theories valid at one energy scale emerge from deeper theories at higher scales, through renormalization group flow. The question naturally arises: what is the deepest "underlying" theory?

4. **Superfluid 3He discovery (1972)**: Experiments revealed that liquid 3He undergoes a superfluid transition at ~2 millikelvin, with an order parameter and symmetry structure distinct from 4He. The anisotropic B phase and axial A phase have rich topological structure.

5. **Volovik's realization (1990s-2000)**: Volovik recognized that superfluid 3He-A, with its order parameter structure, naturally supports a spectrum of relativistic fermions (Weyl quasiparticles) and exhibits effective general covariance. This 2001 paper synthesizes this insight into a comprehensive analogy between superfluid physics and quantum field theory/cosmology.

The profound implication: if a "simple" condensed matter system can generate quantum field theory, then perhaps nature's vacuum is similarly a condensate with richer underlying structure.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### Superfluid 3He-A Order Parameter and Effective Metric

The superfluid 3He-A has an order parameter that is a complex 3x3 matrix:

$$A_{ij}(\mathbf{r}) = (A_x \hat{\mathbf{d}}, A_y \hat{\mathbf{d}}, A_z \hat{\mathbf{d}})_{ij}$$

where $\hat{\mathbf{d}}$ is the orbital angular momentum direction (a unit vector specifying the pairing). In the bulk ground state, the order parameter has a definite configuration. Quasiparticles (excitations) propagate through this order parameter.

The key insight: the order parameter acts as an **effective metric** in momentum space. For a Bogoliubov quasiparticle with momentum $\mathbf{p}$ and energy $E$, the dispersion relation is:

$$E(\mathbf{p}) = c_F |\mathbf{p}|$$

for momenta near special points in momentum space (gapless nodes). This is identical to the dispersion relation for a massless relativistic particle, $E = pc$ (with $c_F$ playing the role of light speed).

More precisely, the effective metric tensor $g^{\mu\nu}$ in momentum space is determined by the order parameter's spatial and temporal structure:

$$g^{\mu\nu} = \begin{pmatrix} -c_F^{-2} & \text{spatial components} \\ \text{spatial components} & \delta^{ij} \end{pmatrix}$$

Quasiparticles experience an effective "gravitational field" encoded in spatial variations of the order parameter (inhomogeneities). A vortex line in the superfluid—where the order parameter winds by $2\pi$—creates a region of modified metric, analogous to a gravitational field or spacetime curvature.

### Weyl Fermions and Chiral Symmetry

In superfluid 3He-A, the low-energy quasiparticle spectrum consists of Weyl fermions at special points in momentum space:

$$\mathcal{H}_{\text{Weyl}} = \sigma^i p_i$$

where $\sigma^i$ are Pauli matrices. This is the Weyl Hamiltonian, describing a chirality-one quasiparticle. The spectrum has a linear nodal point at $\mathbf{p} = 0$:

$$E = \pm v_F |\mathbf{p}|$$

where $v_F$ is the Fermi velocity. Left-handed and right-handed chiral fermions appear as excitations on different branches.

This chiral structure is crucial: it explains why the superfluid spontaneously breaks parity symmetry and supports chiral anomalies—the same phenomena observed in high-energy physics particle interactions.

### Emergent Gauge Fields and General Covariance

Superfluid 3He-A features several types of defects:

1. **Vortices**: singular lines around which the order parameter winds
2. **Domain walls**: interfaces between regions with different order parameter orientations
3. **Skyrmions**: topological textures in the order parameter

Each defect acts as a source of an effective gauge field. A vortex with winding number $n$ creates an effective angular momentum $n\hbar$ and acts as a solenoid for the quasiparticles—an analogue of a charged particle experiencing a magnetic field.

More broadly, the superfluid's inhomogeneity induces an effective metric $g_{\mu\nu}(\mathbf{r})$ that varies in space. Quasiparticles propagating through this inhomogeneous metric experience effects equivalent to general relativity:

$$\nabla_\mu g_{\nu\rho} = 0$$

is automatically satisfied by the emergent geometry. Gravity "emerges" as the long-wavelength effective description of phonon and quasiparticle propagation in the superfluid.

### Hawking Radiation in Superfluid Vortex Cores

A vortex in superfluid 3He-A has a core—a region of radius ~coherence length $\xi$ where the order parameter vanishes. Near the core, the metric becomes singular, analogous to an event horizon in general relativity.

The quasiparticle spectrum is modified near the vortex core. Some quasiparticles can be trapped in bound states in the core. Others scatter off the core and are reflected. This scattering creates an analogue of Hawking radiation:

- Quasiparticle pairs (one real, one virtual) are created near the vortex core
- One branch of the pair falls into the "event horizon" (core); the other escapes
- Net result: radiation of quasiparticles from the vortex, with spectrum similar to Hawking's prediction for black holes

The temperature of the effective Hawking radiation is:

$$T_{\text{Hawking}} = \frac{\hbar \kappa}{2\pi k_B}$$

where $\kappa$ is the surface gravity analog (related to the vortex core structure). In superfluid 3He, $T_{\text{Hawking}} \sim 1$ mK—small but measurable.

### Particle Creation and Anomalies

The axial anomaly in quantum field theory—the non-conservation of axial current due to triangle diagrams—has an analogue in superfluid 3He. When the effective metric or gauge field changes (e.g., domain walls sweeping through the superfluid), the chirality of quasiparticles is not conserved:

$$\partial_\mu j_\mu^5 \propto g^{\mu\nu} \partial_\mu A_\nu$$

This leads to real particle creation: the number of left-handed vs. right-handed quasiparticles is not conserved, and new quasiparticles are spontaneously generated.

The process is analogous to baryogenesis in the early universe, where CP-violating interactions during electroweak phase transition created an asymmetry between matter and antimatter. In the superfluid, fermion-number-violating processes can create an excess of quasiparticles of one chirality, analogous to matter-antimatter asymmetry.

### Vacuum Energy and Cosmological Constant

The superfluid has a ground state energy density. In effective field theory, this appears as the cosmological constant $\Lambda$. Naively, quantum corrections to the ground state energy scale as $\Lambda \sim \rho_{\text{quasiparticles}}^5$ (from power-counting in the low-energy effective theory).

But this naive estimate gives $\Lambda \sim 10^{120}$ times larger than the observed cosmological constant in our universe—the famous fine-tuning problem. Why is $\Lambda$ so small?

Volovik's insight: In superfluid 3He, the ground state energy is exactly zero at equilibrium (the superfluid's pressure balances its stress tensor perfectly). Higher-order corrections (from quasiparticle interactions) are exponentially suppressed. This suggests that nature's vacuum energy might also be exactly zero at equilibrium, with the tiny observed cosmological constant arising from non-equilibrium (inflation, dark energy fields, etc.).

## Key Results

1. **Complete relativistic spectrum realized**: Superfluid 3He-A supports a spectrum of quasiparticles (Weyl fermions) with linear dispersion $E = v_F |\mathbf{p}|$, identical to massless relativistic particles. All symmetries of quantum field theory (Lorentz invariance, gauge invariance, chiral symmetry) emerge.

2. **Effective spacetime metric confirmed**: The superfluid's order parameter determines an effective metric tensor $g_{\mu\nu}$ in momentum space (and coordinate space via inhomogeneities). Quasiparticles propagate as if moving through curved spacetime.

3. **Hawking radiation mechanism verified**: Quasiparticle radiation from vortex cores shows temperature scaling consistent with black hole Hawking radiation. The vortex acts as an effective black hole analogue.

4. **Chiral anomaly observed**: Experiments on domain wall dynamics confirm the axial anomaly: when domain walls move through the superfluid, fermion number is not conserved, and quasiparticles are created. This is the first direct observation of the chiral anomaly in any system.

5. **Vacuum energy problem solved (in the superfluid)**: The superfluid's ground state energy is exactly zero at equilibrium, resolving the naïve infinite cosmological constant. This suggests that fine-tuning in our universe might not be as extreme as appeared.

6. **Gravity as emergent from phonons**: Long-wavelength phonons in the superfluid (collective density fluctuations) propagate as if through an effective gravitational field. General relativity emerges as the long-wavelength hydrodynamic limit.

7. **Laboratory simulation of cosmology**: Superfluid 3He becomes a testable analog of quantum field theory and early universe cosmology. Phenomena previously inaccessible (Hawking radiation, particle creation in non-equilibrium fields) can be studied in the lab.

## Impact and Legacy

Volovik's 2001 paper is transformative for physics philosophy and methodology:

1. **Emergence vs. fundamentalism**: The paper demonstrates that quantum field theory—previously thought to be fundamental—emerges from more complex structure (the superfluid order parameter). This supports the emergentist view: what we call "fundamental" laws may be low-energy effective descriptions.

2. **Vacuum structure**: The superfluid provides a concrete physical model of the quantum vacuum. Rather than an abstract mathematical field, the vacuum is a real medium with structure, collective excitations (particles), and topological properties (defects).

3. **Cosmology in the lab**: Cosmological phenomena (Hawking radiation, particle creation, baryogenesis) are no longer only theoretical predictions about the unreachable early universe. They can be experimentally studied and measured in superfluid 3He.

4. **Symmetry emergence**: Symmetries previously thought to be fundamental (Lorentz invariance, gauge symmetry, general covariance) emerge dynamically from the superfluid's structure. This raises the question: are our universe's symmetries also emergent?

5. **Fine-tuning resolution**: The cosmological constant problem—why is vacuum energy so tiny?—may have a precedent in superfluid physics, where exact cancellations occur naturally. This offers hope for explaining observed fine-tuning.

6. **Topological insulators and materials**: Subsequent work on topological materials, topological insulators, and Weyl semimetals was directly inspired by Volovik's ideas. The connection between order parameter topology and electronic spectrum became central to condensed matter physics (see also paper 12, topological metamaterials).
