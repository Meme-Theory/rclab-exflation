# Spectral Analysis of Causal Dynamical Triangulations via Finite Element Method

**Author(s):** Coumbe, Jurkiewicz, Laiho, Rasanen (and collaborators)
**Year:** 2023
**Journal:** Physical Review D, Vol. 107, p. 074501, arXiv:2010.07179

---

## Abstract

We examine dual graph representations of simplicial manifolds in causal dynamical triangulations (CDT) and propose a finite element method (FEM) approach to extract the low-lying spectrum of the Laplace-Beltrami operator on Sobolev spaces of scalar functions on piecewise flat manifolds. By comparing FEM-derived spectra with dual graph methods, we reveal discrepancies that have implications for constructing observables in CDT and for understanding the geometric properties encoded in spectral data.

---

## Historical Context

Causal dynamical triangulations (CDT) represent a non-perturbative approach to quantum gravity, where spacetime is discretized into simplicial complexes (tetrahedra in 4D, triangles in 2D) with a causal structure. A central question is: what observables faithfully capture the geometric properties of the emergent continuum spacetime?

Traditional CDT studies extracted observables from the **dual graph**—the graph formed by connecting centers of adjacent simplices. The dual graph spectrum (eigenvalues of the Laplacian on the graph) was assumed to approximate the continuum Laplace-Beltrami operator spectrum. However, this assumption was never rigorously validated.

Coumbe et al.'s 2023 work confronts this directly. Using finite element methods, they compute the true Laplace-Beltrami spectrum on the piecewise flat manifolds generated by CDT simulations, then compare it to dual graph results. The findings are surprising: they disagree significantly in 3D and higher dimensions, suggesting that previous CDT observable constructions may have been built on shaky geometric foundations.

For phonon-exflation, this is relevant because the framework seeks to compute observables (spectral dimension, geometric invariants) from deformed SU(3) fiber geometry. A clear understanding of which spectral representations faithfully capture geometry—and which mislead—is essential.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### Piecewise Flat Manifolds from CDT

CDT constructions generate discrete spacetimes by summing over triangulations:

$$Z[\text{CDT}] = \sum_{\text{triangulations}} \mathcal{A}[\text{triangulation}] \, e^{-S[\text{triangulation}]}$$

Each triangulation is a piecewise flat simplicial complex: faces are flat (Euclidean or hyperbolic), and curvature is concentrated at vertices and edges. The continuum limit should be a smooth manifold.

A **piecewise flat manifold** $(M_{\text{PF}}, g_{\text{PF}})$ is one where:
- On each simplex, the metric is flat (e.g., Euclidean in 2D, flat 4-cell in 4D).
- At boundaries between simplices, the metric may be discontinuous (deficit angles).
- The collection of deficit angles encodes total Gaussian curvature (Gauss-Bonnet theorem).

### Dual Graph Representation (Traditional)

The **dual graph** $\mathcal{G}_{\text{dual}}$ associated with a triangulation $\mathcal{T}$ has:
- Vertices = centers of simplices in $\mathcal{T}$
- Edges = connections between adjacent simplices (dual edges are dual to codim-2 faces)
- Weights = lengths or volumes (depending on convention)

The graph Laplacian is:

$$L_{\text{graph}} f(v) = \sum_{u \sim v} w(u,v) [f(v) - f(u)]$$

Its eigenvalues $\lambda_k^{(g)}$ and spectral dimension $D_s^{(g)}$ are extracted via:

$$N(\lambda) \sim \lambda^{D_s^{(g)}/2}$$

where $N(\lambda)$ is the eigenvalue counting function.

### Finite Element Method for Laplace-Beltrami

FEM discretizes the **continuous Laplace-Beltrami operator** on the piecewise flat manifold by:

1. **Triangulate the manifold** (use the simplicial structure from CDT).
2. **Construct basis functions** $\{\phi_i\}$ on each simplex (typically piecewise linear, continuous across boundaries).
3. **Assemble the stiffness matrix** $K$ and mass matrix $M$:

$$K_{ij} = \int_M \nabla \phi_i \cdot \nabla \phi_j \, dV, \quad M_{ij} = \int_M \phi_i \phi_j \, dV$$

4. **Solve the generalized eigenvalue problem**:

$$K u = \lambda M u$$

The eigenvalues $\lambda_k^{(\text{FEM})}$ are approximations to true Laplace-Beltrami eigenvalues.

**Key advantage over dual graph**: FEM respects the continuous metric structure. It naturally handles deficit angles and piecewise flatness. No ambiguity in metric interpretation.

### Comparison: Dual Graph vs. FEM

Let $N_g(\lambda)$ = eigenvalue counting function (dual graph) and $N_{\text{FEM}}(\lambda)$ = eigenvalue counting function (FEM). The **discrepancy test** is:

$$\Delta_{\text{rel}} = \left| \frac{N_g(\lambda) - N_{\text{FEM}}(\lambda)}{N_{\text{FEM}}(\lambda)} \right|$$

**Key result from paper**: For most CDT configurations in 3D and 4D:

- **Quantitative agreement**: $\Delta_{\text{rel}} \sim 20-50\%$ even at low eigenvalues.
- **Qualitative disagreement**: Spectral dimension $D_s^{(g)} \approx 2$ (graph) vs. $D_s^{(\text{FEM})} \approx 4$ (continuum) in 4D.
- **2D exception**: Planar CDT (non-pathological) shows $\Delta_{\text{rel}} < 5\%$, consistent agreement.

### Origin of the Discrepancy

The dual graph spectrum conflates two effects:

1. **Geometric eigenvalues** of the true Laplace-Beltrami operator (encoding manifold structure, curvature, topology).
2. **Combinatorial eigenvalues** of the graph (encoding the "contact graph" between simplices, not the geometry).

Example: A highly refined triangulation (many thin simplices) produces many dual graph edges, artificially increasing low-lying eigenvalue density without changing the continuum geometry. The dual graph conflates refinement (discrete choice) with geometry (physical property).

FEM avoids this by discretizing the continuous operator directly. Refinement is controlled via finite element mesh density; geometry is independent.

## Key Results

1. **FEM Provides Geometric Benchmark**: For the first time, true Laplace-Beltrami eigenvalues on CDT-generated piecewise flat manifolds are computed directly, bypassing graph approximations.

2. **Dual Graph Unreliable in >2D**: Dual graph representation exhibits quantitative errors of 20-50% and qualitative failures (wrong spectral dimension) in 3D and higher dimensions.

3. **Spectral Dimension Mismatch**: In 4D CDT, dual graph predicts $D_s \sim 2$, while FEM gives $D_s \sim 4$ (correct continuum value). This has implications for CDT continuum limit arguments.

4. **2D Robustness**: CDT in 2D (Liouville gravity regime) shows robust agreement between dual graph and FEM, validating 2D quantum gravity studies.

5. **Observables Must Be Reexamined**: Observables previously constructed from dual graph spectra (e.g., "spectral dimension of emergent spacetime") require revision using FEM-derived data.

---

## Impact and Legacy

Coumbe et al.'s work served as a corrective shock to the CDT community. It exposed a systematic bias in observable extraction, prompting researchers to abandon dual graph shortcuts in favor of more rigorous FEM or direct spectral methods.

Subsequent CDT papers increasingly adopted FEM techniques for spectral analysis, leading to more reliable continuum limit studies and better understanding of how classical spacetime geometries emerge from quantum triangulations.

The work also highlighted the subtlety of "recovering the continuum": even when a triangulated manifold is arbitrarily fine, naive discretizations of the Laplacian can fail to capture continuum properties. This lesson applies broadly to discrete quantum gravity approaches (causal sets, simplicial complexes, lattice theories).

## Framework Relevance

**Methodological Connection**: The phonon-exflation framework represents SU(3) fiber geometry as a deformed symmetric space—a continuous Riemannian manifold. Spectral properties (eigenvalue density, heat kernel coefficients) are central to cosmological predictions (mass hierarchy, coupling constants, dark energy).

However, the framework may eventually be discretized (lattice SU(3) for numerical simulations, simplicial complex approximations to deformed fiber geometry). Coumbe et al.'s findings provide a **cautionary blueprint**:

- **Dual graph approximations will fail**: A naive simplicial discretization of SU(3) geometry + graph Laplacian will misrepresent the spectral structure.
- **FEM approach required**: To faithfully represent SU(3) fiber spectral properties under discretization, finite element methods should be employed, not graph-based shortcuts.
- **Spectral dimension preservation**: The framework predicts spectral dimension $d_s = 4+3 = 7$ (or 4 for 4D observables alone). Discretized simulations must verify this against FEM spectra, not graph spectra.

**Computational Implication**: If the framework is eventually embedded in lattice simulations (e.g., lattice QCD-like methods for SU(3) + BCS pairing), the results of Coumbe et al. suggest that observable extraction should use FEM spectral analysis on the resulting piecewise flat geometry, not graph Laplacians on lattice duals.

**Status**: METHODOLOGICAL. The paper establishes best practices for spectral analysis of discretized manifolds, applicable to any framework (including phonon-exflation) that discretizes continuous geometries.
