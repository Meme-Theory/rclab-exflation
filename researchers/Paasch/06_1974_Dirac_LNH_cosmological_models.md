# Cosmological Models and the Large Numbers Hypothesis

**Authors**: Dirac P.A.M.
**Year**: 1974
**Journal**: Proceedings of the Royal Society of London A, vol. 338, no. 1615, pp. 439-446
**DOI**: 10.1098/rspa.1974.0095
**Source**: https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rspa.1974.0095

---

## Abstract

The Large Numbers Hypothesis asserts that all the large dimensionless numbers occurring in
Nature are connected with the present epoch, expressed in atomic units, and thus vary with
time. The hypothesis requires that the gravitational constant $G$ shall vary, and also that
there shall be continuous creation of matter. The consistent following out of the hypothesis
leads to the possibility of only two cosmological models: one with additive creation (matter
created uniformly throughout space) and one with multiplicative creation (matter created where
mass already exists). Both models are examined in detail.

---

## Historical Context

This 1974 paper is Dirac's mature, systematic presentation of the Large Numbers Hypothesis
(LNH), which he first proposed in 1937. By 1974, Dirac had been developing the idea for
nearly four decades, and this paper represents his most complete treatment.

The LNH emerges from a striking empirical observation: certain dimensionless ratios formed
from fundamental constants and cosmological parameters are enormous numbers of roughly
similar magnitude (~$10^{40}$ or its square). Rather than dismiss this as coincidence, Dirac
elevated it to a physical principle: these large numbers must be RELATED, and since some
involve cosmological quantities that change with time (like the age of the universe), ALL
such ratios must be time-dependent.

The most dramatic consequence is that Newton's gravitational constant $G$ must decrease with
cosmic time: $G \propto 1/t$. This paper works out the cosmological consequences of this
hypothesis in detail, deriving two possible cosmological models.

By 1974, General Relativity was firmly established, and Dirac's proposal represented a
radical departure from Einstein's framework. The idea of varying $G$ was considered
speculative by most physicists, but Dirac's stature ensured it received serious attention.

This paper is the SPECIFIC VERSION that Paasch cites as [1] in his Paper 03 (2016). Paasch
adopts the $G \propto 1/t$ relation as the cosmological foundation for his mass model.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### The Large Dimensionless Numbers

Dirac identifies several dimensionless numbers of order $\sim 10^{40}$:

**1. The Weyl-Eddington Ratio** (electromagnetic to gravitational force):

$$N_1 = \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 G m_p m_e} \approx 2.27 \times 10^{39}$$

This is the ratio of the electromagnetic force to the gravitational force between a proton
and electron.

**2. The Cosmological Ratio** (age of universe in atomic units):

$$N_2 = \frac{t_0}{e^2 / (m_e c^3)} \approx 6.6 \times 10^{40}$$

where $t_0$ is the age of the universe and $e^2/(m_e c^3)$ is the classical electron
time scale.

**3. The Eddington Number** (number of particles in the observable universe):

$$N_3 \approx 10^{80} \approx N_1^2$$

The striking observation is that $N_1 \sim N_2$ and $N_3 \sim N_1^2$.

### The Hypothesis

Dirac's hypothesis states:

> Any two of the very large dimensionless numbers occurring in Nature are connected by a
> simple mathematical relation, in which the coefficients are of the order of magnitude
> unity.

Since $N_2$ depends on $t_0$ (which changes as the universe ages), the equality $N_1 \sim N_2$
can only be maintained if $N_1$ also changes with time. Since $e$, $m_p$, $m_e$ are assumed
constant in atomic units, this requires:

$$G \propto 1/t$$

The gravitational constant DECREASES as the universe ages.

### Consequences for $G$

The rate of change of $G$ implied by the LNH is:

$$\frac{\dot{G}}{G} = -\frac{1}{t_0} \approx -7 \times 10^{-11} \text{ yr}^{-1}$$

This is an enormous rate of change by modern standards. Current Lunar Laser Ranging (LLR)
experiments constrain $|\dot{G}/G| < 10^{-13}$ yr$^{-1}$, ruling out the LNH prediction by
a factor of ~100 or more.

### Continuous Creation of Matter

To maintain $N_3 \sim N_1^2$ as $N_1$ varies, the number of particles in the observable
universe must also change: $N_3 \propto t^2$. This requires continuous creation of matter.
Dirac considers two scenarios:

**Additive Creation**: New matter appears uniformly distributed throughout space, independent
of existing matter distributions. This is similar to the Steady State cosmology of Hoyle,
Bondi, and Gold, but within an expanding universe context.

**Multiplicative Creation**: New matter appears preferentially where matter already exists,
proportional to the local matter density. Dirac argues this leads to:

$$\frac{dM}{dt} \propto M$$

i.e., exponential growth of matter concentrations. This would cause stars and planets to
gain mass over cosmic time.

### The Two Cosmological Models

**Model A (Additive)**: Flat, matter-dominated universe with:
- $R(t) \propto t^{1/3}$ (scale factor)
- $\rho(t) \propto t^{-1}$ (density)
- $G(t) \propto t^{-1}$
- Matter created uniformly; galaxies move apart while new matter fills the gaps

**Model B (Multiplicative)**: Curved universe with:
- $R(t) \propto t$ (linear expansion)
- Individual masses grow: $m(t) \propto t$
- $G(t) \propto t^{-1}$ (same)
- Stars, planets gain mass continuously

Dirac preferred Model B (multiplicative creation) in this paper.

## Key Results

1. The Large Numbers Hypothesis requires $G \propto 1/t$ (gravitational constant decreasing
   with cosmic time)
2. $\dot{G}/G \approx -1/t_0 \approx -7 \times 10^{-11}$ yr$^{-1}$
3. Continuous creation of matter is necessary to maintain the large number coincidences
4. Two cosmological models are possible: additive (uniform creation) and multiplicative
   (creation proportional to existing mass)
5. Both models are compatible with observed Hubble expansion
6. The multiplicative model predicts that stellar and planetary masses increase with time
7. The LNH connects microphysics (atomic constants) to cosmology (universe age) through
   dimensionless ratios

## Impact and Legacy

Dirac's LNH was one of the most ambitious attempts to connect microphysics with cosmology.
Its legacy includes:

**Positive influences:**
- Inspired Brans-Dicke theory (a scalar-tensor gravity theory that naturally accommodates
  varying $G$)
- Motivated experimental programs to measure $\dot{G}/G$ (Lunar Laser Ranging, binary pulsar
  timing, Big Bang nucleosynthesis constraints)
- Established the tradition of taking "large number coincidences" seriously as potential
  physical clues
- Influenced the anthropic principle (Dicke's response: we observe these numbers BECAUSE
  they permit our existence)

**Negative results:**
- LLR constraints: $|\dot{G}/G| < (4 \pm 9) \times 10^{-13}$ yr$^{-1}$ (Williams et al.
  2004), ruling out $G \propto 1/t$ by a factor of ~100
- Binary pulsar timing independently constrains $\dot{G}/G$ to similar precision
- Big Bang nucleosynthesis places strong constraints on $G$ variation between $t \sim 1$ min
  and today
- No evidence for continuous matter creation has ever been found

The LNH is now considered EMPIRICALLY REFUTED in its original form. However, the algebraic
structures it inspired (including Paasch's mass numbers) may be independent of the
$G \propto 1/t$ scaffolding.
