## Abstract

Analyses of laser ranges to the Moon provide increasingly stringent limits on any violation
of the Equivalence Principle (EP). They also enable several very accurate tests of
relativistic gravity. The analyses give an EP test of
$\Delta(M_G/M_I)_{EP} = (-1.0 \pm 1.4) \times 10^{-13}$, which yields a strong EP (SEP)
test of $\Delta(M_G/M_I)_{SEP} = (-2.0 \pm 2.0) \times 10^{-13}$. The PPN parameter
$\beta$ is determined to be $\beta - 1 = (1.2 \pm 1.1) \times 10^{-4}$. Geodetic precession
is confirmed with a relative deviation from GR of $K_{gp} = -0.0019 \pm 0.0064$. For the
time variation of the gravitational constant, the result is
$\dot{G}/G = (4 \pm 9) \times 10^{-13}$ yr$^{-1}$.

## Historical Context

Lunar Laser Ranging (LLR) is one of the most precise tests of gravitational theory ever
devised. Since 1969, when Apollo astronauts placed corner retroreflectors on the lunar surface,
laser pulses fired from Earth have been bounced off these reflectors and timed with
extraordinary precision. The Earth-Moon distance is now known to better than 1 centimeter
accuracy.

This precision makes LLR sensitive to a wide range of gravitational effects:

1. **Equivalence Principle**: Does the Moon fall toward the Sun at the same rate as the
   Earth? (Tests whether gravitational self-energy affects free fall.)
2. **PPN parameters**: Do the post-Newtonian parameters agree with General Relativity?
3. **Geodetic precession**: Does the Moon's orbit precess as predicted by GR?
4. **$\dot{G}/G$**: Is the gravitational constant changing with time?

The constraint on $\dot{G}/G$ is the most relevant result for the Paasch framework, because
Paasch's cosmological scaffolding is built on Dirac's Large Numbers Hypothesis, which
predicts $G \propto 1/t$ and therefore $\dot{G}/G = -1/t_0 \approx -7 \times 10^{-11}$
yr$^{-1}$.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### The LLR Technique

The basic measurement is simple in concept: fire a short laser pulse toward the Moon, detect
the returning photons reflected by corner cube retroreflectors, and measure the round-trip
travel time with picosecond precision.

The round-trip travel time gives the Earth-Moon distance to ~1 cm accuracy. Over decades of
observations, the time series of distances reveals:

- **Orbital mechanics**: Tidal dissipation, lunar recession (3.8 cm/yr)
- **Relativistic effects**: Geodetic precession, gravitational time dilation
- **EP violations**: Differential acceleration toward the Sun
- **$\dot{G}$**: Secular change in orbital period

### The $\dot{G}/G$ Constraint

The gravitational constant enters the lunar orbit through Kepler's third law:

$$n^2 a^3 = G(M_E + M_M)$$

where $n$ is the mean motion, $a$ is the semi-major axis, $M_E$ and $M_M$ are the Earth and
Moon masses. If $G$ varies with time:

$$\frac{\dot{G}}{G} = 2\frac{\dot{n}}{n} - 3\frac{\dot{a}}{a}$$

The measured value is:

$$\frac{\dot{G}}{G} = (4 \pm 9) \times 10^{-13} \text{ yr}^{-1}$$

This is consistent with ZERO to within the uncertainty. The key implications:

**Dirac's LNH prediction**: $\dot{G}/G = -1/t_0 \approx -7.2 \times 10^{-11}$ yr$^{-1}$

**LLR constraint**: $|\dot{G}/G| < 1.3 \times 10^{-12}$ yr$^{-1}$ (at 95% confidence)

**Ratio**: The LNH prediction is ruled out by a factor of approximately:

$$\frac{7 \times 10^{-11}}{1.3 \times 10^{-12}} \approx 55-100$$

depending on the exact confidence interval used.

### Equivalence Principle Test

The EP test compares the free-fall acceleration of the Earth and Moon toward the Sun. If the
gravitational self-energy of a body contributes differently to its gravitational mass than
to its inertial mass (violating the Strong Equivalence Principle), the Earth (which has
proportionally more gravitational self-energy than the Moon) would fall differently.

The measured constraint:

$$\Delta\left(\frac{M_G}{M_I}\right)_{SEP} = (-2.0 \pm 2.0) \times 10^{-13}$$

This is the most stringent test of the Strong Equivalence Principle, and it is consistent
with exact equality (GR prediction).

### PPN Parameter $\beta$

The Parameterized Post-Newtonian (PPN) parameter $\beta$ measures the nonlinearity of the
gravitational superposition principle. In GR, $\beta = 1$ exactly. The LLR result:

$$\beta - 1 = (1.2 \pm 1.1) \times 10^{-4}$$

This constrains $\beta$ to agree with GR to 0.01%.

## Key Results

1. $\dot{G}/G = (4 \pm 9) \times 10^{-13}$ yr$^{-1}$ -- consistent with zero, ruling out
   Dirac's LNH by a factor of ~55-100
2. $\Delta(M_G/M_I)_{EP} = (-1.0 \pm 1.4) \times 10^{-13}$ -- Equivalence Principle confirmed
3. $\Delta(M_G/M_I)_{SEP} = (-2.0 \pm 2.0) \times 10^{-13}$ -- Strong EP confirmed
4. $\beta - 1 = (1.2 \pm 1.1) \times 10^{-4}$ -- GR confirmed
5. Geodetic precession: $K_{gp} = -0.0019 \pm 0.0064$ -- GR confirmed
6. No evidence for local (~1 AU) scale expansion of the solar system
7. All results consistent with General Relativity to extraordinary precision

## Impact and Legacy

This paper (and subsequent updates with more data) established the gold standard for
experimental constraints on varying $G$. The results have been updated several times:

- **2012 update**: Williams, Turyshev, Boggs, Class. Quantum Grav. 29, 184004 (2012)
  achieved $\dot{G}/G = (7.1 \pm 7.6) \times 10^{-14}$ yr$^{-1}$ -- an order of
  magnitude improvement
- **Independent confirmation**: Binary pulsar timing (Kaspi et al.) provides comparable
  constraints from completely different physics
- **Big Bang nucleosynthesis**: Constrains $G$ at $t \sim 1$ min, ruling out large variations
  over cosmological time

The LLR constraint is widely considered DEFINITIVE evidence against Dirac's LNH in its
original form. Any theory predicting $G \propto 1/t$ is ruled out by two orders of
magnitude.
