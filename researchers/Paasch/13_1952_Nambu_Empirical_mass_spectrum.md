# An Empirical Mass Spectrum of Elementary Particles

**Authors**: Nambu Y.
**Year**: 1952
**Journal**: Progress of Theoretical Physics, vol. 7, no. 5-6, pp. 595-596
**DOI**: 10.1143/PTP.7.5.595
**Source**: https://academic.oup.com/ptp/article/7/5-6/595/1881337

---

## Abstract

Nambu proposes an empirical formula for the mass spectrum of elementary particles. The
formula generates particle masses as approximate integer or half-integer multiples of a
fundamental mass unit, suggesting a quantized mass spectrum. The famous approximation
$m_\mu/m_e \approx (3/2) \cdot (1/\alpha)$, relating the muon-to-electron mass ratio to
the fine structure constant, originates from this paper.

---

## Historical Context

This brief but remarkably prescient paper, published when Nambu was a young physicist at
Osaka City University (before his move to Chicago in 1952), represents the EARLIEST known
systematic attempt to find algebraic regularities in the elementary particle mass spectrum.

In 1952, the particle zoo was just beginning to expand. Known particles included:
- Electron ($m_e = 0.511$ MeV)
- Muon ($m_\mu = 105.7$ MeV), discovered 1936
- Pion ($m_\pi = 135-140$ MeV), discovered 1947
- Kaon ($m_K \approx 494$ MeV), discovered 1947
- Proton ($m_p = 938.3$ MeV)
- Neutron ($m_n = 939.6$ MeV)
- Lambda ($m_\Lambda \approx 1116$ MeV), discovered 1950

No theoretical framework explained WHY these particles had these specific masses. The quark
model was still a decade away (Gell-Mann and Zweig, 1964), and the Standard Model's Higgs
mechanism even further (1967-1971). In this vacuum, empirical pattern-hunting was a legitimate
scientific strategy.

Nambu -- who would later receive the 2008 Nobel Prize for his work on spontaneous symmetry
breaking -- brought his characteristic mathematical insight to bear on this problem. The
result was a two-page letter that established the entire tradition of empirical mass
quantization that Muraki (1978), Koide (1983), Mac Gregor (2007), and Paasch (2009) would
later continue.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### The Mass Formula

Nambu observed that the masses of known elementary particles could be approximately expressed
as:

$$m_n \approx n \cdot m_0$$

where $n$ is an integer or simple fraction and $m_0$ is a fundamental mass unit related to
the electron mass and the fine structure constant $\alpha \approx 1/137$.

### The Muon-Electron Relation

The most famous result from this paper is the approximation:

$$\frac{m_\mu}{m_e} \approx \frac{3}{2\alpha} = \frac{3}{2} \times 137 = 205.5$$

Compare with the experimental value:

$$\frac{m_\mu}{m_e} = 206.768$$

The agreement is within 0.6%. This is remarkable because:
1. It uses ONLY the fine structure constant (an electromagnetic quantity) to predict a
   mass ratio
2. The factor 3/2 is a simple rational number
3. There is no obvious reason why the muon mass should be related to $\alpha^{-1}$ times
   the electron mass

### Integer Mass Multiples

Nambu noted that several particle masses could be expressed as approximate integer multiples
of a fundamental unit. Using $m_0 = m_e / \alpha \approx 70$ MeV as a reference scale:

| Particle | Mass (MeV) | $m/m_0$ | Nearest integer |
|----------|------------|---------|-----------------|
| Pion ($\pi^\pm$) | 140 | 2.0 | 2 |
| Muon ($\mu$) | 106 | 1.5 | 3/2 |
| Kaon ($K$) | 494 | 7.1 | 7 |
| Proton ($p$) | 938 | 13.4 | 13-14 |

The approximate integer values suggested a quantized mass spectrum analogous to atomic
energy levels, but for particle masses.

### Physical Interpretation

Nambu did not provide a dynamical model to explain these patterns. He treated the mass
formula as a PHENOMENOLOGICAL observation, analogous to Balmer's formula for hydrogen lines
(1885) which preceded quantum mechanics by 40 years. The hope was that the algebraic
pattern would eventually be explained by a deeper theory.

## Key Results

1. Elementary particle masses show approximate integer or half-integer patterns when
   expressed in units of $m_e/\alpha$
2. The muon-electron mass ratio $m_\mu/m_e \approx 3/(2\alpha) = 205.5$ (0.6% accuracy)
3. The fundamental mass unit $m_0 = m_e/\alpha \approx 70$ MeV serves as a natural scale
4. The patterns suggest a quantized mass spectrum with an algebraic origin
5. No dynamical explanation is provided -- the formula is purely empirical
6. The paper is only 2 pages but launched an entire research tradition

## Impact and Legacy

Nambu's 1952 paper had a lasting influence on attempts to understand the particle mass
spectrum:

**Direct influence:**
- Muraki, Mori, Nakagawa (1978): Extended Nambu's integer mass approach to logarithmic
  mass formulas
- Mac Gregor (2007): Developed alpha-quantized mass formulas building on Nambu's observation
  that $\alpha$ governs mass ratios
- Paasch (2009): Combined Nambu's integer mass numbers with a spiral geometry and
  cosmological embedding

**Intellectual lineage:**
Nambu -> Muraki (1978) -> Paasch (2009): a direct line of development from empirical mass
integers to logarithmic mass spirals.

**Contrast with later Nambu work:**
Ironically, Nambu's most important contributions to understanding mass came later through a
completely different route: spontaneous symmetry breaking (1960), which became the conceptual
foundation for the Higgs mechanism. His 1952 empirical approach and his 1960 dynamical
approach represent two fundamentally different strategies for understanding mass -- and the
dynamical approach (SSB/Higgs) won. But the empirical patterns remain UNEXPLAINED by the
Higgs mechanism, which parameterizes but does not predict masses.

**Modern status:**
The $m_\mu/m_e \approx 3/(2\alpha)$ relation remains unexplained. It is either:
1. A coincidence (at the ~0.6% level, roughly a 2-sigma effect)
2. A hint of deeper structure that the SM parameterizes away
