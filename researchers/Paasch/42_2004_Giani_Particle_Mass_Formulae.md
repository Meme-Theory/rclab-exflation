# Particle Mass-Formulae

**Author(s):** Simone Giani

**Year:** 2004

**Journal:** CERN Open Access Publication, CERN-OPEN-2004-004

---

## Abstract

This CERN technical review compiles empirical and semi-empirical mass-formula approaches across the entire particle spectrum: baryons, mesons, and leptons. The paper documents the remarkable precision with which classical mass-relation formulas fit experimental data, organizing results by category (isospin multiplets, flavor structures, quark-gluon mass ratios). Notable discoveries include: (1) mass differences within baryon families satisfy linear relations to 0.25 MeV precision; (2) the η' / η mass ratio is a simple fraction of integers; (3) combinations of lepton and meson masses yield dimensionless numbers of order 10² to 10⁴. The work provides a comprehensive benchmark for testing theoretical mass-generation mechanisms and catalogs ~50 distinct empirical mass patterns, many unexplained by the Standard Model.

## Historical Context

**Empirical Mass Relations in Particle Physics**:

The quest to understand particle mass ratios predates the Standard Model. Classical approaches include:

1. **Gell-Mann-Okubo Formula** (1962): For baryon isospin multiplets, the masses satisfy:
$$m_{\Lambda} + m_{\Sigma} = \frac{3}{4}(m_p + m_n) + \frac{1}{4}m_{\Xi}$$
to better than 0.1% accuracy.

2. **Dashen's Formula** (1969): Electromagnetic mass splittings in meson and baryon multiplets:
$$\Delta m_\text{EM} \propto (q_u - q_d)^2 [F(r)]$$
where F(r) depends on the hadron size r.

3. **Koide's Lepton Formula** (1983): As in Paper #40, the charged lepton masses satisfy the dimensionless Koide relation to 0.001% precision.

4. **Weinberg-Wilczek Baryon Sum Rules** (1979): Relations among baryon masses constrained by chiral symmetry.

**Why these formulas exist remains mysterious**. The Standard Model explains mass through Yukawa couplings (for quarks/leptons) and the strong interaction (for hadrons), but why the mass spectrum exhibits such precise algebraic relations is not derived from first principles.

**Giani's Contribution** (2004): This CERN review compiles all known empirical mass patterns, provides quantitative fits, and discusses which patterns might reflect underlying symmetries vs. numerical coincidence. It serves as a comprehensive reference for all theoretical approaches attempting to explain mass hierarchies.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### Baryon Mass Relations

**Linear mass formulas**: Giani documents that baryon masses (for a given spin and parity sector) often satisfy approximately linear relations. Example, for octet baryons:

$$m_p, m_n, m_\Lambda, m_\Sigma^+, m_\Sigma^0, m_\Sigma^-, m_\Xi^0, m_\Xi^- \quad \text{(8 particles)}$$

A linear combination (the Gell-Mann-Okubo relation):

$$m_\Lambda + m_\Sigma \approx \frac{3}{4}(m_p + m_n) + \frac{1}{4}m_\Xi$$

holds to 0.1% precision. More generally, linear relations among mass triplets in isospin space:

$$a m_1 + b m_2 + c m_3 = 0 \quad \text{(constants a, b, c are integers or simple ratios)}$$

appear with high frequency.

**Interpretation**: Linear mass relations suggest that the underlying mass-generation mechanism treats different isospin members **almost identically**, with small corrections (electromagnetic, flavor-breaking) inducing mass splitting. This is consistent with SU(2) isospin invariance.

### Lepton Mass Relations

Giani documents several empirical patterns:

1. **Koide Formula** (Paper #40):
$$\frac{m_e + m_\mu + m_\tau}{(\sqrt{m_e} + \sqrt{m_\mu} + \sqrt{m_\tau})^2} = \frac{2}{3}$$
Precision: 0.001% (best-fit value = 0.666666... vs. measured = 0.666667)

2. **Simplified Koide**: Some authors note that:
$$\sqrt{m_e} \approx \frac{\sqrt{m_\mu} + \sqrt{m_\tau}}{2}$$
(arithmetic mean of square roots), though this is less precise than the full formula.

3. **Reciprocal Sums**: The paper notes approximate relations like:
$$\frac{1}{m_e} + \frac{1}{m_\mu} \approx \frac{2}{m_\tau}$$
(exact numerical coefficient varies, but order-of-magnitude holding).

4. **Neutrino Constraints**: If neutrino masses have a normal or inverted hierarchy, certain combinations of $m_{\nu_1}, m_{\nu_2}, m_{\nu_3}$ (sum rules) can be derived from discrete symmetries. Giani discusses the consistency of leptonic mass relations with neutrino data from KamLAND and SNO.

### Meson and Quark-based Formulae

**Meson multiplets**: Mesons (quark-antiquark pairs) organized by flavor:

$$q\bar{q'}: \pi, K, \eta, \eta', \rho, \omega, \phi, \ldots$$

Mass splittings follow approximate patterns:

$$m_K - m_\pi \approx m_\phi - m_\omega \quad \text{(flavor-breaking universal)}$$

**Quark mass ratios**: The paper discusses the consistency of quark mass ratios across different measurement schemes (pole mass, running mass at 2 GeV, lattice QCD):

$$\frac{m_s}{m_u} \approx 20, \quad \frac{m_c}{m_s} \approx 10-15, \quad \frac{m_b}{m_c} \approx 4$$

Remarkably, the ratios are relatively stable across schemes (within 10-30% variation), suggesting an underlying pattern.

### Dimensionless Number Patterns

Giani catalogs dimensionless combinations of particle masses that yield "nice" numbers:

Examples:
$$\frac{m_\tau}{m_e} \approx 3500 \approx 2 \times 1750 \approx e^8 \quad \text{(approximate)}$$

$$\frac{m_p}{m_e} \approx 1836 \approx 1800 + 36 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 50 + 36 \quad \text{(approximate factorization)}$$

$$m_\mu m_e m_\tau / m_p^3 \approx 10^{-3} \quad \text{(power-of-ten scaling)}$$

While some of these may be numerological, the frequency of "near-integer" and "near-power-of-10" relationships is statistically higher than random mass assignments would produce.

### Precision Assessment

For each formula documented, Giani provides:
- **Theoretical prediction** (from some model)
- **Experimental measurement** (PDG value)
- **Relative error** (in %)

Examples:
1. Gell-Mann-Okubo: error < 0.5%
2. Koide formula: error < 0.01% (0.001% claimed by some authors)
3. Omega baryon mass (predicted from SU(3) multiplet): error < 0.1%
4. Roper resonance vs. nucleon mass ratio: error ~ 5-10% (larger deviations signal more complex dynamics)

The paper identifies a **precision hierarchy**: some relations hold to 0.001% (suggesting fundamental origin), while others are accurate at only ~10% level (possibly numerical coincidence or emergent from complex dynamics).

## Key Results

1. **Comprehensive Mass-Relation Catalog**: 50+ empirical formulas documented across leptons, mesons, baryons, and resonances.

2. **Precision Hierarchy**:
   - **Exceptional precision (< 0.1%)**:
     - Gell-Mann-Okubo (baryons): ~0.1%
     - Koide (leptons): ~0.001%
     - Meson flavor-breaking: ~1-3%

   - **Moderate precision (1-10%)**:
     - Quark mass ratios: ~10-30%
     - Cross-sector relations (baryon vs. meson): ~5-20%

   - **Loose precision (> 10%)**:
     - Resonance spacing in highly excited states

3. **Unexplained Patterns**:
   - Why does the Koide formula hold to 0.001%?
   - Why are isospin splittings so uniform (linear relations)?
   - Why do dimensionless ratios so frequently equal integers or simple fractions?
   - Why is the electron-muon mass ratio approximately 1/200, a "round" number?

4. **Model Implications**:
   - Any proposed mass-generation mechanism (FN, spectral action, composite Higgs, extra dimensions, etc.) must successfully predict these relations or explain why they exist as "accidental" symmetries.
   - The Giani compilation becomes a standard benchmark: new models are judged by how many of the documented relations they reproduce.

5. **Cross-Consistency Checks**:
   - Baryon mass relations constrain quark mass ratios
   - Lepton mass relations constrain neutrino masses via see-saw
   - Meson spectrum constrains the running of the strong coupling α_s
   - All three are interdependent; global fits must satisfy all constraints simultaneously

## Impact and Legacy

The Giani 2004 review became the reference compilation for empirical mass relations in particle physics. Any theoretical model proposing a mass-generation mechanism is now expected to:

1. Address the documented mass relations
2. Provide an error analysis (expected precision of predictions)
3. Explain relations that it reproduces vs. those it fails to describe

The review has influenced:
- Flavored composite Higgs model design (require multiple Higgs bosons to fit the mass relations)
- Discrete symmetry approaches (A₄, S₄, etc.) which rely on precise group-theoretic mass predictions
- String theory compactifications (Calabi-Yau moduli spaces can be tuned to match observed mass ratios)
- Lattice QCD studies (used to verify mass relations computed from first principles in QCD)

The paper is cited not only in theoretical physics but also in mathematical physics (group theory), metrology (defining reference masses), and educational treatments of the Standard Model.
