## Abstract

MacGregor presents a comprehensive phenomenological analysis of elementary particle masses
and lifetimes, arguing that both are quantized in units related to the fine structure
constant $\alpha \approx 1/137$. The central claim is that particle masses can be expressed
as integer multiples of two fundamental mass quanta: 70 MeV ($= m_e/\alpha \approx 70$ MeV)
and 105 MeV ($= 3m_e/(2\alpha) = m_\mu$, the muon mass). This alpha-quantization extends
to particle lifetimes, which cluster at values separated by factors of $\alpha$.

---

## Historical Context

Malcolm H. MacGregor (1927-2013) spent decades at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory
working on particle physics phenomenology. His analysis of mass and lifetime patterns,
developed over a 30-year period, culminated in this 2007 book.

MacGregor's work sits in the same tradition as Nambu (1952), Muraki (1978), and Paasch (2009):
empirical pattern-hunting in the particle mass spectrum. But MacGregor brought two distinctive
contributions:

1. **The $\alpha$-quantization hypothesis**: Rather than arbitrary integer labels, MacGregor
   identified the fine structure constant $\alpha$ as the organizing principle. Mass intervals
   are multiples of $m_e/\alpha \approx 70$ MeV.

2. **Lifetime quantization**: MacGregor extended the analysis to particle LIFETIMES, showing
   that they cluster at values separated by factors of $\alpha \approx 1/137$. This
   connects mass and lifetime patterns through a single constant.

The book represents the most comprehensive modern compilation of empirical mass quantization
patterns, making it an essential reference for evaluating Paasch's claims.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### The 70 MeV Mass Quantum

The fundamental observation is that many particle mass DIFFERENCES are approximately integer
multiples of 70 MeV:

$$m_0 = \frac{m_e}{\alpha} = \frac{0.511 \text{ MeV}}{1/137.036} \approx 70.0 \text{ MeV}$$

Examples:

| Mass difference | Value (MeV) | $n \times 70$ | $n$ |
|----------------|-------------|---------------|-----|
| $m_\pi - 0$ | 140 | 140 | 2 |
| $m_K - m_\pi$ | 354 | 350 | 5 |
| $m_p - m_K$ | 444 | 420 | 6 |
| $m_\Delta - m_p$ | 294 | 280 | 4 |
| $m_\Sigma - m_\Lambda$ | 77 | 70 | 1 |

The typical agreement is within 5-10%, which is suggestive but not precision physics.

### The 105 MeV Mass Quantum

MacGregor also identifies a secondary mass quantum at 105 MeV:

$$m_\mu = \frac{3m_e}{2\alpha} = \frac{3}{2} \times 70 = 105 \text{ MeV}$$

This is of course the muon mass (Nambu's relation $m_\mu/m_e \approx 3/(2\alpha)$). Some
mass intervals are better fit by multiples of 105 MeV than 70 MeV, suggesting a two-quantum
system.

### The Alpha-Quantized Lifetime Grid

MacGregor's most original contribution is the LIFETIME analysis. He observes that unstable
particle lifetimes cluster at values separated by factors of $\alpha$:

$$\tau_n \sim \tau_0 \cdot \alpha^n$$

where $n$ is an integer and $\tau_0$ is a reference lifetime. The factor of $\alpha$ between
successive lifetime levels corresponds to successive orders of electromagnetic interaction
(each factor of $\alpha$ represents one additional photon vertex in the decay diagram).

Examples of the lifetime grid:

| Level $n$ | $\tau$ (s) | Example particle(s) |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| 0 | $\sim 10^{-23}$ | Strong resonances ($\Delta$, $\rho$, etc.) |
| 1 | $\sim 10^{-20}$ | EM decays ($\pi^0 \to \gamma\gamma$) |
| 2 | $\sim 10^{-17}$ | Weak+EM ($\Sigma^0 \to \Lambda\gamma$) |
| 4 | $\sim 10^{-13}$ | Weak decays ($K_S$, $\Lambda$, $\Xi$) |
| 6 | $\sim 10^{-10}$ | Weak decays ($K_L$, $K^\pm$, hyperon) |
| 8 | $\sim 10^{-8}$ | Weak decays ($\pi^\pm$, $\mu$) |

The factor of $\alpha \approx 1/137$ gives approximately $\log_{10}(\alpha) \approx -2.14$,
so each $\alpha$ step corresponds to ~2 orders of magnitude in lifetime. The observed
clustering at intervals of ~2 decades is well-known empirically.

### The Relativistically Spinning Sphere

MacGregor proposes a physical model underlying the mass quantization: the electron as a
relativistically spinning sphere. In this model:
- The electron has a finite size (the classical electron radius $r_e = \alpha \cdot \hbar/(m_e c)$)
- It spins at the speed of light at its equator
- Other particles are EXCITED STATES of this spinning electron
- Mass quanta of $m_e/\alpha$ correspond to additional rotational excitations
- The factor of $\alpha$ connects the size scale ($r_e$) to the Compton wavelength

This physical picture is NOT accepted by mainstream physics (the electron is treated as a
point particle in the Standard Model), but MacGregor argues it provides an intuitive
explanation for the alpha-quantized mass and lifetime patterns.

### Particle-Hole Pair Mechanism

MacGregor proposes that heavier particles are created through particle-hole pair production
in the vacuum. Each pair adds a mass quantum of $\sim 70$ MeV, and the number of pairs
determines the particle's mass number.

This is reminiscent of:
- Paasch's mass numbers (integer labels determining mass)
- BCS theory (particle-hole pairing in superconductors)
- The BdG class DIII identification from Session 11 (SM spectral triple as topological
  superconductor in internal space)

## Key Results

1. Elementary particle masses are approximately quantized in units of $m_e/\alpha \approx 70$ MeV
2. A secondary quantum of $3m_e/(2\alpha) = 105$ MeV (= muon mass) also appears
3. Particle lifetimes cluster at values separated by factors of $\alpha$
4. The alpha-quantization connects mass and lifetime patterns through a single constant
5. Typical mass-interval agreements are 5-10% (suggestive but not precision)
6. Lifetime clustering is more robust than mass quantization
7. The relativistically spinning sphere model provides a physical picture but is not
   mainstream
8. The 70 MeV quantum is $m_e/\alpha$ -- the electron mass enhanced by the electromagnetic
   coupling

## Impact and Legacy

MacGregor's book is the most comprehensive compilation of empirical mass quantization
patterns available. Its impact includes:

**Positive:**
- Systematic, data-driven analysis of ALL known particle masses and lifetimes
- The lifetime grid ($\tau \propto \alpha^n$) is a genuine empirical pattern with a
  natural explanation (successive electromagnetic vertices)
- Established 70 MeV as a phenomenological mass quantum
- Connected mass and lifetime patterns through a single constant ($\alpha$)

**Negative:**
- The relativistically spinning sphere model is outside the mainstream
- Mass-interval fits at 5-10% are not conclusive
- The $m_e/\alpha$ mass quantum has no derivation from the Standard Model
- The pattern becomes less convincing when examining INDIVIDUAL particles rather than
  mass DIFFERENCES
- No prediction of NEW particles or their masses

**Relationship to similar work:**
- Nambu (1952): Pioneer of the same approach ($m_\mu/m_e \approx 3/(2\alpha)$)
- Muraki (1978): Logarithmic mass formula with integer quantum numbers
- Paasch (2009): Mass numbers on a spiral with golden ratio structure
- MacGregor: Most systematic compilation, connecting masses AND lifetimes via $\alpha$

## Relevance to Paasch Framework

MacGregor's work is directly relevant to evaluating Paasch's mass number scheme:

1. **Same tradition**: Both Paasch and MacGregor seek integer patterns in the mass spectrum.
   MacGregor uses $\alpha$ as the organizing constant; Paasch uses $\phi_P$ (= 1.53158, from $x = e^{-x^2}$; NOT the golden ratio).
   These are DIFFERENT claims about what constant organizes the spectrum.

2. **70 MeV = $m_e/\alpha$**: MacGregor's fundamental mass quantum is Nambu's
   $m_e/\alpha \approx 70$ MeV. Paasch's reference mass is different (related to $m_e$
   and phi_paasch through the mass number scheme). If both are correct, they must be COMPATIBLE,
   which constrains the relationship between $\alpha$ and phi_paasch.

3. **Lifetime grid**: MacGregor's $\tau \propto \alpha^n$ lifetime grid is an INDEPENDENT
   pattern that Paasch's framework should either reproduce or explain. The lifetime grid
   has a more natural Standard Model explanation (perturbative expansion in $\alpha$) than
   the mass quantization.

4. **Complementary or competing**: MacGregor and Paasch may be seeing the SAME underlying
   pattern from different angles (alpha-quantization vs. phi_paasch-quantization), or they may be
   fitting different coincidences. A decisive test would be to check whether the Dirac
   spectrum eigenvalues on deformed SU(3) reproduce BOTH patterns (alpha-quantization and phi_paasch-quantization) simultaneously.
