# The Symmetry Approach to Quark and Lepton Masses and Mixing

**Author(s):** Gui-Jun Ding and Jose W.F. Valle

**Year:** 2025

**Journal:** Physics Reports, vol. 1091, pp. 1-149 (arXiv:2402.16963)

---

## Abstract

The Standard Model provides no organizing principle for the flavor sector: why do leptons and quarks appear in three nearly identical families? Why is the quark mixing pattern (CKM) so different from the lepton pattern (PMNS)? And why do fermion masses span seven orders of magnitude? This comprehensive review examines the symmetry approach to flavor, in which discrete family symmetries (A₄, S₄, etc.) and CP symmetries act as organizing principles. The authors present seesaw and scotogenic neutrino mass mechanisms, evaluate existing mixing patterns, and show how family symmetries can generate novel, predictive mixing patterns including trimaximal (TM1, TM2) and bimaximal (BM) textures. The review covers model-independent predictions, higher-dimensional completions with warped geometry, and modular symmetries as a recent frontier in flavor physics.

---

## Historical Context

The flavor problem has plagued the Standard Model since its inception. Gell-Mann and Goldstone noted in 1965 that "the family structure is not understood" — a statement that remains largely true 60 years later.

**Early approaches** (1980s-1990s): Froggatt-Nielsen mechanism, horizontal symmetries, family symmetry ansatz.

**Modern phase** (2000s-2010s): Discrete family symmetries (particularly A₄, S₄) emerged as a framework for predicting specific CKM and PMNS patterns. The discovery of θ₁₃ ≈ 9° in lepton mixing broke the early tribimaximal assumption but reinvigorated discrete symmetry model-building.

**Current landscape**: Modular symmetries (inspired by string theory modular forms), CP symmetries, and extra-dimensional approaches (orbifolds, warped spaces) extend the scope. Neutrino oscillations and cosmological constraints now provide precision tests of flavor predictions.

This review represents the state-of-the-art synthesis of the discrete symmetry program by two of its leading architects.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### Family Symmetries and Representation Theory

A discrete family symmetry G acts on the three generations as a triplet or irreducible representation of G. For the group A₄, the simplest faithful triplet $\mathbf{3}$ decomposes under subgroups into smaller representations. The key insight is that **subgroup alignment** (which subgroup is conserved at low energy) determines the mixing pattern.

For A₄, the residual discrete symmetry in the charged lepton sector is typically Z₃ (generated by elements like $(1, \omega, \omega^2)$ where $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$), while the neutrino mass matrix retains a different residual symmetry (e.g., Z₂). This mismatch between the two sectors is the source of mixing.

The PMNS matrix is constructed as:

$$U_{\text{PMNS}} = U_e^\dagger U_\nu$$

where $U_e$ and $U_\nu$ are the unitary rotations diagonalizing the charged lepton and neutrino mass matrices. The residual symmetries constrain these unitary matrices, yielding specific predictions for $\theta_{12}$, $\theta_{23}$, $\theta_{13}$, and the CP-violating phase δ.

### Trimaximal Mixing (TM2)

One successful class of predictions is trimaximal mixing, exemplified by TM2:

$$|U_{\text{PMNS}}|_{\text{TM2}} = \begin{pmatrix}
\sqrt{2/3} & \sqrt{1/3} & 0 \\
\sqrt{1/6} & \sqrt{1/3} & \sqrt{1/2} \\
\sqrt{1/6} & \sqrt{1/3} & -\sqrt{1/2}
\end{pmatrix}$$

This predicts $\sin^2 \theta_{12} = 1/3$, $\sin^2 \theta_{23} = 1/2$ exactly, and $\theta_{13} = 0$ at tree level. NuFIT 5.2 (2023) gives $\sin^2 \theta_{12} = 0.310 \pm 0.013$ and $\sin^2 \theta_{23} = 0.560 \pm 0.020$, showing that TM2 is at approximately 2σ tension with data. One-loop corrections can partially alleviate this tension.

### CP-Symmetric Corrections

Neutrino mixing can receive corrections from CP symmetry acting as an independent discrete constraint. The combination of family symmetry (G) and CP acts as $G \rtimes \mathbb{Z}_2^{CP}$. The CP action is encoded as:

$$\rho_\text{CP}(C): \nu_L \to C \overline{\nu_L}^T$$

where C is a unitary matrix in flavor space. The interplay between G and CP can predict CP-violating phases, including the Dirac phase δ_CP in PMNS.

### Seesaw and Scotogenic Mechanisms

The paper distinguishes two primary neutrino mass generation mechanisms:

**Type-I Seesaw** (heaviest right-handed neutrino mass $M_R \sim 10^{12-14}$ GeV):
$$m_\nu \sim \frac{v^2}{M_R} \sim 0.1 \text{ eV}$$

**Scotogenic** (dark-matter-mediated, new Higgs singlets and fermions at TeV scale):
$$m_\nu = \frac{\lambda^2 v^2}{M_\text{dark}}$$

where dark matter particles form a loop connecting neutrino pairs. The scotogenic approach is increasingly favored because it can simultaneously explain dark matter and neutrino masses, testable at colliders.

### Model-Independent Mass Sum Rules

Family symmetries can generate mass sum rules, e.g., for A₄ with specific alignment:

$$m_1 + m_2 = 2 m_3 \quad \text{(example)}$$

or relationships among Yukawa eigenvalues. These are testable in future neutrino experiments and beta-decay searches.

## Key Results

1. **Trimaximal Mixing (TM2)**: Predicts $\sin^2 \theta_{12} = 1/3$ exactly. Current data favor $1/3 \approx 0.333$ vs. observed $0.310$, a 2σ discrepancy manageable by radiative corrections.

2. **Bimaximal Mixing (BM)**: $\sin^2 \theta_{12} = 1/2$. Excluded at 2σ by oscillation data.

3. **S₄ + CP Symmetry**: The S₄ group with an external CP symmetry (not a subgroup element) can predict mixing angles and CP phases simultaneously, with fewer free parameters than generic models.

4. **Warped Extra Dimensions + T′ Symmetry**: An orbifolded warped geometry with custodial symmetries and the T′ group predicts TM1 (golden ratio mixed) patterns with natural Higgs protection.

5. **Modular Symmetry (Γ₃ ≈ PSL(2,Z))**: Recent approaches using modular forms predict mixing angles and mass ratios as functions of a single modular parameter τ (related to the complex structure of extra dimensions). Predictions include correlations between CKM and PMNS, testable in precision flavor experiments.

6. **Quark-Lepton Complementarity**: Certain models predict $\theta_{12}^{CKM} + \theta_{12}^{PMNS} = π/4$, i.e., approximately 45°. Data support this to within 2σ.

## Impact and Legacy

This review became the standard reference for discrete family symmetry approaches in the 2020s. It unified previously scattered results across A₄, S₄, and other groups, provided a coherent pedagogical treatment, and elevated modular symmetries from curiosity to mainstream consideration. The work has influenced hundreds of subsequent papers proposing extensions and applications.

The review also crystallized a paradigm shift: rather than asking "why are there three families?" (a question without known answer), the symmetry approach asks "**what discrete structure naturally generates the observed mixing pattern?**" This reframing has proven more tractable for model-building and phenomenology.

## Connection to Phonon-Exflation Framework

**Critical relevance**: The Paasch relations (papers #01-18) are empirical mass ratio relations (e.g., $m_\mu / m_e = 207$, Koide formula). This review examines whether such ratios arise from symmetry-based mass mechanisms or are accidental.

In the phonon-exflation framework, particle masses emerge from SU(3) Dirac spectrum gaps (the fold at τ ≈ 0.2). Those gaps carry specific quantum numbers under the residual gauge and global symmetries. The question is: **do the gap values exhibit accidental discrete symmetries that predict mass ratios?**

For instance, if the phonon-exflation mechanism produces a Dirac spectrum with A₄-like symmetry among the gap eigenvalues, then Ding-Valle's predictions (TM2, sum rules, etc.) might apply to the lepton sector masses directly.

**Application strategy**: Compare phonon-exflation's predicted mass spectrum against discrete family symmetry predictions. If alignment is found, this would demonstrate that the fold mechanism spontaneously breaks a family symmetry, explaining both flavor and mass hierarchy simultaneously.
