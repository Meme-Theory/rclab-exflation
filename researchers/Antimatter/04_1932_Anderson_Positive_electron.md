# The Positive Electron (1932)

**Author**: Carl David Anderson
**Published**: Science, Vol. 76, No. 1967, pp. 238-239 (9 September 1932); Physical Review 43, 491 (1933)
**Nobel Prize**: 1936 (shared with Victor Hess)

## Abstract

While studying cosmic ray showers in a cloud chamber immersed in a strong magnetic field, Anderson observes tracks that can only be explained by a positively charged particle with the mass of an electron — the first experimental detection of antimatter.

## Experimental Apparatus

### Cloud Chamber Design
- **Diameter**: 16 cm, thickness 4 cm
- **Medium**: Water-alcohol vapor mixture with rapid expansion piston
- **Magnetic field**: Up to 2.4 Tesla from a DC electromagnet (600 kW power)
- **Lead plate**: 6 mm thick, placed horizontally across the chamber center

### The Role of the Lead Plate
The lead plate was Anderson's key innovation for determining particle direction. When a charged particle passes through lead:
- It loses energy through ionization and bremsstrahlung
- Its momentum decreases
- In a magnetic field, lower momentum → smaller radius of curvature
- Therefore, the track is MORE curved on the exit side
- This unambiguously determines the direction of travel

### Triggering
The cloud chamber was triggered by Geiger-Müller counters placed above and below, responding to cosmic ray particles.

## The Historic Observation

### Date: August 2, 1932 (evening)

Anderson photographed a track that showed:
1. A particle entering from below (less curved side → higher energy)
2. Passing through the lead plate
3. Exiting above with increased curvature (lower energy after lead)
4. Curvature direction indicating **positive charge**
5. Energy: 63 MeV before the lead plate
6. Track ionization density consistent with electron mass (NOT proton mass)

### Elimination of Alternatives

| Hypothesis | Why Rejected |
|-----------|-------------|
| Proton | A proton of this curvature would have only ~300 MeV energy and would stop in the lead plate (range too short). The track clearly continues through. |
| Upward-moving electron | Direction determined by lead plate energy loss — particle definitely moves upward, so curvature indicates positive charge |
| Two coincident particles | Statistically negligible probability; single continuous track with consistent ionization |

### Statistics
Out of approximately 1,300 cosmic ray photographs:
- 15 tracks showed positively charged particles with electron mass
- These could not be explained by any known particle

## Publication and Reception

Anderson published a brief announcement in Science (September 1932), followed by a detailed paper in Physical Review (1933). He coined the term **"positron"** for the positive electron.

### Independent Confirmation
- **Blackett and Occhialini** (1933): Observed electron-positron pairs produced simultaneously by cosmic rays, providing clear evidence for pair production (γ → e⁻e⁺). Their counter-triggered cloud chamber work was more systematic and directly confirmed Dirac's pair creation mechanism.
- **Joliot-Curie** (1933): Also observed positron tracks independently.

## Significance

### First Predicted Particle
The positron was the FIRST fundamental particle predicted theoretically (by Dirac, 1930-31) before experimental discovery. This established a pattern that would repeat with:
- The neutrino (predicted 1930, detected 1956)
- The antiproton (predicted 1932, detected 1955)
- The W and Z bosons (predicted ~1960s, detected 1983)
- The Higgs boson (predicted 1964, detected 2012)

### Validation of the Dirac Equation
The positron's properties exactly matched Dirac's predictions:
- Same mass as the electron: mₑ = 0.511 MeV/c²
- Opposite charge: +e
- Same spin: 1/2
- Same magnetic moment (magnitude)

### Birth of Antimatter Physics
Anderson's discovery opened the era of antimatter research and established that every particle has an antiparticle — one of the deepest symmetries of nature.
