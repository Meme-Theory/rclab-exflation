# Quantum Electrodynamics. I. A Covariant Formulation

**Author:** Julian Schwinger
**Year:** 1948
**Journal:** *Physical Review*, 74(10), 1439--1461

---

## Abstract

Schwinger presents the first systematically covariant formulation of quantum electrodynamics using the operator (canonical) approach, demonstrating that the infinities of QED can be isolated, interpreted, and removed through a consistent renormalization procedure. Unlike Feynman's spacetime/diagrammatic approach, Schwinger works entirely within the Hamiltonian framework, developing the interaction representation (Tomonaga-Schwinger picture) and introducing powerful computational tools: proper-time parametrizations, Green's function methods, and the effective action. The paper derives the anomalous magnetic moment of the electron ($a_e = \alpha/2\pi$) and the Lamb shift from first principles, establishing that QED is a predictive theory despite its perturbative infinities. Schwinger's approach, while technically more demanding than Feynman's, provides a more rigorous foundation for the renormalization program and directly leads to the effective action formalism central to modern quantum field theory.

---

## Historical Context

### The Operator Tradition

Schwinger's approach grows from the canonical quantization tradition of Dirac, Heisenberg, and Pauli. In this framework, fields are promoted to operators satisfying canonical commutation (or anti-commutation) relations, and physical quantities are matrix elements of these operators between states. Time evolution is generated by the Hamiltonian.

The advantage of this approach is mathematical rigor: every step can (in principle) be justified within the axioms of quantum mechanics. The disadvantage is computational complexity: tracking all the operator orderings, commutators, and normal-ordering prescriptions is extremely laborious.

### The Tomonaga-Schwinger Equation

The key technical innovation is the interaction representation, developed independently by Tomonaga (1943) and Schwinger (1948). In this picture, the field operators evolve according to the free Hamiltonian $H_0$, while the states evolve according to the interaction Hamiltonian $H_{\text{int}}$:

$$i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}|\psi_I(t)\rangle = H_{\text{int}}^I(t)|\psi_I(t)\rangle$$

where $H_{\text{int}}^I(t) = e^{iH_0 t/\hbar}H_{\text{int}}e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar}$.

Tomonaga and Schwinger generalized this to a covariant formulation by replacing the time parameter with a spacelike surface $\sigma$:

$$i\hbar c \frac{\delta |\psi[\sigma]\rangle}{\delta\sigma(x)} = \mathcal{H}_{\text{int}}(x)|\psi[\sigma]\rangle$$

This equation is manifestly Lorentz covariant: the state depends on the entire spacelike surface $\sigma$, not just on a particular time. The integrability condition (consistency of the equation for arbitrary deformations of $\sigma$) requires:

$$[\mathcal{H}_{\text{int}}(x), \mathcal{H}_{\text{int}}(x')] = 0 \quad \text{for spacelike } (x - x')$$

which is automatically satisfied for local interactions.

### The Shelter Island Conference

At the Shelter Island Conference (June 1947), the experimental results that drove the renormalization program were announced: the Lamb shift (Lamb and Retherford) and the anomalous magnetic moment of the electron (Kusch and Foley). Bethe's non-relativistic estimate of the Lamb shift showed that the infinities could be controlled by subtraction, but a fully relativistic, covariant treatment was needed. Schwinger set to work immediately and had results within months.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### 1. The S-Matrix in the Interaction Representation

The S-matrix connecting initial and final states is:

$$S = T\exp\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} H_{\text{int}}^I(t) \, dt\right) = T\exp\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar c}\int \mathcal{H}_{\text{int}}(x) \, d^4x\right)$$

where $T$ denotes time ordering and $\mathcal{H}_{\text{int}}(x) = -e\bar{\psi}(x)\gamma^\mu\psi(x)A_\mu(x)$ is the QED interaction Hamiltonian density.

Expanding the time-ordered exponential:

$$S = 1 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-i)^n}{n!\hbar^n c^n}\int \cdots \int T[\mathcal{H}_{\text{int}}(x_1)\cdots\mathcal{H}_{\text{int}}(x_n)] \, d^4x_1 \cdots d^4x_n$$

### 2. Wick's Theorem and Contractions

To evaluate the time-ordered products, Schwinger develops the technology of contracting field operators (later systematized by Wick in 1950). A contraction of two field operators is defined as:

$$\overline{\psi(x)\bar{\psi}(y)} = T[\psi(x)\bar{\psi}(y)] - :\psi(x)\bar{\psi}(y): = iS_F(x - y)$$

$$\overline{A_\mu(x)A_\nu(y)} = T[A_\mu(x)A_\nu(y)] - :A_\mu(x)A_\nu(y): = iD_F^{\mu\nu}(x - y)$$

where $S_F$ is the electron Feynman propagator and $D_F^{\mu\nu}$ is the photon Feynman propagator. The normal ordering $:\cdot:$ ensures zero vacuum expectation value.

Wick's theorem expresses the time-ordered product as a sum over all possible contractions:

$$T[\phi_1\phi_2\cdots\phi_n] = :\phi_1\phi_2\cdots\phi_n: + \text{all single contractions} + \text{all double contractions} + \cdots$$

Each contraction pattern corresponds to a Feynman diagram, but Schwinger arrives at the same result through operator algebra rather than spacetime pictures.

### 3. The Electron Self-Energy

The second-order correction to the electron propagator involves the self-energy operator $\Sigma(p)$. In Schwinger's approach, this is computed as a vacuum expectation value:

$$\langle 0 | T[\psi(x)\bar{\psi}(y)] | 0 \rangle^{(2)} = \int d^4z_1 \, d^4z_2 \, S_F(x - z_1)(-ie\gamma^\mu) S_F(z_1 - z_2)(-ie\gamma^\nu) S_F(z_2 - y) D_{F\mu\nu}(z_1 - z_2)$$

In momentum space:

$$\Sigma(p) = -ie^2\int \frac{d^4k}{(2\pi)^4} \gamma^\mu S_F(p - k)\gamma_\mu D_F(k)$$

### 4. The Proper-Time Method

Schwinger's most powerful computational innovation is the proper-time representation. The propagator denominator is written as:

$$\frac{1}{p^2 - m^2 + i\epsilon} = -i\int_0^\infty ds \, e^{is(p^2 - m^2 + i\epsilon)}$$

where $s$ is the "proper time" parameter. This representation allows the momentum integral to be performed as a Gaussian, leaving a single proper-time integral that isolates the divergences.

For the electron in an external electromagnetic field, the propagator satisfies:

$$(\not{D} + m)G(x,y) = \delta^4(x - y), \quad D_\mu = \partial_\mu + ieA_\mu$$

Schwinger writes the proper-time representation:

$$G(x,y) = -i(\not{D}_x + m)\int_0^\infty ds \, e^{-ism^2}\langle x | e^{is(\not{D})^2} | y \rangle$$

where:

$$(\not{D})^2 = D^\mu D_\mu + \frac{e}{2}\sigma^{\mu\nu}F_{\mu\nu}$$

The matrix element $\langle x | e^{is(\not{D})^2} | y \rangle$ is a quantum-mechanical transition amplitude in a fictitious one-dimensional quantum system with "time" parameter $s$. This is the Schwinger proper-time formalism, which reduces QFT loop calculations to quantum-mechanical path integrals.

### 5. The Anomalous Magnetic Moment

Schwinger's most celebrated result is the computation of the electron's anomalous magnetic moment. He considers the vertex function $\Gamma^\mu(p',p)$ for an electron interacting with an external electromagnetic field and extracts the part proportional to $\sigma^{\mu\nu}q_\nu$ (with $q = p' - p$):

$$\Gamma^\mu(p', p) = \gamma^\mu F_1(q^2) + \frac{i\sigma^{\mu\nu}q_\nu}{2m}F_2(q^2)$$

At one loop, using the proper-time representation and careful handling of the Dirac algebra:

$$F_2(0) = \frac{e^2}{8\pi^2}\int_0^1 dz \, \frac{2m^2 z(1-z)}{m^2(1-z)^2} = \frac{e^2}{8\pi^2}\int_0^1 \frac{2z}{1-z} dz$$

After regularization (the integral requires careful treatment of the $z \to 1$ endpoint):

$$F_2(0) = \frac{\alpha}{2\pi}$$

The magnetic moment of the electron is:

$$\mu = \frac{e}{2m}\left(1 + \frac{\alpha}{2\pi}\right) = \frac{e}{2m}(1.001\,161\,4\ldots)$$

Schwinger announced this result at the APS meeting in January 1948, and it was immediately recognized as a triumph.

### 6. Vacuum Polarization and the Effective Action

Schwinger introduces the effective action $\Gamma[A]$, defined as the quantum-corrected action for the electromagnetic field after integrating out the electron:

$$e^{i\Gamma[A]/\hbar} = \int \mathcal{D}[\bar{\psi}]\mathcal{D}[\psi] \, e^{iS[\psi, \bar{\psi}, A]/\hbar}$$

The one-loop effective action (Euler-Heisenberg effective action) is:

$$\Gamma^{(1)}[A] = -i\hbar \ln \det(\not{D} + m) = -i\hbar \text{Tr}\ln(\not{D} + m)$$

Using the proper-time representation:

$$\Gamma^{(1)}[A] = i\hbar \int_0^\infty \frac{ds}{s} e^{-ism^2} \text{Tr}\langle x | e^{is(\not{D})^2} | x \rangle$$

For a constant electromagnetic field $F_{\mu\nu}$, Schwinger evaluates this trace exactly, obtaining the Euler-Heisenberg Lagrangian:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{EH}} = -\frac{1}{4}F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu} + \frac{2\alpha^2}{45m^4}\left[(F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu})^2 + \frac{7}{4}(F_{\mu\nu}\tilde{F}^{\mu\nu})^2\right] + \cdots$$

where $\tilde{F}^{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}F_{\rho\sigma}$ is the dual field tensor. This predicts light-by-light scattering and vacuum birefringence (photon splitting in a strong magnetic field).

### 7. Renormalization in the Operator Formalism

Schwinger's renormalization procedure is more systematic than Feynman's. He introduces counterterms directly into the Lagrangian:

$$\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_0 + \delta m \bar{\psi}\psi + \frac{\delta Z_2}{2}\bar{\psi}(i\not{\partial})\psi + \frac{\delta Z_3}{4}F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu} + \delta Z_1 e\bar{\psi}\gamma^\mu\psi A_\mu$$

The counterterms $\delta m$, $\delta Z_1$, $\delta Z_2$, $\delta Z_3$ are fixed by renormalization conditions: the physical mass is $m$, the physical charge is $e$, and the propagator residue is 1. The Ward-Takahashi identity ensures $\delta Z_1 = \delta Z_2$, so only three independent renormalizations are needed.

## Physical Interpretation

### The Operator vs. Spacetime Pictures

Schwinger's operator approach and Feynman's spacetime approach are complementary:

| Feature | Schwinger | Feynman |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Language | Operators, commutators, states | Paths, diagrams, amplitudes |
| Gauge invariance | Manifest (Ward identity derived) | Must be checked diagram by diagram |
| Computation | Systematic but laborious | Efficient for individual diagrams |
| Generalization | Natural for effective actions | Natural for scattering amplitudes |
| Physical intuition | Field-theoretic | Particle-trajectory |

### The Effective Action

Schwinger's effective action $\Gamma[A]$ is arguably his most lasting technical contribution. It encodes all quantum corrections to the classical action: the extremum of $\Gamma$ gives the quantum-corrected equations of motion, and the second variation gives the exact propagator (including all loop corrections). The effective action formalism is the standard framework for:
- The Higgs mechanism (the effective potential determines spontaneous symmetry breaking)
- Non-perturbative physics (instantons are saddle points of the Euclidean effective action)
- Anomalies (the chiral anomaly appears as a non-invariance of $\Gamma$ under gauge transformations)

## Impact and Legacy

### Renormalization Theory

Schwinger's systematic approach to renormalization, combined with Dyson's proof of equivalence and Dyson's proof of renormalizability to all orders, established the modern renormalization program. This was extended to non-Abelian gauge theories by 't Hooft and Veltman (1971-72) and to effective field theories by Weinberg (1979).

### The Schwinger Effect

The Euler-Heisenberg effective Lagrangian predicts pair creation in a strong electric field (Schwinger effect):

$$\Gamma_{\text{pair}} = \frac{\alpha E^2}{\pi^2}\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2}e^{-n\pi m^2/(eE)} \approx \frac{\alpha E^2}{\pi^2}e^{-\pi E_c/E}$$

where $E_c = m^2c^3/(e\hbar) \approx 1.3 \times 10^{18}$ V/m is the critical Schwinger field. This is a non-perturbative effect (not visible in any finite order of perturbation theory) that has connections to Hawking radiation and the Unruh effect.

### Source Theory

In the 1960s and 70s, Schwinger developed "source theory," an alternative to the path integral formalism that emphasized external sources as the fundamental objects. While less widely adopted than the path integral, source theory influenced the development of the generating functional approach and the Schwinger-Keldysh formalism for non-equilibrium quantum field theory.

## Connections to Modern Physics

1. **Schwinger-Dyson equations:** The exact equations of motion for Green's functions, derived from Schwinger's variational approach, remain central to non-perturbative QFT and condensed matter physics.

2. **Schwinger pair production:** Near-critical electric fields are being approached by ultra-intense laser facilities (ELI, XCELS). Detection of the Schwinger effect would confirm a 75-year-old prediction.

3. **Proper-time and strings:** Schwinger's proper-time representation $\int_0^\infty ds/s \, e^{-sm^2}$ for the one-loop effective action is the point-particle limit of the string theory one-loop amplitude $\int d\tau/\tau \, Z(\tau)$, where $\tau$ is the modular parameter of the torus and $Z$ is the string partition function.

4. **Anomalies:** The chiral anomaly, first identified by Adler and Bell-Jackiw (1969), can be elegantly derived using Schwinger's proper-time method. The anomaly appears as a non-cancellation of the regulator in the proper-time integral.

5. **Schwinger-Keldysh formalism:** The closed-time-path formalism for out-of-equilibrium quantum systems, developed from Schwinger's functional methods, is essential for early-universe cosmology, heavy-ion collisions, and quantum kinetic theory.

6. **Functional renormalization group:** The Wetterich effective action (an exact renormalization group equation for the flowing effective action $\Gamma_k$) is a direct descendant of Schwinger's effective action formalism, providing the framework for asymptotic safety and non-perturbative studies of gauge theories.
