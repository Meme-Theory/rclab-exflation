# Consistently Large Cosmic Flows on Scales of 100 Mpc/h: A Challenge for the Standard ΛCDM Cosmology

**Author(s):** Richard Watkins, Hume A. Feldman, Michael J. Hudson

**Year:** 2009 (first version); 2010 (updated version in MNRAS)

**Journal:** Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society, Volume 392-407, Pages 743-756 and 2328-2338

---

## Abstract

Using peculiar velocity data from galaxy surveys, Watkins et al. measure the bulk flow of matter at 100 Mpc/h scale and find it to be surprisingly large: significantly larger than predicted by ΛCDM simulations. The bulk flow is a measure of the large-scale motion of all matter in a region, reflecting the asymmetric distribution of matter and the resulting gravitational force. A large bulk flow at large scales would suggest either anomalous large-scale structure (e.g., the Shapley Concentration or other massive nearby structures) or deviations from ΛCDM.

---

## Historical Context

Peculiar velocities (deviations from the Hubble flow) can be measured from galaxy distances and redshifts, revealing the gravitational forces at work. By 2009, surveys of nearby galaxies (within z ~ 0.1) had provided enough data to measure bulk flows reliably. Watkins et al.'s finding that bulk flows exceed ΛCDM predictions sparked debate about whether the anomaly reflects real structure or observational/methodological issues.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### Peculiar Velocity and Bulk Flow

A galaxy's **peculiar velocity** is its velocity relative to the Hubble flow:

$$\mathbf{v}_{\text{pec}} = \mathbf{v}_{\text{obs}} - H_0 \mathbf{d}$$

where $\mathbf{v}_{\text{obs}}$ is the observed velocity (from redshift) and $\mathbf{d}$ is the distance.

The **bulk flow** of a region is the mean peculiar velocity in that region:

$$\mathbf{V}_{\text{bulk}} = \left\langle \mathbf{v}_{\text{pec}} \right\rangle$$

For a sphere of radius $R$ centered on the observer, the magnitude of the bulk flow is:

$$V_{\text{bulk}} = \left| \frac{1}{N} \sum_i \mathbf{v}_{\text{pec}, i} \right|$$

### Measurement Method: Minimum Variance Bulk Flow

Watkins et al. developed an optimal estimator for bulk flow that accounts for the non-uniform survey geometry and sparse sampling. The **minimum variance bulk flow** minimizes the variance of the estimator:

$$\mathbf{V}_{\text{MV}} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{V}} \sum_i w_i (\mathbf{v}_i - \mathbf{V})^2$$

where $w_i$ are weights that account for distance uncertainty and survey selection effects.

### Comparison with ΛCDM Predictions

N-body simulations of ΛCDM produce bulk flows due to the density contrast from large-scale structure. Theoretical predictions for bulk flow amplitudes (RMS over many realizations):

$$V_{\text{bulk}}^{\text{ΛCDM}}(R = 100 \text{ Mpc/h}) \sim 200-250 \text{ km/s}$$

Watkins et al.'s measurement:

$$V_{\text{bulk}}^{\text{obs}}(R = 100 \text{ Mpc/h}) \sim 400-600 \text{ km/s}$$

approximately **2-3 times larger** than ΛCDM predictions. This discrepancy is the key result.

### Dipole Analysis

The bulk flow can be analyzed in terms of the **dipole** of the density field:

$$\mathbf{d}_1 = \int \frac{\mathbf{x}}{x^3} \delta(\mathbf{x}) d^3\mathbf{x}$$

which relates to the bulk flow via:

$$\mathbf{V}_{\text{bulk}} \propto \mathbf{d}_1$$

A large dipole indicates asymmetric mass distribution—more matter in one direction than another.

## Key Results

1. **Large bulk flows observed**: Bulk flows at 100 Mpc/h scale are 2-3 times larger than ΛCDM predictions.

2. **Persistent across multiple surveys**: The large bulk flow is robust across different galaxy catalogs and distance measurements.

3. **Dipole anomaly**: The low-order dipole moment of the density distribution is anomalously large, indicating asymmetric structure (possibly the Shapley Concentration or other massive distant structures).

4. **Kinetic energy imbalance**: The bulk kinetic energy exceeds what ΛCDM simulations predict should be present.
