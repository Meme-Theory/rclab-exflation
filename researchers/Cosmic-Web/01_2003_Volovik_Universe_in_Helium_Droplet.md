# The Universe in a Helium Droplet

**Author(s):** Grigory E. Volovik

**Year:** 2003

**Journal:** Oxford University Press (Clarendon Press), International Series of Monographs on Physics

---

## Abstract

This monograph presents a revolutionary perspective on fundamental physics: gravity, spacetime, and the quantum vacuum emerge from the low-energy corner of an underlying condensed matter system. Using superfluid helium-3 as a physical analog, Volovik demonstrates how relativistic quantum field theory, Lorentz symmetry, local gauge invariance, and general relativity arise naturally as collective phenomena in a superfluid vacuum. The work bridges condensed matter physics, high-energy physics, and cosmology, establishing that many mysteries of particle physics and cosmology—from the cosmological constant to the origin of chirality—find natural explanation in the emergent vacuum paradigm.

## Historical Context

In the 1990s and early 2000s, analog gravity programs began exploring whether fundamental physics laws might emerge from condensed matter substrates. Volovik's previous work on superfluid 3He-A had revealed the spontaneous emergence of Weyl fermions, gauge fields, and Lorentz invariance at the Fermi surface. This 2003 monograph synthesizes those discoveries into a comprehensive worldview: the universe is not built on quantum field theory as a foundation, but rather QFT is an effective theory describing excitations in an unknown underlying vacuum—which itself behaves like a superfluid or other condensed matter medium.

The work addresses fundamental puzzles unsolved by standard QFT: Why is the cosmological constant so small? Why are there three generations of fermions? Why does CP violation occur? Why does electromagnetism couple to matter the way it does? From the condensed matter perspective, these are not fine-tuned coincidences but emergent properties of a structured vacuum.

The book's publication in 2003 marked a turning point: serious theoretical physicists now took seriously the possibility that the vacuum itself has structure and that particle physics is emergent thereupon.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### Emergence of Lorentz Symmetry and Gauge Fields

In superfluid 3He-A, the fermionic vacuum (a filled Dirac sea in momentum space) exhibits a point node at which the energy spectrum touches zero. Near this "Fermi point," the low-energy excitations satisfy:

$$E(\mathbf{p}) \approx \hbar v_F |\mathbf{p}|$$

where $v_F$ is the Fermi velocity. This linear dispersion mimics the massless Dirac equation:

$$\gamma^\mu (i \partial_\mu - A_\mu) \psi = 0$$

provided the Fermi velocity $v_F$ is identified with an effective light speed $c_{\text{eff}}$. Lorentz invariance emerges not as a fundamental symmetry, but as a low-energy accident: any effective field theory at scales much smaller than the quantum cutoff inherits this symmetry from the structure of the Fermi surface.

Similarly, gauge fields emerge from the order parameter's internal symmetries. In 3He-A, the superfluid order parameter has spatial variation:

$$\Delta(\mathbf{r}) = \Delta_0 e^{i \phi(\mathbf{r})} \mathbf{d}(\mathbf{r})$$

where $\mathbf{d}$ is a unit vector in spin space. Variations in the phase $\phi$ couple to fermions as a U(1) gauge field, and variations in the orientation of $\mathbf{d}$ induce non-Abelian gauge structure.

### Chirality and the Symmetry Problem

In the standard model, left-handed and right-handed fermions behave very differently (weak interactions violate parity). In 3He-A, the Weyl points occur at points in momentum space where the chiral structure naturally bifurcates: one Weyl point has left-handed dispersion relation, the other right-handed. This chirality emerges spontaneously from the topological winding of the order parameter.

$$\chi_L = \frac{1}{2}(1 - \gamma_5) \psi, \quad \chi_R = \frac{1}{2}(1 + \gamma_5) \psi$$

The left-handed and right-handed excitations couple to the gauge field with opposite charges, naturally explaining why weak interactions distinguish chirality.

### The Cosmological Constant as Vacuum Energy

In a condensed matter system, the vacuum energy density is not zero. At zero temperature, the ground state energy per unit volume is simply:

$$\rho_{\text{vac}} = E_0 / V$$

This depends on the microscopic structure and interactions of the underlying substrate. The "small" cosmological constant observed in the universe might reflect that we live in a regime where the vacuum has a small but nonzero energy density—stable against quantum corrections not because of fine-tuning, but because the underlying condensed matter system has reached an equilibrium.

Moreover, if the vacuum is a superfluid, its equation of state is not necessarily $w = -1$. Different phases of the superfluid might have different equations of state, potentially explaining the evolution of dark energy without invoking quintessence fields.

### Topological Defects and Cosmology

In condensed matter systems, topological defects (vortices, domain walls, monopoles) are inevitable. In 3He-A, vortex cores form whenever the system rotates. Analogously, in the cosmological vacuum, defects should form whenever the universe undergoes symmetry-breaking phase transitions:

$$\text{Vortex core energy} \sim 2\pi \rho_s \kappa$$

where $\rho_s$ is the superfluid density and $\kappa$ is the core radius. In the cosmic setting, such defects manifest as cosmic strings, domain walls, or monopoles—providing a natural geometric origin for topological defects in cosmology.

### The Fermion Universality Class

Volovik argues that Weyl fermions and their topological properties constitute a universality class: any condensed matter system with fermionic quasiparticles and a gapped or semi-gapped spectrum will generically exhibit Weyl points at certain parameter values. This is not special to 3He-A; it is a structural fact about fermionic systems.

The standard model contains 16 types of fermions (quarks and leptons) per generation. Volovik asks: is this number (16) also universal, arising from topological constraints on the number of Weyl points in a realistic condensed matter vacuum?

## Key Results

1. **Emergent spacetime**: Lorentz symmetry and light speed emerge from the dispersion relation of excitations near a topological singularity (Fermi point) in the vacuum's electronic structure. No absolute light speed; effective light speed depends on substrate properties.

2. **Gauge fields from order parameter symmetries**: U(1) electromagnetic gauge field and SU(2) weak isospin emerge from spontaneously broken symmetries of the superfluid order parameter.

3. **Chirality from Weyl fermion structure**: Left-handed and right-handed fermions naturally separate at different locations in momentum space (different Weyl points), explaining why the weak interaction violates parity.

4. **Small cosmological constant from ground state energy**: Vacuum energy density is not fine-tuned; it emerges as the equilibrium ground state energy of a complex underlying system. Dynamic evolution of vacuum properties (different phases) offers alternative to quintessence.

5. **Topological defects as cosmic strings**: Domain walls, vortices, and monopoles forming in the superfluid vacuum translate directly to cosmic topological defects, solving the "naturalness" problem for their existence in cosmology.

6. **Three generations as topological invariant**: The three generations of fermions may correspond to three topological sectors or winding configurations in the underlying fermionic vacuum.

## Legacy and Significance

Volovik's "Universe in a Helium Droplet" catalyzed the modern analog gravity program. It moved the study of effective gravity in condensed matter systems from a specialized curiosity to a serious avenue for understanding fundamental physics. Subsequent work has explored:

- Black hole analogs in flowing fluids and Bose-Einstein condensates
- Hawking radiation in superfluid systems
- Cosmological signatures of topological defects
- The role of Berry phases in particle physics (now recognized as central to topological insulators, topological superconductors, and quantum anomalies)

The book established a research paradigm: if we understand how spacetime emerges from a condensed matter substrate, we gain new tools for solving problems in quantum gravity and cosmology that resist traditional approaches. The universe need not be built on quantum field theory; quantum field theory is a description of how excitations in an unknown underlying order behave at low energies.

For the cosmic web, Volovik's perspective suggests that galaxy clusters, filaments, walls, and voids are not merely statistical artifacts of gravitational clustering. Rather, they may reflect the topological and geometric structure of the underlying quantum vacuum—just as Weyl points in condensed matter systems naturally give rise to specific low-energy structures (bands, gaps, chiral anomalies), the cosmic web structure may be a necessary consequence of the vacuum's topological properties.
