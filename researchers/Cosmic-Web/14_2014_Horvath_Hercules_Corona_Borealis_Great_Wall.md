# The Largest Structure of the Universe Defined by Gamma-Ray Bursts: The Hercules-Corona Borealis Great Wall

**Author(s):** István Horváth, Jon Hakkila, Zsolt Bagoly, et al.

**Year:** 2013 (discovery); 2015 (confirmation study)

**Journal:** arXiv:1510.01933; Astronomy & Astrophysics, Volume 584, A48 (2015)

---

## Abstract

Using gamma-ray burst (GRB) locations, István Horváth and collaborators claim to have discovered the largest known structure in the observable universe: the Hercules-Corona Borealis Great Wall (HCBGW), a putative galaxy filament spanning approximately 10 billion light-years (3 Gpc), some 250 times larger than typical galaxy clusters. While controversial (other researchers question whether the structure is real or a statistical artifact), the discovery raises profound questions about the limits of large-scale homogeneity and whether the universe exhibits even larger-scale structures than previously thought.

---

## Historical Context

Gamma-ray bursts are extremely luminous transient phenomena observable across the observable universe. By 2013, large catalogs of GRB positions and redshifts had accumulated, and Horváth recognized that the 3D distribution of GRBs might reveal structure beyond what galaxy surveys could detect (since galaxy surveys have flux limits and don't extend uniformly to very large distances). Horváth's claim that GRBs cluster in an enormous wall sparked intense debate, with some supporting the finding and others arguing that statistical methods were flawed.

---

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### GRB as Structure Tracers

Gamma-ray bursts come in two types: short (< 2 sec, from neutron star mergers) and long (> 2 sec, from massive star collapse). Long GRBs are associated with massive stars, which trace star formation, which follows dark matter distribution. Thus, long GRBs can serve as tracers of large-scale structure, similar to galaxies.

The key advantage: GRBs are extremely luminous, potentially observable to very large redshifts (z > 5), whereas optical surveys are flux-limited and miss distant structures.

### Detection of Spatial Clustering in GRB Catalog

Horváth et al. analyzed the distribution of GRB positions in 3D (using measured or estimated redshifts) using several statistical tests:

1. **Single-linkage hierarchical clustering**: Connect GRBs separated by distance $< d_{\text{thresh}}$. Identify connected clusters as potential structures.

2. **Cuzick-Edwards test**: Statistical test for spatial clustering in a point process. If GRBs are randomly distributed, the test statistic $T$ has a known null distribution. If $T$ is anomalously high, clustering is significant.

3. **Power spectrum analysis**: Compute the 3D power spectrum $P(k)$ of the GRB distribution. Look for peaks (preferred scales) that would indicate clustering.

### The Hercules-Corona Borealis Great Wall

The analysis identified a statistically significant concentration of GRBs at redshifts $1.6 < z < 2.1$. The spatial extent:

- **Redshift range**: $\Delta z \approx 0.5$ comoving to ~3 Gpc at z = 1.8
- **Angular size**: ~2000-3000 Mpc (comoving) across the sky
- **Linear size estimate**: ~10 Gpc (3 billion light-years)

This would make it one of the largest structures in the observable universe, larger than the "Great Wall" discovered by Geller and Huchra (which is ~500 Mpc).

### Statistical Significance

Horváth reported the clustering was significant at the 3-4σ level using their tests. However, independent analysis raised concerns:

1. **Multiple comparisons problem**: If you perform many statistical tests on the data, some will by chance give apparently significant results even in random data.

2. **Selection effects**: GRB catalogs are not uniformly complete. Dust extinction, detector sensitivity, and other factors bias the sample. Correcting for these biases might change the conclusion.

3. **Redshift uncertainty**: Many GRB redshifts are estimated, not measured, introducing uncertainty.

---

## Key Results

1. **Detection of GRB clustering**: Statistically significant over-density of GRBs at z ~ 1.6-2.1 in a particular region of the sky.

2. **Enormous spatial extent**: If real, this structure spans ~3 Gpc, exceeding the homogeneity scale assumed in cosmology.

3. **Challenge to cosmological principle**: The structure would be anomalously large, pushing the limits of the homogeneity assumption.

4. **Gamma-ray bursts as structure tracers**: Demonstrates that GRBs can be used to map cosmic structure at high redshifts.

## Legacy and Significance

The HCBGW claim remains controversial but has sparked useful discussions about:

- Large-scale structure limits
- Statistical methods for detecting clustering
- The validity of the homogeneity assumption
- GRBs as cosmological probes
