# A Giant Arc on the Sky: A New Candidate for the Largest Structure in the Universe

**Author(s):** Alexia M. Lopez, Roger G. Clowes, Gerard M. Williger

**Year:** 2022

**Journal:** Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society, Volume 516, Pages 1557--1573

---

## Abstract

Lopez, Clowes, and Williger report the discovery of a giant arc spanning approximately 1 gigaparsec (3 billion light-years) at redshift z ~ 0.8, identified via Mg II absorption systems in background quasar spectra. Using multiple statistical tests (single-linkage clustering, Cuzick-Edwards, power-spectrum analysis), they demonstrate that the structure's existence at >4σ confidence level challenges the cosmological principle of large-scale homogeneity. If confirmed, this discovery would represent one of the largest known structures in the universe and would necessitate reassessment of structure formation theory and the universe's homogeneity scale.

---

## Historical Context

Following the controversial claim of the Hercules-Corona Borealis Great Wall (Horváth et al., 2013-2015), researchers have continued searching for anomalously large structures. Lopez et al.'s detection of a giant arc using an independent method (Mg II absorbers) and redshift range adds credibility to the possibility that the universe contains structures larger than previously thought.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### Mg II Absorption as Structure Tracer

Mg II is an ion that strongly absorbs ultraviolet light at specific wavelengths. When light from distant quasars passes through intervening galaxies and gas clouds, Mg II absorption lines appear in the quasar spectrum at wavelengths shifted by the redshift of the absorbing material:

$$\lambda_{\text{obs}} = \lambda_{\text{rest}} (1 + z_{\text{abs}})$$

By measuring absorption lines in multiple quasar spectra, one can map the 3D distribution of Mg II systems (which trace galaxies/gas clouds) as a function of redshift, effectively creating a 3D map of cosmic structure.

Advantages:
- Independent of galaxy luminosity (even dark galaxies can be detected via absorption)
- Samples large cosmic volume (quasars are luminous background sources)
- Sensitive to both galaxies and diffuse gas

### Detecting the Giant Arc

Lopez et al. analyzed a catalog of Mg II absorbers at redshifts z ~ 0.8, using:

1. **Single-linkage clustering**: Connect absorbers within distance $d < 50$ Mpc (comoving). Identify connected components as potential structures.

2. **Statistical significance tests**: Compare the observed structure to random catalogs with the same Poisson density. Tests yield statistical significance.

3. **Multiple comparison corrections**: Account for the fact that many potential structures could be found by chance.

Results:
- **Angular extent**: ~2000-3000 Mpc
- **Redshift range**: z ~ 0.75-0.90 (spanning ~1 Gpc in comoving distance)
- **Morphology**: Roughly arc-shaped, not a simple cluster
- **Statistical significance**: >4σ by multiple methods

### Challenges to Homogeneity

A structure spanning ~1 Gpc at z ~ 0.8 would violate the assumption of large-scale homogeneity (that at sufficiently large scales, the universe appears uniform). The homogeneity scale is usually assumed to be ~100-300 Mpc based on galaxy survey data. A 1 Gpc structure would extend well beyond this.

However, critical responses note:

1. **Sample size**: The Mg II absorber catalog is relatively small; random fluctuations could mimic structures.

2. **Completeness**: The Mg II absorber sample is not uniformly complete; selection effects could bias the result.

3. **Alternative explanations**: The "arc" morphology might be coincidental; random distributions can produce elongated features.

## Key Results

1. **Detection of a 1 Gpc-scale structure**: Via Mg II absorption systems, a statistically significant overdensity spanning ~1 Gpc.

2. **Challenges to homogeneity**: If confirmed, would be one of the largest known structures and would require reassessment of the homogeneity scale.

3. **Alternative structure tracer**: Mg II absorbers provide a complementary method to galaxy surveys and GRBs for mapping cosmic structure.

   $$z_{\text{abs}} = \frac{\lambda_{\text{obs}} - \lambda_{\text{rest}}}{\lambda_{\text{rest}}}$$

2. **Comoving distance**:
   $$d_c = \frac{c}{H_0} \int_0^z \frac{dz'}{E(z')}$$
