# The Supercluster-Void Network: Large-Scale Structure of the Universe

**Author(s):** Jaan Einasto, et al.

**Year:** 2001 (comprehensive review); original discoveries 1978-1990

**Journal:** Astrophysics and Space Science (or Astronomy & Astrophysics)

---

## Abstract

Building on observational discoveries spanning two decades, Einasto and collaborators (Joeveer, Tago, and others) synthesize evidence that the universe's large-scale structure consists of a network of superclusters (clusters of galaxy clusters) separated by vast voids. Rather than a uniform distribution, galaxies and clusters form thin elongated filaments connecting supercluster nodes, with nearly empty space between. This paper catalogs superclusters, measures filament properties, characterizes void sizes and shapes, and establishes that the supercluster-void network has a periodic or quasi-periodic structure, suggesting a preferred length scale in the universe.

---

## Historical Context

In 1978, Joeveer, Einasto, and Tago made a startling discovery: when galaxy positions from redshift surveys were plotted, they revealed not a random or smooth distribution, but a striking pattern—galaxies concentrated in thin sheets and filaments, with vast empty voids between. This discovery came simultaneously from independent groups but Einasto's careful analysis and comprehensive catalogs became definitive. By the 1980s-1990s, redshift surveys (CfA, SSRS) confirmed the structure on ever-larger scales. By 2001, with improved catalogs and analysis, Einasto's work provided the most detailed map of the supercluster network and the first quantitative description of void statistics.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### Discovery of the Supercluster-Void Structure

Initial analysis of galaxy catalogs showed that instead of a Poisson random distribution, galaxies exhibited strong clustering. When the three-dimensional positions were plotted (using redshift as a distance proxy), a striking pattern emerged: galaxies were **not** uniformly distributed throughout space, but rather concentrated into:

1. **Compact clusters**: Groups of 2-20 galaxies within ~1-2 Mpc, density $\rho \sim 100-1000 \langle \rho \rangle$
2. **Superclusters**: Collections of clusters spanning 10-50+ Mpc, density $\rho \sim 5-10 \langle \rho \rangle$
3. **Filaments**: Thin, elongated structures connecting superclusters, width ~2-3 Mpc, length 50-200+ Mpc
4. **Walls**: 2D sheets of intermediate density
5. **Voids**: Nearly empty regions, density $\rho \sim 0.1-0.2 \langle \rho \rangle$, sizes 10-100+ Mpc

The **void fraction** (fraction of volume with $\rho < 0.2 \langle \rho \rangle$) is approximately **60-70%**, meaning the bulk of the universe's volume is nearly empty.

### Supercluster Catalog and Properties

Einasto et al. compiled comprehensive catalogs of superclusters, defining them as clusters of clusters connected via filaments. Key properties:

- **Number**: Several hundred superclusters identified in local universe (out to z ~ 0.1), with extrapolated tens of thousands in observed universe
- **Mass range**: $10^{15}$ to $10^{17}$ $M_\odot$ (from a few clusters to dozens)
- **Size range**: 10-100+ Mpc (comoving)
- **Density contrast**: $\delta = (\rho - \langle \rho \rangle) / \langle \rho \rangle \sim 5-20$ for supercluster centers
- **Shape**: Highly elongated, with aspect ratios 2-5:1 (filamentary)

Notable examples include:
- **Virgo Supercluster**: Contains the Local Group; mass ~10^15 $M_\odot$; dominated by Virgo cluster
- **Coma Supercluster**: Major structure in local universe; contains Coma cluster and associated groups
- **Shapley Concentration**: Largest nearby concentration, influencing local flows

### Filament Properties

Filaments connecting superclusters have distinctive properties:

- **Width**: 2-5 Mpc (comoving), with substructure on smaller scales
- **Length**: 50-500+ Mpc, often connecting 2-5 major clusters
- **Density profile**: Roughly Gaussian in transverse direction, with central density peak of 2-5 times mean
- **Mass**: Contains ~30-50% of all matter in the universe (despite comprising only ~5-10% of volume)
- **Dynamics**: Generally in infall toward clusters at filament ends; some rotation or shear flow along filament length

Filaments exhibit **clustering in position space**: major filaments tend to align along preferred directions (related to the filament's embedding in the supercluster structure), suggesting anisotropic stress from nearby massive structures.

### Void Statistics and Structure

Voids are characterized by:

- **Size distribution**: Roughly log-normal, with typical volumes $\sim (20-50 \text{ Mpc})^3$
- **Density contrast**: $\delta \sim -0.8$ to $-0.95$ (almost empty)
- **Shape**: Roughly spherical in 2D (ellipticity $e \sim 0.2-0.3$), but connected in 3D into a network
- **Void walls**: Voids are separated by thin walls (density contrast $\delta \sim +1$ to $+5$), not by diffuse intermediate-density regions

The void network has **Euler characteristic** $\chi < 0$ (genus > 0), indicating topological complexity: voids are interconnected like a foam.

Void evolution: In ΛCDM simulations, voids expand during the matter-dominated era, then expand faster during the dark-energy-dominated era. Their shapes evolve from spherical to more aspherical (tidal stretching).

### Periodicity and Length-Scale Universality

One of Einasto's more controversial claims: the supercluster-void network exhibits **quasi-periodicity** with a characteristic scale:

$$\lambda_{\text{char}} \sim 100-130 \text{ Mpc}$$

This would correspond to a preferred length scale in the universe. Evidence:
1. The period of the largest eigenmode in power spectrum: $k_{\text{max}} \sim 2\pi / 100 \text{ Mpc}$
2. Typical supercluster separations: ~100-150 Mpc
3. Typical void sizes: ~50-80 Mpc

**Interpretation**: If real, this would suggest the universe's large-scale structure is not generic but reflects a specific pattern-formation process. However, later analyses suggest the apparent periodicity may be a statistical artifact of small-sample bias or selection effects.

### Matter Distribution and Filament Orientation

Einasto noted that filaments in the supercluster network are **not randomly oriented**. Instead:

1. Superclusters tend to be **prolate** (elongated along one axis)
2. Major filaments connecting superclusters align with the longest axis of the structure
3. This suggests **tidal torque**: the matter distribution shapes structures through gravitational tidal forces, aligning elongated bodies with the principal axes of the tidal tensor

This insight—that structure shapes are determined by tidal geometry—became central to understanding structure formation in the context of Zeldovich approximation and tidal torque theory.

## Key Results

1. **Supercluster-void network structure**: The universe's matter is organized into a hierarchical network of superclusters and voids, not a random or smooth distribution.

2. **Void dominance**: Voids comprise ~60-70% of universe's volume but contain only ~10-20% of mass, while filaments (5-10% of volume) contain 30-50% of mass.

3. **Scale hierarchy**: Characteristic scales are ~1-2 Mpc (clusters), ~10-30 Mpc (superclusters), ~100 Mpc (supercluster separations), suggesting hierarchical structure formation.

4. **Filament mass and extent**: Major filaments can contain $10^{15}$-$10^{16}$ $M_\odot$ and extend 50-500 Mpc, playing a crucial role in matter distribution and dynamics.

5. **Void network topology**: Voids interconnect to form a foam-like network with topological complexity (non-trivial genus), not isolated bubbles.

6. **Tidal alignment**: Elongated structures (filaments, superclusters) align with the principal axes of the tidal tensor, indicating tidal torque is the primary driver of structure shape.

7. **Quasi-periodicity claim**: Possible characteristic scale ~100-130 Mpc (controversial, likely statistical artifact).

## Legacy and Significance

Einasto's work established the field of large-scale structure cosmology. It showed that:

1. The universe has structure, not random or uniform matter distribution
2. This structure is organized in a hierarchy of scales
3. Topological and geometric properties matter for understanding the universe

Subsequent work built on these discoveries:
- **Power spectrum analysis**: Quantified the distribution of structure at different scales
- **Structure formation simulations**: N-body codes modeled how superclusters and voids form from initial density fluctuations
- **Observational surveys**: SDSS, 2dFGRS, DESI all mapped the cosmic web with unprecedented precision
- **Void cosmology**: Voids recognized as powerful probes of dark energy and modified gravity

For the cosmic web's role in fundamental physics, Einasto's work suggests that large-scale structure is not generic but reflects deep properties—potentially the topological and geometric properties of an underlying quantum substrate, as phonon-exflation proposes.
