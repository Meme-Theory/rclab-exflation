# Mapping the Universe: The CfA Redshift Survey and the Discovery of the Great Wall

**Author(s):** Margaret J. Geller, John P. Huchra

**Year:** 1989

**Journal:** Science, Volume 246, Pages 897--903

---

## Abstract

Geller and Huchra present a three-dimensional map of galaxy positions derived from redshift measurements of nearly 18,000 galaxies in the northern sky. Their map reveals a striking filamentary structure: galaxies are concentrated in thin sheets and elongated filaments, with vast empty voids between. Most notably, they discover the "Great Wall"—a thin sheet of galaxies spanning at least 500 million light-years with only 15 million light-years thickness. This landmark paper provided the first detailed map of the cosmic web and established that the universe's large-scale structure is far more organized and geometrically complex than previously imagined.

---

## Historical Context

Prior to the 1980s, the large-scale structure of the universe was poorly known. While Einasto and others had detected voids and filaments statistically, no detailed visual map existed. The CfA Redshift Survey, initiated in 1977 under Davis, Huchra, Latham, and Tonry, measured recession velocities (redshifts) for thousands of galaxies, treating redshift as a measure of distance. By the mid-1980s, Geller and Huchra had accumulated enough data to create a detailed 3D map. When they plotted the galaxy distribution, the result was stunning: rather than a uniform or random distribution, galaxies formed a cosmic web of filaments and voids.

The 1989 publication in Science brought this discovery to the broader community and to the public, sparking intense interest in large-scale structure and structure formation theory. Subsequent redshift surveys (SDSS, 2dFGRS, and beyond) have confirmed and extended Geller and Huchra's findings.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### The CfA Redshift Survey Methodology

The CfA surveys (CfA1 and CfA2) used the 1.5-meter Tillinghast telescope at the Fred L. Whipple Observatory, measuring redshifts by taking spectra of galaxies and measuring the Doppler shift of spectral lines:

$$z = \frac{\lambda_{\text{obs}} - \lambda_{\text{rest}}}{\lambda_{\text{rest}}}$$

The recession velocity is related to redshift by:

$$v = c z \quad \text{(non-relativistic approximation, valid for } z \ll 1 \text{)}$$

Using the Hubble law, $v = H_0 d$, the distance is:

$$d = \frac{cz}{H_0}$$

For $H_0 = 100$ km/s/Mpc (typical in 1989), a galaxy with $z = 0.03$ (9,000 km/s) is at distance d ~ 90 Mpc.

The CfA2 survey measured **~18,000 galaxies** in the northern sky, primarily in the magnitude range 14.5-15.5. The galaxies were not uniformly distributed in magnitude; rather, the sample was designed to be flux-limited, meaning fainter galaxies at greater distances are progressively absent.

### Three-Dimensional Mapping and Data Visualization

To visualize the 3D structure, Geller and Huchra plotted **"slice" maps**: they selected galaxies within thin wedges of the sky (e.g., 8 hours to 16 hours in right ascension, declination band ±30 degrees) and plotted each galaxy as a point in 2D (right ascension on the x-axis, distance on the y-axis).

The resulting maps showed:

1. **Clusters**: Dense concentrations of points (actual clusters of galaxies)
2. **Filaments**: Linear or ribbon-like structures connecting clusters
3. **Voids**: Large empty regions with few or no galaxies

The most prominent structure was the **Great Wall**:
- **Length**: At least 500 million light-years (Gpc scale)
- **Thickness**: Only 15-20 million light-years (anisotropic, sheet-like)
- **Distance**: Approximately 200-300 Mpc from our galaxy
- **Extent**: Spans a significant fraction of the surveyed volume

This structure was so large and thin that it could not have been a chance fluctuation; it represented a fundamental feature of cosmic large-scale structure.

### Statistical Characterization

To quantify the structure, Geller and Huchra computed:

1. **Correlation function**: $\xi(r)$, measuring clustering of galaxies at different separations
2. **Void statistics**: Size and spatial distribution of empty regions
3. **Filament properties**: Width, length, and connectivity

The correlation function showed:

$$\xi(r) \sim (r / 5 \text{ Mpc})^{-1.8}$$

for scales 0.1 Mpc $< r <$ 30 Mpc, indicating power-law clustering consistent with hierarchical structure formation. At very large scales (r > 100 Mpc), the correlation function drops and approaches zero, indicating the approach to homogeneity.

The clustering amplitude and scaling indicated that the power spectrum of density fluctuations has significant power on large scales (filament scales ~10-100 Mpc).

### Cosmological Implications

The existence of the Great Wall and other large-scale structures posed a challenge for cosmology:

1. **Large-scale homogeneity**: At what scale does the universe become uniform? The Great Wall was so large that it challenged the assumption of homogeneity on scales < 500 Mpc.

2. **Structure formation timescale**: The Great Wall and other structures would require time to form under gravitational instability. Geller and Huchra noted that if the universe is younger than the gravitational assembly timescale, structures might have formed from primordial fluctuations that had not yet reached equilibrium.

3. **Initial spectrum of fluctuations**: The observed clustering pattern constrains the primordial power spectrum P(k) of density fluctuations. The observed power spectrum seemed to have significant power on large scales, potentially inconsistent with simple inflation models (which generically predict a scale-invariant power spectrum $P(k) \propto k$).

## Key Results

1. **First high-resolution 3D map of the cosmic web**: The CfA redshift map revealed the detailed structure of the nearby universe (z < 0.05), showing filaments, voids, and clusters.

2. **Discovery of the Great Wall**: A structure spanning 500+ Mpc with thickness ~15-20 Mpc, demonstrating that the universe has organized, large-scale structures.

3. **Characterization of void properties**: Voids are not small isolated bubbles but large extended regions, interconnected into a network that fills most of the volume.

4. **Clustering power law**: The correlation function on large scales follows a power law $\xi(r) \propto r^{-\gamma}$ with $\gamma \approx 1.8$, providing constraints on the power spectrum.

5. **Anisotropic structure**: The discovery that structures are highly anisotropic (thin sheets and filaments, not isotropic clusters) constrains growth mechanisms and dynamics.

6. **Statistical significance**: The structures are not random fluctuations but represent fundamental features of cosmic organization.

## Key Equations

1. **Redshift-distance relation** (Hubble law):
   $$d = \frac{cz}{H_0}$$

2. **Recession velocity**:
   $$v = cz$$

3. **Correlation function power law**:
   $$\xi(r) = (r / r_0)^{-\gamma}, \quad \gamma \approx 1.8, \quad r_0 \approx 5 \text{ Mpc}$$

4. **Number count as function of distance**:
   $$N(< d) \propto d^3 \quad \text{(for uniform distribution)}$$

5. **Actual number counts from CfA data** (showing clustering):
   $$\frac{dN}{dV} = \bar{n} (1 + \xi(r))$$

## Legacy and Significance

Geller and Huchra's work opened the era of large-scale structure cosmology. Key developments:

- **SDSS (Sloan Digital Sky Survey)**: Measured redshifts of a million galaxies, providing the highest-resolution map of the cosmic web to date
- **2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey**: Complementary survey of the southern sky with similar precision
- **Structure formation simulations**: N-body codes now routinely model how structures like the Great Wall form from primordial density fluctuations
- **Topology and persistent homology**: Modern mathematical methods for characterizing cosmic web structure
- **Void cosmology**: Recognition that voids can be used as cosmological probes (now a major research area)

For the cosmic web's role in fundamental physics, Geller and Huchra's map established that cosmic structure is not trivial—it has characteristic scales, anisotropies, and topology that any fundamental theory must explain. Whether that theory is ΛCDM+inflation or phonon-exflation or something else, it must account for the cosmic web as observed by Geller and Huchra.
