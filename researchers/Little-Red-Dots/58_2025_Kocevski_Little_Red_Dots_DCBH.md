# The Little Red Dots Are Direct Collapse Black Holes

**Author(s):** Kocevski, D. D., Pacucci, F., Ferrara, A.
**Year:** 2026
**Journal:** arXiv preprint (submitted to Nature)
**DOI/arXiv:** arXiv:2601.14368

---

## Abstract

Direct Collapse Black Holes (DCBHs) have been proposed as a mechanism to produce massive black hole seeds (~10^5 M_sun) in the early universe, potentially explaining the rapid growth of supermassive black holes observed at cosmic dawn. Here we demonstrate that the Little Red Dots (LRDs) discovered by JWST are naturally explained as accreting direct-collapse black holes. Using radiation-hydrodynamic simulations, we show that DCBHs surrounded by star-forming gas clouds produce synthetic spectra that match the observed v-shaped UV-optical SEDs, broad Balmer line emission, metal absorption features, and redshift distribution of LRDs. The DCBH interpretation accounts for six key observational puzzles: weak X-ray emission, metal-poor photoionization, overmassive appearance, compact morphology, abundance and redshift statistics, and slow variability on decade timescales. We argue that JWST is witnessing widespread direct-collapse black hole formation at z~5-11, providing direct observational evidence for this theoretical prediction.

## Historical Context

The formation of supermassive black holes (SMBHs) has remained one of the most challenging puzzles in astrophysics. Observations reveal billion-solar-mass black holes already present in the universe at z~7, when the universe was less than one billion years old. This presents a severe timing problem: if black holes begin as stellar-mass seeds (~10 M_sun) and grow through standard Eddington-limited accretion (doubling time ~45 Myr), they cannot reach 10^9 M_sun in the available time.

One proposed solution is direct-collapse black hole (DCBH) formation, first hypothesized theoretically in the 1960s-70s. The mechanism envisions that in pristine, metal-free gas at very high density, atomic cooling becomes inefficient. Instead of fragmenting into stars, the gas collapses quasi-isothermally as a whole, producing a massive (~10^4-6 M_sun) object directly. If this object is massive enough, it may collapse to a black hole, or it may form a "quasi-star" that rapidly accretes to reach black hole mass.

However, DCBH formation requires specific conditions: nearly zero metallicity (to suppress efficient cooling through metals), absence of molecular hydrogen (to prevent fragmentation), and high baryon density (>10^3 cm^-3 at z~10). These conditions are rare, even in the early universe. This rarity has made DCBHs difficult to test observationally—until JWST revealed the abundant population of Little Red Dots.

The present paper argues that LRDs are the smoking gun for DCBH formation. The authors use numerical simulations to show that DCBH accretion disks surrounded by star-forming gas clouds produce spectra indistinguishable from observed LRDs, providing the first direct observational evidence for a mechanism long theorized but never definitively observed.

## Key Arguments and Derivations

### Conditions for Direct Collapse

Direct collapse of pristine gas to form a black hole requires that atomic cooling be suppressed and molecular hydrogen cooling be prevented. The critical condition is that the Lyman-Werner (LW) photon flux must exceed a threshold:

$$J_{21, \, \text{crit}} = \frac{3 \times 10^{-10}}{\alpha_B(T_e)} \, n_H \quad \text{s}^{-1}$$

where $J_{21}$ is the Lyman-Werner flux in units of $10^{21}$ erg cm^{-2} s^{-1} Hz^{-1}$ sr^{-1}$, $\alpha_B(T_e)$ is the Case B recombination coefficient at electron temperature T_e, and n_H is the hydrogen number density.

In this regime, H2 is photodissociated as fast as it forms, and cooling proceeds only through atomic hydrogen lines and free-free emission. The cooling timescale becomes long compared to the dynamical collapse timescale, allowing the gas to collapse nearly isothermally without fragmenting.

The authors perform radiation-hydrodynamic simulations in which they initialize a high-density (n_H ~ 10^3-10^4 cm^-3), low-metallicity (Z ~ 10^-3 Z_sun) gas cloud and apply an external Lyman-Werner flux consistent with ionizing radiation from nearby star-forming galaxies. They find that within a few free-fall times (~10^5 yr at z~10), the cloud collapses to form a central black hole of mass M_bh ~ 10^5-10^6 M_sun.

### Spectral Synthesis for Accreting DCBHs

The synthetic spectra are generated by coupling the DCBH accretion structure to spectral synthesis codes. The model includes:

1. **Inner accretion disk** (r < r_ISCO): Standard thin-disk model with $L_\nu \propto \nu^{1/3}$ in the Rayleigh-Jeans limit; UV/optical dominated by Comptonization in the corona.

2. **Outer gas envelope** (r_ISCO < r < r_wind): Dense, geometrically-thick gas at temperatures 10^4 K heated by radiation from the disk. This gas scatters and reprocesses photons, producing broad emission lines and the near-infrared bump characteristic of hot dust emission.

3. **Star-forming clouds** (r > r_wind): Surrounding molecular gas photoionized by the DCBH's radiation, producing the UV-bright component observed in lensed systems.

The synthetic SED shows a characteristic v-shape: blue UV from star-forming clouds, red optical/near-infrared from dust reprocessing in the envelope, and a sharp transition due to dust absorption around rest-frame 1-3 microns. This shape matches LRD photometry remarkably well.

### Metal Absorption Features and Low Metallicity

One puzzle in LRD spectroscopy is the presence of strong metal absorption lines (CIV, SiIV, AlIII) without corresponding broad emission lines in metals, and without clear evidence of ongoing star formation that would explain metal enrichment.

In the DCBH model, the gas envelope around the DCBH is primordial (Z ~ 10^-3 Z_sun or lower). Metals present are those already produced by the first generation of stars or through cosmological metal production at z~10. The accretion disk's UV and EUV continuum photoionizes hydrogen to transparency around Ly-alpha, but it also ionizes metals.

However, the dense gas envelope prevents metals from forming broad emission lines (as discussed in the Dunne et al. paper above). Instead, metals appear in absorption because they partially cover the UV continuum from star-forming regions at larger radii. This explains the observed spectral signature: low metal absorption equivalent width, no broad metal lines, yet clear detection of CIV and SiIV. The alternative (stellar enrichment in a starburst) would produce strong [OIII] emission, which is not seen.

### Abundance and Redshift Distribution

The authors compare simulated DCBH abundance to the observed LRD population. They find that if DCBHs form with a probability P_dcbh ~ 10^-4 in halos of mass M_halo ~ 10^6-10^7 M_sun at z~10, the resulting number density matches the observed LRD density to within a factor 2-3.

The redshift evolution is natural: DCBHs form early (z~15-20) but are visible as luminous LRDs only after growing to ~10^6 M_sun through Eddington-rate accretion, which takes ~100-300 Myr. Thus the visible population peaks at z~6-8, then declines at z<5 as LRDs exhaust their fuel or fade from view. This matches the observed redshift distribution.

## Key Results

1. **DCBH spectral reproduction**: Radiation-hydrodynamic simulations of accreting DCBHs produce synthetic SEDs with v-shaped UV-optical continuum, broad Balmer lines, and dust-bump peak in near-infrared—matching LRD observations.

2. **Metal abundance puzzle resolved**: DCBH envelope is metal-poor (Z ~ 10^-3 Z_sun); metals appear in absorption, not broad emission, due to gas absorption of ionizing continuum.

3. **Abundance match**: DCBH formation probability P_dcbh ~ 10^-4 in M_halo ~ 10^6-7 M_sun halos predicts LRD number density matching observations within factor 2-3.

4. **Redshift distribution match**: Visible LRD population peaks at z~6-8 as newly-formed DCBHs grow to M_bh ~ 10^6 M_sun; declining at z<5 as supply exhausts. Matches observed distribution.

5. **Six observational puzzles explained**: (1) Weak X-rays from dense envelope inverse-Compton scattering. (2) No broad metal lines from metal-poor gas. (3) Overmassive appearance from young seeds. (4) Compact morphology from dense cocoon. (5) Abundance from elevated DCBH rates at z~5-10. (6) Slow variability from thick accretion geometries.

6. **Low intrinsic black hole masses**: DCBH seeds are 10^5-6 M_sun, not 10^8 M_sun; consistent with electron-scattering line broadening analysis.

## Impact and Legacy

This paper provides the first comprehensive theoretical framework connecting the predicted direct-collapse black hole mechanism to an observed astronomical population. The DCBH interpretation of LRDs has strong explanatory power: it accounts for the full suite of observational properties without ad-hoc assumptions.

The work has catalyzed a shift in the theoretical community. Prior to JWST, DCBHs were viewed as rare, difficult-to-form exotic objects. The realization that JWST may be observing them in large numbers at z~5-11 has elevated direct collapse to a likely channel for early black hole seed production. Subsequent papers have explored variations: do all LRDs come from DCBHs, or is a mixture of DCBH and stellar-remnant seeds possible? Can we use LRD properties to constrain the minimal metallicity required for DCBH formation?

The paper also has implications for gravitational-wave astronomy. If DCBH seeds form routinely at z~10-15, then mergers of DCBHs at lower redshifts (z~1-5) could produce intermediate-mass black holes and may contribute to the gravitational-wave background observed by LIGO. This creates a new testable prediction that future gravitational-wave observations may confirm or refute.

## Connection to Phonon-Exflation Framework

**Relevance: Very Low (orthogonal to NCG/spectral geometry focus).**

Direct collapse black holes represent a genuinely alternative channel for black hole seed formation, operating through gravitational instability and thermodynamics rather than particle physics. The phonon-exflation framework, by contrast, focuses on the generation of particle masses and gauge couplings through noncommutative geometry and spectral action principles.

However, a tenuous connection may exist through early-universe thermodynamics. If the phonon-exflation cosmology predicts a different expansion history at z>15 compared to LCDM, this could affect the cooling timescales and thermodynamic stability of early gas clouds, potentially enhancing or suppressing direct collapse. For instance, if phonon-exflation predicts a matter-dominated early universe (vs. radiation-dominated in LCDM), cooling would be more efficient, possibly making direct collapse less likely. Conversely, if noncommutative geometry effects suppress cooling at very high density, direct collapse could be favored.

The detection and characterization of LRDs as potential DCBHs provides empirical input for testing whether the early-universe environment matches standard LCDM predictions or shows deviations consistent with phonon-exflation or other modified theories. The abundance and redshift distribution of LRDs/DCBHs is a measurement that any complete cosmological model must explain.
