### Session 41 connections

**SU(3) as phononic crystal (S41 W3-1b)**. The identification of SU(3) under Jensen deformation as a phononic crystal is the central S41 contribution from the quantum acoustics perspective. The literature on phononic crystals (Jin et al. 2024 roadmap, the topological phonon classification in PNAS 2016) provides the standard vocabulary: Brillouin zone (= Peter-Weyl lattice), band structure (= B1/B2/B3 branches), band gap (= 0.820 M_KK hard gap), van Hove singularities (= rho = 14.02, smooth wall DOS). The crucial difference from standard phononic crystals is that the SU(3) "lattice" is a compact Lie group, not a periodic Euclidean structure. This is why:

- **Umklapp does not exist** (S41 structural result). Standard phononic crystals have finite Brillouin zones and Umklapp scattering that relaxes crystal momentum. The SU(3) rep lattice is infinite and non-periodic. The 2025 hyperbolic lattice paper (arXiv:2511.16328) establishes that Goldstone theorem failure and modified phonon spectra are generic features of non-Euclidean lattice dynamics. Our compact-manifold result is a specific instance of this general phenomenon.

- **The Debye temperature is transcendently high** (T/Theta_D ~ 10^{-22}). Standard phononic crystals become classical at T > Theta_D. Our crystal is quantum at all physically relevant temperatures. The quantum acoustics literature (Aspelmeyer optomechanics, Cleland ground-state cooling) provides the experimental context: ground-state occupation of phonon modes is achievable, and our crystal is effectively always in its quantum ground state.

- **N_eff step function** (S41 W2-1). The mode count jumps from 32 to 240 at infinitesimal tau. In phononic crystal language, this is an instantaneous band-splitting event: the round SU(3) has maximal accidental degeneracy (all modes within each sector are degenerate), and any deformation immediately resolves all bands. The "gradual refinement" picture is falsified. The standard phononic crystal literature does not have an analog of this -- it corresponds to a crystal that transitions from a single-atom basis to a multi-atom basis at infinitesimal strain.
