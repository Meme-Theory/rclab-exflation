In [physics](/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **Kaluza–Klein theory** (**KK theory**) is an attempt at creating a [unified field theory](/wiki/Unified_field_theory "Unified field theory") of [gravitation](/wiki/Gravitation "Gravitation") and [electromagnetism](/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism") based on the idea of a [fifth dimension](/wiki/Five-dimensional_space#Physics "Five-dimensional space") of space beyond the conventional four-dimensional spacetime of general relativity. According to this proposal, there are three dimensions of space and one of time but with an additional spatial dimension in the shape of a tiny circle. The Kaluza-Klein theory is named after [Theodor Kaluza](/wiki/Theodor_Kaluza "Theodor Kaluza") and [Oskar Klein](/wiki/Oskar_Klein "Oskar Klein"). It is [not supported by experiments](#Empirical_tests), but is a precursor to [supergravity](/wiki/Supergravity "Supergravity") and modern [string theory](/wiki/String_theory "String theory") (in eleven-dimensional spacetime).[[1]](#cite_note-:2-1)

## Field equations from the Kaluza hypothesis

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Field equations from the Kaluza hypothesis")]

The [Kaluza–Klein–Einstein field equations](/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein%E2%80%93Einstein_field_equations "Kaluza–Klein–Einstein field equations") of the five-dimensional theory were never adequately provided by Kaluza or Klein because they ignored the [scalar field](/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field"). The full Kaluza field equations are generally attributed to Thiry,[[3]](#cite_note-thry-3) who obtained vacuum field equations, although Kaluza[[2]](#cite_note-kal-2) originally provided a stress–energy tensor for his theory, and Thiry included a stress–energy tensor in his thesis. But as described by Gonner,[[4]](#cite_note-gon-4) several independent groups worked on the field equations in the 1940s and earlier. Thiry is perhaps best known only because an English translation was provided by Applequist, Chodos, & Freund in their review book.[[5]](#cite_note-5) Applequist et al. also provided an English translation of Kaluza's article. Translations of the three (1946, 1947, 1948) Jordan articles can be found on the [ResearchGate](/wiki/ResearchGate "ResearchGate") and [Academia.edu](/wiki/Academia.edu "Academia.edu") archives.[[6]](#cite_note-jor1-6)[[7]](#cite_note-jor2-7)[[8]](#cite_note-jor3-8) The first correct English-language Kaluza field equations, including the scalar field, were provided by Williams.[[9]](#cite_note-LLW-9)

## Group theory interpretation

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Group theory interpretation")]

The space *M* × *C* is compactified over the compact set *C*, and after Kaluza–Klein decomposition one has an [effective field theory](/wiki/Effective_field_theory "Effective field theory") over *M*.

In 1926, Oskar Klein proposed that the fourth spatial dimension is curled up in a circle of a very small [radius](/wiki/Radius "Radius"), so that a [particle](/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle") moving a short distance along that axis would return to where it began. The distance a particle can travel before reaching its initial position is said to be the size of the dimension. This extra dimension is a [compact set](/wiki/Compact_set "Compact set"), and construction of this compact dimension is referred to as [compactification](/wiki/Compactification_(physics) "Compactification (physics)").

In modern geometry, the extra fifth dimension can be understood to be the [circle group](/wiki/Circle_group "Circle group") [U(1)](/wiki/U(1) "U(1)"), as [electromagnetism](/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism") can essentially be formulated as a [gauge theory](/wiki/Gauge_theory "Gauge theory") on a [fiber bundle](/wiki/Fiber_bundle "Fiber bundle"), the [circle bundle](/wiki/Circle_bundle "Circle bundle"), with [gauge group](/wiki/Gauge_group "Gauge group") U(1). In Kaluza–Klein theory this group suggests that gauge symmetry is the symmetry of circular compact dimensions. Once this geometrical interpretation is understood, it is relatively straightforward to replace U(1) by a general [Lie group](/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group"). Such generalizations are often called [Yang–Mills theories](/wiki/Yang%E2%80%93Mills_theory "Yang–Mills theory"). If a distinction is drawn, then it is that Yang–Mills theories occur on a flat spacetime, whereas Kaluza–Klein treats the more general case of curved spacetime. The base space of Kaluza–Klein theory need not be four-dimensional spacetime; it can be any ([pseudo-](/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold"))[Riemannian manifold](/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"), or even a [supersymmetric](/wiki/Supersymmetry "Supersymmetry") manifold or [orbifold](/wiki/Orbifold "Orbifold") or even a [noncommutative space](/wiki/Noncommutative_space "Noncommutative space").

The construction can be outlined, roughly, as follows.[[18]](#cite_note-18) One starts by considering a [principal fiber bundle](/wiki/Principal_fiber_bundle "Principal fiber bundle") *P* with [gauge group](/wiki/Gauge_group "Gauge group") *G* over a [manifold](/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") M. Given a [connection](/wiki/Connection_(fibred_manifold) "Connection (fibred manifold)") on the bundle, and a [metric](/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") on the base manifold, and a gauge invariant metric on the tangent of each fiber, one can construct a [bundle metric](/wiki/Metric_(vector_bundle) "Metric (vector bundle)") defined on the entire bundle. Computing the [scalar curvature](/wiki/Scalar_curvature "Scalar curvature") of this bundle metric, one finds that it is constant on each fiber: this is the "Kaluza miracle". One did not have to explicitly impose a cylinder condition, or to compactify: by assumption, the gauge group is already compact. Next, one takes this scalar curvature as the [Lagrangian density](/wiki/Lagrangian_density "Lagrangian density"), and, from this, constructs the [Einstein–Hilbert action](/wiki/Einstein%E2%80%93Hilbert_action "Einstein–Hilbert action") for the bundle, as a whole. The equations of motion, the [Euler–Lagrange equations](/wiki/Euler%E2%80%93Lagrange_equations "Euler–Lagrange equations"), can be then obtained by considering where the action is [stationary](/wiki/Stationary_state "Stationary state") with respect to variations of either the metric on the base manifold, or of the gauge connection. Variations with respect to the base metric gives the [Einstein field equations](/wiki/Einstein_field_equations "Einstein field equations") on the base manifold, with the [energy–momentum tensor](/wiki/Energy%E2%80%93momentum_tensor "Energy–momentum tensor") given by the [curvature](/wiki/Curvature_form "Curvature form") ([field strength](/wiki/Field_strength "Field strength")) of the gauge connection. On the flip side, the action is stationary against variations of the gauge connection precisely when the gauge connection solves the [Yang–Mills equations](/wiki/Yang%E2%80%93Mills_theory "Yang–Mills theory"). Thus, by applying a single idea: the [principle of least action](/wiki/Principle_of_least_action "Principle of least action"), to a single quantity: the scalar curvature on the bundle (as a whole), one obtains simultaneously all of the needed field equations, for both the spacetime and the gauge field.

As an approach to the unification of the forces, it is straightforward to apply the Kaluza–Klein theory in an attempt to unify gravity with the [strong](/wiki/Strong_force "Strong force") and [electroweak](/wiki/Electroweak "Electroweak") forces by using the symmetry group of the [Standard Model](/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model"), [SU(3)](/wiki/SU(3) "SU(3)") × [SU(2)](/wiki/SU(2) "SU(2)") × [U(1)](/wiki/U(1) "U(1)"). However, an attempt to convert this interesting geometrical construction into a bona-fide model of reality flounders on a number of issues, including the fact that the [fermions](/wiki/Fermion "Fermion") must be introduced in an artificial way (in nonsupersymmetric models). Nonetheless, KK remains an important [touchstone](/wiki/Touchstone_(metaphor) "Touchstone (metaphor)") in theoretical physics and is often embedded in more sophisticated theories. It is studied in its own right as an object of geometric interest in [K-theory](/wiki/K-theory "K-theory").

Even in the absence of a completely satisfying theoretical physics framework, the idea of exploring extra, compactified, dimensions is of considerable interest in the [experimental physics](/wiki/Experimental_physics "Experimental physics") and [astrophysics](/wiki/Astrophysics "Astrophysics") communities. A variety of predictions, with real experimental consequences, can be made (in the case of [large extra dimensions](/wiki/Large_extra_dimension "Large extra dimension") and [warped models](/wiki/Warped_model "Warped model")). For example, on the simplest of principles, one might expect to have [standing waves](/wiki/Standing_wave "Standing wave") in the extra compactified dimension(s). If a spatial extra dimension is of radius *R*, the invariant [mass](/wiki/Mass "Mass") of such standing waves would be *M**n* = *nh*/*Rc* with *n* an [integer](/wiki/Integer "Integer"), *h* being the [Planck constant](/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant") and *c* the [speed of light](/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"). This set of possible mass values is often called the **Kaluza–Klein tower**. Similarly, in [Thermal quantum field theory](/wiki/Thermal_quantum_field_theory "Thermal quantum field theory") a compactification of the euclidean time dimension leads to the [Matsubara frequencies](/wiki/Matsubara_frequency "Matsubara frequency") and thus to a discretized thermal energy spectrum.

However, Klein's approach to a quantum theory is flawed[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*] and, for example, leads to a calculated electron mass in the order of magnitude of the [Planck mass](/wiki/Planck_mass "Planck mass").[[19]](#cite_note-19)

Examples of experimental pursuits include work by the [CDF](/wiki/Collider_Detector_at_Fermilab "Collider Detector at Fermilab") collaboration, which has re-analyzed [particle collider](/wiki/Particle_collider "Particle collider") data for the signature of effects associated with large extra dimensions/[warped models](/wiki/Warped_model "Warped model").[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*]

[Robert Brandenberger](/wiki/Robert_Brandenberger "Robert Brandenberger") and [Cumrun Vafa](/wiki/Cumrun_Vafa "Cumrun Vafa") have speculated that in the early universe, [cosmic inflation](/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation") causes three of the space dimensions to expand to cosmological size while the remaining dimensions of space remained microscopic.[[20]](#cite_note-20)

### Generalizations

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Generalizations")]

In the above, the size of the loop 


Λ
{\displaystyle \Lambda }
 acts as a coupling constant between the gravitational field and the electromagnetic field. If the base manifold is four-dimensional, the Kaluza–Klein manifold *P* is five-dimensional. The fifth dimension is a [compact space](/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space") and is called the **compact dimension**. The technique of introducing compact dimensions to obtain a higher-dimensional manifold is referred to as [compactification](/wiki/Compactification_(physics) "Compactification (physics)"). Compactification does not produce group actions on [chiral](/wiki/Chirality_(physics) "Chirality (physics)") [fermions](/wiki/Fermions "Fermions") except in very specific cases: the dimension of the total space must be 2 mod 8, and the G-index of the Dirac operator of the compact space must be nonzero.[[22]](#cite_note-22)

The above development generalizes in a more-or-less straightforward fashion to general [principal *G*-bundles](/wiki/Principal_G-bundle "Principal G-bundle") for some arbitrary [Lie group](/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group") *G* taking the place of [U(1)](/wiki/U(1) "U(1)"). In such a case, the theory is often referred to as a [Yang–Mills theory](/wiki/Yang%E2%80%93Mills_theory "Yang–Mills theory") and is sometimes taken to be synonymous. If the underlying manifold is [supersymmetric](/wiki/Supersymmetric "Supersymmetric"), the resulting theory is a super-symmetric Yang–Mills theory.

## History

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: History")]

[Theodor Kaluza](/wiki/Theodor_Kaluza "Theodor Kaluza") proposed the possibility of unifying gravitation with electromagnetism in a paper he sent to [Albert Einstein](/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") in 1919.[[23]](#cite_note-:0-23): 329–30 ([Gunnar Nordström](/wiki/Gunnar_Nordstr%C3%B6m "Gunnar Nordström") published a similar idea in 1914. But in that case, a fifth component was added to the electromagnetic vector potential, a scalar representing the Newtonian gravitational potential, and writing the Maxwell equations in five dimensions.[[24]](#cite_note-nrd-24)[[23]](#cite_note-:0-23): 329 But this had to be abandoned because it could not account for [gravitational lensing](/wiki/Gravitational_lens "Gravitational lens").[[1]](#cite_note-:2-1)) Einstein wrote back, saying he liked the idea "enormously" but was not entirely convinced. But two years later, Einstein finally agreed to present Kaluza's paper to the [Prussian Academy of Sciences](/wiki/Prussian_Academy_of_Sciences "Prussian Academy of Sciences") in Berlin.[[1]](#cite_note-:2-1)[[25]](#cite_note-:1-25): 197 Kaluza published it in the same year.[[2]](#cite_note-kal-2) Kaluza presented a purely classical extension of [general relativity](/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") to five dimensions of space and time, with a metric [tensor](/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") of 15 components. Correspondingly, the five-dimensional framework produced four-dimensional [Einstein field equations](/wiki/Einstein_field_equations "Einstein field equations") of gravitation, [Maxwell's equations](/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations") of electromagnetism, and an equation for the scalar field representing a hypothetical particle called the [dilaton](/wiki/Dilaton "Dilaton"). Kaluza also introduced the "cylinder condition" hypothesis, that no component of the five-dimensional metric depends on the fifth dimension. Without this restriction, terms are introduced that involve derivatives of the fields with respect to the fifth coordinate, and this extra degree of freedom makes the mathematics of the fully variable 5D relativity enormously complex. Standard 4D physics seems to manifest this "cylinder condition" and, along with it, simpler mathematics.

In 1926, [Oskar Klein](/wiki/Oskar_Klein "Oskar Klein") extended Kaluza's five-dimensional classical field theory by suggesting that the fifth dimension of space had to do with quantization.[[17]](#cite_note-KZ-17)[[16]](#cite_note-KN-16)[[23]](#cite_note-:0-23): 331–2 Klein introduced the hypothesis that the fifth dimension was curled up and microscopic, to explain the cylinder condition. Klein suggested that the geometry of the extra fifth dimension could take the form of a circle, with the radius of 10−30 cm. More precisely, the radius of the circular dimension is 23 times the [Planck length](/wiki/Planck_units "Planck units"), which in turn is of the order of 10−33 cm.[[16]](#cite_note-KN-16) Klein also made a contribution to the classical theory by providing a properly normalized 5D metric.[[17]](#cite_note-KZ-17) Klein argued that solutions to the [Schrödinger equation](/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation") in five dimensions could be interpreted as [waves or particles](/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality "Wave–particle duality") moving under the influence of electromagnetism and gravity in four-dimensional spacetime.[[1]](#cite_note-:2-1) However, in the original papers by Kaluza and Klein, it was not clear whether the hypothetical fifth dimension was merely a mathematical artifice or physically real.[[1]](#cite_note-:2-1) Einstein, [Wolfgang Pauli](/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli "Wolfgang Pauli"), [Peter Bergmann](/wiki/Peter_Bergmann "Peter Bergmann"), and [Valentine Bargmann](/wiki/Valentine_Bargmann "Valentine Bargmann") explored the Kaluza-Klein theory further during the 1930s and early 1940s.[[1]](#cite_note-:2-1)[[23]](#cite_note-:0-23): 334–6, 347 (At this time, Einstein and a number of other physicists and mathematicians were exploring various possibilities of formulating unified field theories.[[26]](#cite_note-:12-26)[[23]](#cite_note-:0-23): 341–51)

In the 1940s, the classical theory was completed, and the full field equations including the scalar field were obtained by three independent research groups:[[4]](#cite_note-gon-4) Yves Thiry,[[27]](#cite_note-27)[[3]](#cite_note-thry-3)[[28]](#cite_note-28) working in France on his dissertation under [André Lichnerowicz](/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Lichnerowicz "André Lichnerowicz"); [Pascual Jordan](/wiki/Pascual_Jordan "Pascual Jordan"), Günther Ludwig, and Claus Müller in Germany,[[6]](#cite_note-jor1-6)[[7]](#cite_note-jor2-7)[[29]](#cite_note-29)[[8]](#cite_note-jor3-8)[[30]](#cite_note-30) with critical input from Pauli and [Markus Fierz](/wiki/Markus_Fierz "Markus Fierz"); and [Paul Scherrer](/wiki/Paul_Scherrer "Paul Scherrer")[[31]](#cite_note-31)[[32]](#cite_note-32)[[33]](#cite_note-33) working alone in Switzerland. Jordan's work led to the scalar–tensor theory of [Brans–Dicke](/wiki/Brans%E2%80%93Dicke_theory "Brans–Dicke theory");[[34]](#cite_note-34) [Carl H. Brans](/wiki/Carl_H._Brans "Carl H. Brans") and [Robert H. Dicke](/wiki/Robert_H._Dicke "Robert H. Dicke") were apparently unaware of Thiry or Scherrer. The curvature tensors for the complete Kaluza equations were evaluated using [tensor-algebra software](/wiki/Tensor_software "Tensor software") in 2015,[[9]](#cite_note-LLW-9) verifying results of J. A. Ferrari[[35]](#cite_note-fri-35) and R. Coquereaux & G. Esposito-Farese.[[36]](#cite_note-coq-36) The 5D covariant form of the energy–momentum source terms is treated by L. L. Williams.[[37]](#cite_note-LLW2-37)

However, most theoretical physicists who were initially interested in the Kaluza-Klein theory ultimately lost interest because it was in sharp disagreement with experimental results.[[25]](#cite_note-:1-25): 197 All other attempts to generalize [Riemannian geometry](/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry") in order to unify electromagnetism and gravitation have also failed.[[38]](#cite_note-:14-38): 1135 But the basic idea of unification of the fundamental forces using higher dimensions of space was revived during the 1970s with the arrival of string theory and supergravity.[[1]](#cite_note-:2-1)

## Empirical tests

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Empirical tests")]

The Kaluza–Klein theory's prediction of [electron mass](/wiki/Electron_mass "Electron mass") is off by a factor of about 1018.[[39]](#cite_note-39)

No experimental or observational signs of extra dimensions have been officially reported. Many theoretical search techniques for detecting Kaluza–Klein resonances have been proposed using the mass couplings of such resonances with the [top quark](/wiki/Top_quark "Top quark"). An analysis of results from the [Large Hadron Collider](/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider "Large Hadron Collider") (LHC) in December 2010 severely constrains theories with [large extra dimensions](/wiki/Large_extra_dimensions "Large extra dimensions").[[40]](#cite_note-arxiv.org_1-40)

Observations of the gravitational-wave event [GW170817](/wiki/GW170817 "GW170817") refuted the hypothesis that gravity is leaking into higher dimensions as in [brane theory](/wiki/Brane_theory "Brane theory"). [Gravitational waves](/wiki/Gravitational_wave "Gravitational wave") propagate in (3+1)-dimensional spacetime.[[41]](#cite_note-arxiv.org_2-41)[[42]](#cite_note-42)
